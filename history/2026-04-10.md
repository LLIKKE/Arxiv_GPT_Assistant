## 💡 Daily Research Summary

### RL for LLMs & Agentic Policy Optimization
A significant thrust in today's research focuses on refining and scaling reinforcement learning for LLMs, particularly for agentic and reasoning tasks. A key theme is addressing the instability and inefficiency of traditional RL pipelines. Novel frameworks like **T-STAR**and**Android Coach**introduce hierarchical and surgical optimization methods, leveraging structures like cognitive trees or single-state multiple actions to improve credit assignment and training efficiency. Concurrently, papers like**RAGEN-2**and**Limits of Difficulty Scaling**provide critical diagnostics, analyzing failure modes like reasoning collapse and the diminishing returns of hard samples in methods like GRPO. This collective work moves beyond simple policy updates towards more structured, efficient, and diagnosable RL processes that are essential for developing robust, multi-step agents.

### Latent Reasoning & Model Architecture
Research into the internal mechanisms of reasoning sees a dual focus on understanding limits and proposing new architectures. Foundational studies like**The Depth Ceiling**and**The Illusion of Superposition?**directly probe the boundaries of latent multi-step planning and superposition within existing models, establishing a principled baseline for the field. In parallel, novel architectures are being proposed to transcend these limits.**Neural Computers**introduces a paradigm-shifting concept of learned runtime states, effectively moving computation into a dynamic model state, while**Multi-objective Evolutionary Merging**offers a compression-based approach to make reasoning traces more efficient. This direction highlights a mature shift from merely using latent reasoning to fundamentally re-engineering how it is implemented and understood.

### Multimodal & Embodied Agent Alignment
The alignment of multimodal large language models (MLLMs) with embodied, task-oriented actions is a growing frontier. The central challenge is bridging the "reasoning-action gap," where semantic understanding does not translate to effective policy.**Walk the Talk**directly tackles this with Multimodal Agentic Policy Optimization (MAPO), a novel RL method that couples visual semantic alignment with task rewards. This theme of closing the loop between perception, reasoning, and action is further supported by work in simulation environments, such as**TwinLoop**, which uses digital twins for accelerated multi-agent RL policy improvement after environmental shifts. These advances are critical for deploying capable agents in complex, real-world scenarios.

### Generative Model RL & Fine-tuning
Reinforcement learning techniques are being specialized and optimized for diverse generative model families beyond standard language models. For diffusion models,**FP4 Explore, BF16 Train**proposes a highly efficient two-stage RL framework that uses aggressive quantization for scalable rollouts. Similarly, for hybrid autoregressive-diffusion image models,**MAR-GRPO**introduces a stabilized RL variant with novel gradient noise reduction. The field is also seeing unified theoretical frameworks, as demonstrated by**Discrete Flow Matching Policy Optimization**, which provides a new policy gradient formulation applicable to discrete flow matching models. This specialization indicates RL's maturation as a core tool for aligning and improving various state-of-the-art generative architectures.

### Self-Improvement & Continual Learning Systems
A sophisticated line of inquiry examines how systems can be designed for ongoing adaptation and self-refinement. This moves beyond single training runs to frameworks for continuous evolution. The**Self-Learning Diagnostic Agent**exemplifies this with a dual-memory module and reinforcement training for joint optimization of reasoning and memory. The concept of iterative, mutual enhancement is also central to**Discourse Coherence and Response-Guided Context Rewriting**, where rewriter and responder models improve each other in a loop. Furthermore,**How Much LLM Does a Self-Revising Agent Actually Need?**takes a meta-view, externalizing agent state to critically analyze the marginal role of the LLM itself, pushing towards more efficient and transparent self-improvement architectures.

### SFT, Generalization & Reward Modeling
Research continues to refine the foundational stages of model alignment, with a focus on understanding generalization and improving reward signals.**Rethinking Generalization in Reasoning SFT**provides a crucial conditional analysis comparing SFT and RL, directly informing when and why each method succeeds based on optimization, data, and model capability. On the reward modeling front,**Personalized RewardBench** addresses a key limitation by introducing a benchmark for personalized human preferences, demonstrating its correlation to downstream RL performance. This work ensures that the preference data driving RLHF and related methods is more nuanced, scalable, and evaluable, forming a more reliable base for advanced optimization.

# Research Interest Criteria

Please evaluate papers using a strict filter. We only want a small set of strongly aligned papers, not a broad survey of adjacent work.

## Global Selection Principle
- Mark a paper as relevant only if one of the three topics below is a central contribution of the paper, not a side application, benchmark, or discussion point.
- Be conservative by default: if the connection is indirect, partial, or ambiguous, treat it as not relevant or score it low.
- Prefer papers that introduce new methods, training frameworks, reward designs, agent improvement loops, or latent reasoning architectures.
- Do not favor papers just because they mention "LLM", "agent", "reasoning", "RL", "alignment", or "CoT" in a generic way.
- Exclude broad foundation model papers, generic post-training papers, generic tool-use agents, application/system papers, domain-specific deployments, and pure benchmark papers unless their core method strongly matches one of the topics below.

## 1. Reinforcement Learning (RL) for LLMs
We care about RL methods that directly improve LLM behavior, alignment, or reasoning through explicit reward design, RL pipelines, verifier-driven optimization, or scalable preference/feedback mechanisms.
- **Strong match:** RLHF/RLAIF variants, verifiable reward based RL, rejection sampling plus RL pipelines, reward model design, outcome/process reward methods, online RL for LLMs, and methods that materially improve helpfulness, honesty, harmlessness, or hallucination behavior.
- **Especially valuable:** Papers that propose a new reward function, a new scalable RL training recipe, or a new data-generation / self-improvement loop for post-training LLMs.
- **Usually not relevant:** Generic reinforcement learning papers, RL for robotics/control/games, prompt optimization without RL, supervised finetuning only, inference-time search without a training contribution, or alignment papers with no substantive RL component.

## 2. Agents for LLMs
We care specifically about self-evolving agents rather than static agent systems. The central question is whether the agent can improve itself over time through interaction, reflection, tool-use traces, self-generated supervision, or automated curriculum/data generation.
- **Strong match:** Self-improving agent frameworks, reflection/self-critique loops that feed back into learning, trajectory-driven continual improvement, automatic tool-use policy improvement, environment interaction that produces new supervision, and long-horizon agent adaptation.
- **Especially valuable:** Closed-loop systems showing measurable capability gain over time, reliable agent learning from interaction traces, or agent architectures that turn execution experience into better future performance.
- **Usually not relevant:** Vanilla ReAct-style agents, workflow orchestration, multi-agent communication only, tool calling systems without learning, benchmark papers, memory systems without self-improvement, or application-focused agent papers.

## 3. Latent CoT/Reasoning
We care about methods that move reasoning away from verbose explicit text chains into compact latent computation while preserving or improving reasoning quality.
- **Strong match:** Latent reasoning architectures, hidden-state reasoning, implicit CoT, recurrent depth / recurrent latent computation, pause or thinking tokens for internal computation, explicit-to-latent distillation, and methods that reduce reasoning token cost or KV-cache use while retaining accuracy.
- **Especially valuable:** New architectures or objectives for latent reasoning, hidden-state alignment/distillation, compression of reasoning traces, or methods that materially improve reasoning efficiency on math and logic tasks.
- **Usually not relevant:** Standard explicit CoT prompting, generic reasoning finetuning, test-time scaling without latent computation, reasoning benchmarks, or papers whose main contribution is evaluation rather than a latent reasoning mechanism.

## Final Preference
If a paper is merely adjacent to these areas, do not select it. Favor precision over recall. It is better to miss a borderline paper than to include many weakly related ones.
