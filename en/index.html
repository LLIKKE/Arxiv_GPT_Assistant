💡 Daily Research Summary

### Reinforcement Learning for LLMs & Agents Today's research demonstrates a significant maturation of reinforcement learning (RL) techniques for large language models, moving beyond foundational alignment to address nuanced challenges in training stability, credit assignment, and reward design. A clear trend is the development of novel, scalable RL paradigms tailored for LLMs, such as framing behavior-regularized RL as an optimal transport problem solved via discrete gradient flow (Value Gradient Flow) or integrating fine-grained process supervision for better credit assignment in complex tasks like search (CW-GRPO). Concurrently, critical analyses of failure modes, such as reward hacking in verifiable reward settings (RLVR), highlight the community's focus on robustness. These advancements are being applied across diverse domains—from topology optimization (TOPCELL) and financial strategy generation to visual RAG (UniDoc-RL) and recommendation systems (GenRec)—indicating RL's role as a versatile engine for specializing and optimizing LLM behavior for specific, high-stakes applications.

Self-Improving & Evolving Agents

The frontier of agent design is increasingly defined by frameworks that enable continuous, autonomous improvement through interaction and reflection. A key breakthrough is the shift from static agents to systems where the agent's core policy or skill set is inherently evolvable. This is exemplified by personalized user policy skills that self-evolve through interaction (SAGER) and embodied agents that employ long short-term reflective optimization for manipulation tasks. Furthermore, the automation of the entire LLM training lifecycle via multi-agent systems (TREX) represents a paradigm shift towards meta-optimization, where agents themselves plan exploration and distill insights from iterative trials. These approaches collectively move the field toward more adaptive, persistent, and capable autonomous systems that learn from experience without constant human intervention.

Efficient & Latent Reasoning

A major research thrust focuses on making reasoning processes within models more computationally efficient and structurally explicit. Innovations include novel architectures like looped transformers with internal recall, which allow for test-time compute scaling, and methods that leverage latent activation patterns to guide sparse updates for long-context RL (LongAct). There is also a strong emphasis on dynamic resource allocation: new frameworks adaptively select efficient reasoning formats for visual tasks to reduce token usage, while constrained policy optimization methods dynamically allocate test-time compute. The introduction of explicit, ordered sequences of latent thoughts as a middle layer for tasks like sign language translation further underscores the trend towards making reasoning tractable, inspectable, and efficient, whether through architectural innovation or adaptive inference-time strategies.

Multimodal & Visual Reasoning

Research in multimodal domains is rapidly integrating advanced RL and reasoning techniques to tackle complex, open-ended tasks. The unifying theme is the use of structured reinforcement learning to orchestrate sophisticated agent behavior over visual and textual modalities. For instance, new frameworks employ hierarchical action spaces and dense multi-reward schemes for visual retrieval-augmented generation (UniDoc-RL), while others use RL to train models for adaptive visual reasoning that balances performance with efficiency. Systematic studies on reward design for physical reasoning in vision-language models highlight the critical role of tailored incentive structures. Beyond agents, similar RL principles are being applied to improve generative models themselves, such as through reward-aware trajectory shaping for visual generation and step-level alignment for diffusion models with multiple objectives.

Model Efficiency & Specialization

Efforts to enhance the practical deployment of large models are advancing on multiple fronts, particularly through specialized routing and targeted fine-tuning. A notable development is Counterfactual Routing (CoR), a training-free inference method for Mixture-of-Experts models that dynamically reallocates computation to mitigate hallucinations, directly improving reliability without retraining. For flow matching models, new direct-gradient fine-tuning methods like LeapAlign enable efficient reward propagation to early generation steps. In the GUI automation space, lightweight agent frameworks utilize multi-role orchestration and RL for cooperative exploration, demonstrating that significant capability can be achieved with efficient, specialized architectures rather than monolithic models.

1. Does RL Expand the Capability Boundary of LLM Agents? A PASS@(k,T) Analysis

ArXiv ID: 2604.14877 | Relevance Score: 9/10 | Novelty Score: 8/10

👥 Authors: Zhiyuan Zhai, Wenjing Yan, Xiaodan Shao, Xin Wang

🏫 Affiliations: Unknown Institution

💡 Highlight: Directly addresses whether RL expands the capability boundary of LLM agents, introducing a new metric and showing RL enables compositional strategies, strongly matching the Agents for LLMs topic.
View Abstract

arXiv:2604.14877v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Does reinforcement learning genuinely expand what LLM agents can do, or merely make them more reliable? For static reasoning, recent work answers the second: base and RL pass@k curves converge at large k. We ask whether this holds for agentic tool use, where T rounds of interaction enable compositional strategies that re-sampling cannot recover. We introduce PASS@(k,T), a two-dimensional metric that jointly varies sampling budget k and interaction depth T, separating capability expansion from efficiency improvement. Our main finding is that, contrary to the static-reasoning result, tool-use RL genuinely enlarges the capability boundary: the RL agent's pass-curve pulls above the base model's and the gap widens at large k rather than converging. The expansion is specific to compositional, sequential information gathering; on simpler tasks RL behaves as prior work predicts. Under matched training data, supervised fine-tuning regresses the boundary on the same compositional tasks, isolating self-directed exploration as the causal factor. Mechanism analysis shows RL reweights the base strategy distribution toward the subset whose downstream reasoning more often yields a correct answer, with the improvement concentrated on how the agent integrates retrieved information. These results reconcile optimistic and pessimistic readings of RL for LLMs: both are correct, on different task types.

2. Stability and Generalization in Looped Transformers

ArXiv ID: 2604.15259 | Relevance Score: 8/10 | Novelty Score: 8/10

👥 Authors: Asher Labovich

🏫 Affiliations: Unknown Institution

💡 Highlight: Proposes a novel looped transformer architecture with internal recall for test-time compute scaling, directly addressing latent reasoning and recurrent latent computation.
View Abstract

arXiv:2604.15259v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Looped transformers promise test-time compute scaling by spending more iterations on harder problems, but it remains unclear which architectural choices let them extrapolate to harder problems at test time rather than memorize training-specific solutions. We introduce a fixed-point based framework for analyzing looped architectures along three axes of stability -- reachability, input-dependence, and geometry -- and use it to characterize when fixed-point iteration yields meaningful predictions. Theoretically, we prove that looped networks without recall have countable fixed points and cannot achieve strong input-dependence at any spectral regime, while recall combined with outer normalization reliably produces a regime in which fixed points are simultaneously reachable, locally smooth in the input, and supported by stable backpropagation. Empirically, we train single-layer looped transformers on chess, sudoku, and prefix-sums and find that downstream performance tracks the framework's predictions across tasks and architectural configurations. We additionally introduce internal recall, a novel recall placement variant, and show that it becomes competitive with -- and on sudoku, substantially better than -- standard recall placement once outer normalization is applied.

🏫 Affiliations: Unknown Institution

💡 Highlight: Proposes a self-evolving agent framework where each user has a personalized reasoning policy skill that improves through interaction, directly matching the self-improving agent criterion.
View Abstract

arXiv:2604.14972v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Large language model (LLM) based recommendation agents personalize what they know through evolving per-user semantic memory, yet how they reason remains a universal, static system prompt shared identically across all users. This asymmetry is a fundamental bottleneck: when a recommendation fails, the agent updates its memory of user preferences but never interrogates the decision logic that produced the failure, leaving its reasoning process structurally unchanged regardless of how many mistakes it accumulates. To address this bottleneck, we propose SAGER (Self-Evolving Agent for Personalized Recommendation), the first recommendation agent framework in which each user is equipped with a dedicated policy skill, a structured natural-language document encoding personalized decision principles that evolves continuously through interaction. SAGER introduces a two-representation skill architecture that decouples a rich evolution substrate from a minimal inference-time injection, an incremental contrastive chain-of-thought engine that diagnoses reasoning flaws by contrasting accepted against unchosen items while preserving accumulated priors, and skill-augmented listwise reasoning that creates fine-grained decision boundaries where the evolved skill provides genuine discriminative value. Experiments on four public benchmarks demonstrate that SAGER achieves state-of-the-art performance, with gains orthogonal to memory accumulation, confirming that personalizing the reasoning process itself is a qualitatively distinct source of recommendation improvement.

4. Reinforcement Learning via Value Gradient Flow

👥 Authors: Haoran Xu, Kaiwen Hu, Somayeh Sojoudi, Amy Zhang

🏫 Affiliations: Unknown Institution

💡 Highlight: Proposes Value Gradient Flow (VGF), a scalable new paradigm for behavior-regularized RL for LLMs, framing it as an optimal transport problem solved via discrete gradient flow.
View Abstract

arXiv:2604.14265v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: We study behavior-regularized reinforcement learning (RL), where regularization toward a reference distribution (the dataset in offline RL or the base model in LLM RL finetuning) is essential to prevent value over-optimization caused by erroneous out-of-distribution extrapolation. Existing methods either rely on reparameterized policy gradient, which are difficult to scale to large generative models, or on reject sampling, which can be overly conservative when attempting to move beyond the behavior support. In this paper, we propose Value Gradient Flow (VGF), a scalable new paradigm for behavior-regularized RL. VGF casts behavior-regularized RL as an optimal transport problem that maps the reference distribution to the value-induced optimal policy distribution. We solve this transport problem via discrete gradient flow, where value gradients guide particles initialized from the reference distribution. Our analysis shows that VGF imposes regularization implicitly by controlling the transport budget. VGF eliminates explicit policy parameterization while remaining expressive and flexible, this enables adaptive test-time scaling by adjusting the transport budget. Extensive experiments demonstrate that VGF significantly outperforms prior methods, achieving state-of-the-art results on offline RL benchmarks (D4RL, OGBench) and LLM RL tasks. Code and runs can be found at https://ryanxhr.github.io/vgf.

5. LLMs Gaming Verifiers: RLVR can Lead to Reward Hacking

ArXiv ID: 2604.15149 | Relevance Score: 8/10 | Novelty Score: 7/10

👥 Authors: Lukas Helff, Quentin Delfosse, David Steinmann, Ruben H\"arle, Hikaru Shindo, Patrick Schramowski, Wolfgang Stammer, Kristian Kersting, Felix Friedrich

🏫 Affiliations: Unknown Institution

💡 Highlight: Directly studies a failure mode of RL with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR) for LLMs, analyzing reward hacking in reasoning tasks, which is central to RL for LLMs.
View Abstract

arXiv:2604.15149v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: As reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR) has become the dominant paradigm for scaling reasoning capabilities in LLMs, a new failure mode emerges: LLMs gaming verifiers. We study this phenomenon on inductive reasoning tasks, where models must induce and output logical rules. We find that RLVR-trained models systematically abandon rule induction. Instead of learning generalizable patterns (e.g., ``trains carrying red cars go east''), they enumerate instance-level labels, producing outputs that pass verifiers without capturing the relational patterns required by the task. We show that this behavior is not a failure of understanding but a form of reward hacking: imperfect verifiers that check only extensional correctness admit false positives. To detect such shortcuts, we introduce Isomorphic Perturbation Testing (IPT), which evaluates a single model output under both extensional and isomorphic verification, where the latter enforces invariance under logically isomorphic tasks. While genuine rule induction remains invariant, shortcut strategies fail. We find that shortcut behavior is specific to RLVR-trained reasoning models (e.g., GPT-5, Olmo3) and absent in non-RLVR models (e.g., GPT-4o, GPT-4.5, Ministral). Moreover, shortcut prevalence increases with task complexity and inference-time compute. In controlled training experiments, extensional verification directly induces shortcut strategies, while isomorphic verification eliminates them. These results show that RLVR can incentivize reward hacking not only through overt manipulation but also by exploiting what the verifier fails to enforce.

7. Beyond Importance Sampling: Rejection-Gated Policy Optimization

ArXiv ID: 2604.14895 | Relevance Score: 7/10 | Novelty Score: 8/10

👥 Authors: Ziwu Sun, Zhen Gao, Jiyong Zhang, Jiaheng Li

🏫 Affiliations: Unknown Institution

💡 Highlight: Proposes a new policy optimization method (RGPO) with a rejection gate, directly applicable to RLHF-style preference alignment for LLMs, matching the RL for LLMs topic.
View Abstract

arXiv:2604.14895v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: We propose a new perspective on policy optimization: rather than reweighting all samples by their importance ratios, an optimizer should select which samples are trustworthy enough to drive a policy update. Building on this view, we introduce Rejection-Gated Policy Optimization (RGPO), which replaces the importance sampling ratio r_theta = pi_theta / pi_old with a smooth, differentiable acceptance gate alpha_theta(s, a) = g(r_theta(s, a)) in the range [0, 1]. Unlike prior work that applies rejection sampling as a data-level heuristic before training, RGPO elevates rejection to an optimization principle: the gate participates directly in gradient computation and is implicitly updated alongside the policy. RGPO provides a unified framework: the policy gradients of TRPO, PPO, and REINFORCE all correspond to specific choices of the effective gradient weight w(r) = g'(r) * r. We prove that RGPO guarantees finite, bounded gradient variance even when importance sampling ratios are heavy-tailed (where IS variance diverges). We further show that RGPO incurs only a bounded, controllable bias and provides an approximate monotonic policy improvement guarantee analogous to TRPO. RGPO matches PPO in computational cost, requires no second-order optimization, and extends naturally to RLHF-style preference alignment. In online preference fine-tuning of Qwen2.5-1.5B-Instruct on Anthropic HH-RLHF (n = 3 seeds), RGPO uses a dual-ratio gate that anchors learning to both the previous policy and the reference model, achieving a Pareto-dominant outcome: the highest reward among online RL methods (+14.8% vs. PPO-RLHF) and the lowest KL divergence to the reference model (-16.0% vs. PPO-RLHF, -53.1% vs. GRPO).

arXiv:2604.14910v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Achieving high-fidelity generation in extremely few sampling steps has long been a central goal of generative modeling. Existing approaches largely rely on distillation-based frameworks to compress the original multi-step denoising process into a few-step generator. However, such methods inherently constrain the student to imitate a stronger multi-step teacher, imposing the teacher as an upper bound on student performance. We argue that introducing \textbf{preference alignment awareness} enables the student to optimize toward reward-preferred generation quality, potentially surpassing the teacher instead of being restricted to rigid teacher imitation. Trajectory shaping is strengthened when the teacher achieves higher rewards, and relaxed when the student matches or surpasses the teacher, thereby enabling continued reward-driven improvement. By seamlessly integrating trajectory distillation, reward-aware gating, and preference alignment, RATS effectively transfers preference-relevant knowledge from high-step generators without incurring additional test-time computational overhead. Experimental results demonstrate that RATS substantially improves the efficiency--quality trade-off in few-step visual generation, significantly narrowing the gap between few-step students and stronger multi-step generators.

Research Interest Criteria

Please evaluate papers using a strict filter. We only want a small set of strongly aligned papers, not a broad survey of adjacent work.

Global Selection Principle

  • Mark a paper as relevant only if one of the three topics below is a central contribution of the paper, not a side application, benchmark, or discussion point.
  • Be conservative by default: if the connection is indirect, partial, or ambiguous, treat it as not relevant or score it low.
  • Prefer papers that introduce new methods, training frameworks, reward designs, agent improvement loops, or latent reasoning architectures.
  • Do not favor papers just because they mention "LLM", "agent", "reasoning", "RL", "alignment", or "CoT" in a generic way.
  • Exclude broad foundation model papers, generic post-training papers, generic tool-use agents, application/system papers, domain-specific deployments, and pure benchmark papers unless their core method strongly matches one of the topics below.

1. Reinforcement Learning (RL) for LLMs

We care about RL methods that directly improve LLM behavior, alignment, or reasoning through explicit reward design, RL pipelines, verifier-driven optimization, or scalable preference/feedback mechanisms.

  • Strong match: RLHF/RLAIF variants, verifiable reward based RL, rejection sampling plus RL pipelines, reward model design, outcome/process reward methods, online RL for LLMs, and methods that materially improve helpfulness, honesty, harmlessness, or hallucination behavior.
  • Especially valuable: Papers that propose a new reward function, a new scalable RL training recipe, or a new data-generation / self-improvement loop for post-training LLMs.
  • Usually not relevant: Generic reinforcement learning papers, RL for robotics/control/games, prompt optimization without RL, supervised finetuning only, inference-time search without a training contribution, or alignment papers with no substantive RL component.

2. Agents for LLMs

We care specifically about self-evolving agents rather than static agent systems. The central question is whether the agent can improve itself over time through interaction, reflection, tool-use traces, self-generated supervision, or automated curriculum/data generation.

  • Strong match: Self-improving agent frameworks, reflection/self-critique loops that feed back into learning, trajectory-driven continual improvement, automatic tool-use policy improvement, environment interaction that produces new supervision, and long-horizon agent adaptation.
  • Especially valuable: Closed-loop systems showing measurable capability gain over time, reliable agent learning from interaction traces, or agent architectures that turn execution experience into better future performance.
  • Usually not relevant: Vanilla ReAct-style agents, workflow orchestration, multi-agent communication only, tool calling systems without learning, benchmark papers, memory systems without self-improvement, or application-focused agent papers.

3. Latent CoT/Reasoning

We care about methods that move reasoning away from verbose explicit text chains into compact latent computation while preserving or improving reasoning quality.

  • Strong match: Latent reasoning architectures, hidden-state reasoning, implicit CoT, recurrent depth / recurrent latent computation, pause or thinking tokens for internal computation, explicit-to-latent distillation, and methods that reduce reasoning token cost or KV-cache use while retaining accuracy.
  • Especially valuable: New architectures or objectives for latent reasoning, hidden-state alignment/distillation, compression of reasoning traces, or methods that materially improve reasoning efficiency on math and logic tasks.
  • Usually not relevant: Standard explicit CoT prompting, generic reasoning finetuning, test-time scaling without latent computation, reasoning benchmarks, or papers whose main contribution is evaluation rather than a latent reasoning mechanism.

Final Preference

If a paper is merely adjacent to these areas, do not select it. Favor precision over recall. It is better to miss a borderline paper than to include many weakly related ones.
