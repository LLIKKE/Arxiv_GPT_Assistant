💡 Daily Research Summary

### RL for LLMs & Reasoning A dominant trend this week is the maturation of Reinforcement Learning from Verifiable Rewards (RLVR) into a more principled and theoretically grounded field. Several works move beyond monolithic reward signals. For instance, *Soft-SVeRL* and *VCap* introduce decomposed, fine-grained rewards—checklist-based and hypergeometric-distribution-level, respectively—while *Multi-Sample Code Generation* proposes redundancy-aware rewards. A critical theoretical advance is provided by *Off-Policy Learning to Reason Works Because It Is More Pessimistic Than You Think*, which offers a formal understanding of implicit pessimism in off-policy methods, directly stabilizing training. This is complemented by mechanistic analyses like *Mechanistically Interpreting the Role of Sample Difficulty*, which uses T-SAE-guided signals for adaptive strategies. Practical innovations in training dynamics are also prominent: *ZipRL* introduces adaptive context compression for RLVR, *Where Rollouts Begin* (REFT) targets first-token diversification for better exploration, and *BPPO* uses binary prefix optimization for efficiency. The field is clearly converging on a deeper understanding of the optimization landscape, moving from simple reward engineering to adaptive, theory-informed, and mechanistically-grounded training pipelines.

Self-Improving & Evolving Agents

The concept of the self-improving agent is being operationalized through a diverse set of novel frameworks. A key theme is the internalization of skills from experience. SKILLC uses contrastive credit assignment for autonomous skill internalization, while SkillGrad optimizes agent skills using a gradient-descent-inspired approach on trajectory-level losses. PEAM takes this to embodied domains, creating parametric memory via contrastive learning. Hierarchical and meta-level evolution is also a focus: You Live More Than Once introduces a framework for meta-evolving the skill-evolving strategy itself, and Learn from Weaknesses uses a stronger agent to identify and patch student weaknesses. Bidirectional search is a powerful new paradigm, as seen in Bidirectional Evolutionary Search (BES), which combines forward evolution with backward goal decomposition. These works collectively demonstrate a shift from static, hand-crafted agent behaviors to dynamic systems capable of closed-loop capability gain through structured reflection, credit assignment, and meta-learning.

Latent Reasoning & Compression

A revolutionary sub-field is emerging around "latent reasoning," where models internalize thought processes into hidden states rather than explicit tokens. Transformers Provably Learn to Internalize Chain-of-Thought provides a theoretical foundation for this, while CIRF tokenizes reasoning into discrete, reusable latent functional units. Thinking as Compression provocatively frames reasoning traces as compressed context, using reward-driven optimization. VITAL extends this to medical MLLMs with visual-semantic dual supervision for interpretability. Zipping the Thought offers a systematic study of when and how compressed reasoning data works in post-training. This direction promises to bridge the efficiency of implicit reasoning with the interpretability and verifiability of explicit CoT, potentially unlocking new frontiers in inference-time compute scaling, as also hinted at by LaneRoPE's collaborative parallel reasoning.

Multimodal & Agentic Reasoning

Multimodal reasoning is advancing through sophisticated RL frameworks that address modality-specific challenges. MAPO directly tackles modality collapse in audio reasoning with a dual-branch RL policy. Mags-RL equips MLLMs with a super-resolution agent for fine-grained visual reasoning in a two-round loop. For 3D understanding, SSR3D-LLM introduces latent spatial reasoning steps for fine-grained grounding. In the agentic space, AXPO addresses the "Thinking-Acting Gap" by resampling tool calls with fixed thinking prefixes. The Retrieval, Reward, and Training Protocols paper provides a crucial empirical study isolating the key components for training effective search agents. The trend is clear: RL is being used not just to improve a single model, but to orchestrate multi-model, multi-step reasoning and tool-use pipelines, with a strong emphasis on grounding in diverse modalities.

Alignment, Safety & Oversight

The challenge of aligning increasingly capable models is being met with innovative RL-based oversight mechanisms. Calibrating Conservatism for Scalable Oversight proposes calibrated collective oversight using conformal prediction. Debate Helps Weak Judges Reward Stronger Models shows how debate protocols can enable weaker models to provide effective reward signals. Mitigating Adaptive Attacks against Reasoning Models introduces activation consistency training as a new safety paradigm. Reward Bias Substitution formalizes a critical failure mode in RLHF where mitigating one bias redirects pressure. Confidence-Orchestrated Self-Evolution (COSE) addresses the problem of noisy self-generated feedback by modulating PPO updates with intrinsic confidence. These works reflect a growing sophistication in understanding the socio-technical dynamics of RL-based alignment, moving beyond simple reward hacking to address systemic issues of oversight, bias, and robustness.

Novel Architectures & Optimization Methods

Beyond core RL algorithms, several papers introduce novel architectural and optimization components. LaneRoPE presents a new positional encoding for collaborative parallel reasoning. Skill-Conditioned Gated Self-Distillation and Restoring the Sweet Spot (SC-SDPO) offer novel loss-level modifications to improve reasoning self-distillation. AdaDPO introduces a self-adaptive DPO variant with balanced gradients. Cyclical Entropy Eruption identifies and stabilizes a previously unknown training dynamic in agent RL. Extrapolative Weight Averaging provides a training-free method to navigate correctness-efficiency frontiers. These works demonstrate that progress in LLM reasoning is not solely dependent on new RL algorithms but is also being driven by clever architectural innovations, loss function design, and a deeper understanding of training dynamics.

💡 Highlight: Provides the first theoretical analysis of Implicit Chain-of-Thought (ICoT), proving that transformers can internalize reasoning steps into hidden states with a novel Log-ICoT curriculum, matching explicit CoT efficiency without inference overhead.
View Abstract

arXiv:2605.28600v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting substantially improves the sample efficiency of transformers, reducing the complexity of tasks like parity learning from exponential to polynomial in the input length. However, generating explicit reasoning steps at inference is computationally expensive. Implicit Chain-of-Thought (ICoT) has emerged as a promising empirical remedy that trains models to internalize intermediate steps within their hidden states, but its theoretical foundations remain poorly understood. We give the first theoretical analysis of ICoT, proving that an $L$-layer transformer trained under our proposed Log-ICoT curriculum learns $k$-parity with $\mathsf{poly}(n)$ samples and $L = \log_2 k$ training stages. This matches the sample efficiency of explicit CoT while eliminating its inference overhead, and extends prior one-layer parity guarantees to multi-layer architectures. Compared to standard ICoT, which removes thinking tokens one at a time, Log-ICoT removes them in geometric chunks, reducing the number of stages from linear in $k$ to logarithmic. Experiments on multi-layer transformers confirm the theory and visualize how reasoning is progressively absorbed into deeper layers.

🏫 Affiliations: Unknown Institution

💡 Highlight: Introduces Contrastive Reflection (CORE), a non-parametric self-improvement algorithm for reasoning that distills insights from successful/unsuccessful traces, strongly matching self-evolving agents and RL for LLMs.
View Abstract

arXiv:2605.28742v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Language models can use verifiable rewards to improve at a wide variety of reasoning tasks. However, both parametric (e.g. RLVR) and non-parametric (e.g. prompt optimization) approaches to doing so typically require hundreds of training samples and thousands of model rollouts, making them expensive in the best case and intractable in the worst. To address this challenge, we introduce Contrastive Reflection (CORE), a non-parametric learning algorithm that compares past reasoning traces to generate insights: short natural-language descriptions of reasoning strategies and constraints that capture differences between successful and unsuccessful problem attempts. Across four reasoning tasks, we demonstrate that CORE enables more rapid improvement than both parametric (GRPO) and non-parametric (GEPA, episodic RAG, and MemRL) methods, while using fewer rollouts. Under fixed rollout budgets with as few as five training samples, we then show that CORE also achieves comparable or greater performance gains than each baseline. Finally, we highlight how CORE is also substantially more context-efficient than non-parametric baselines, requiring fewer prompt tokens while storing learned knowledge as compact, interpretable natural-language insights. Our results therefore suggest that distilling contrasts between successful and unsuccessful reasoning traces into abstract and useful insights can provide a more efficient and interpretable route to model self-improvement than weight updates, prompt optimization, or direct reuse of stored reasoning traces.

4. Mechanistically Interpreting the Role of Sample Difficulty in RLVR for LLMs

👥 Authors: Yue Cheng, Jiajun Zhang, Xiaohui Gao, Weiwei Xing, Zheng Wang, Zhanxing Zhu

🏫 Affiliations: Unknown Institution

💡 Highlight: Proposes a mechanistic analysis of sample difficulty in RLVR for LLMs, introducing difficulty-adaptive strategies and T-SAE-guided training signals, directly advancing RL for LLMs.
View Abstract

arXiv:2605.28388v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Reward (RLVR) is empirically shown to notably enhance the reasoning performance of large language models (LLMs), particularly in mathematics and programming. However, the mechanistic role of Sample Difficulty in RLVR remains poorly understood. In this paper, we investigate RLVR through the lens of difficulty-wise and one-sample analysis. We find that sample difficulty has a non-monotonic effect on RLVR: easy and medium-difficulty problems yield the strongest and most stable reasoning improvements, whereas overly hard problems often provide weak learning signals, induce degenerate behaviors such as answer repetition or skipping necessary computation, and can ultimately degrade the model's pre-existing capabilities. Beyond the obverse of response, we further analyze the model's internal feature dynamics using Temporal Sparse Autoencoders (T-SAE). Easy problems mainly reinforce direct-answer and basic-computation features while suppressing deliberative-reasoning features; hard problems activate reasoning-related features but become useful only when successful trajectories are sampled; medium-difficulty problems provide a more balanced signal, strengthening both computation and multi-step reasoning features. Motivated by these findings, we propose difficulty-adaptive strategies for hard-sample utilization, using backward-reasoning reformulation and T-SAE-guided training signals to improve reward density and credit assignment during RLVR. Overall, our results identify sample difficulty as a key factor governing both the optimization dynamics and representation evolution of RLVR.

arXiv:2605.27741v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Audio and omni-modal large language models exhibit impressive cross-modal reasoning capabilities. However, applying standard reinforcement learning post-training algorithms to these models exposes a critical structural vulnerability: methods like GRPO apply uniform policy gradients across all tokens, ignoring their unequal dependence on the non-text source modality. This exacerbates late-stage modality collapse during extended chain-of-thought generation, where models progressively abandon the primary source signal in favor of compressed textual priors, leading to confident but ungrounded hallucinations. To address this, we introduce Modality-Aware Policy Optimization (MAPO), a novel dual-branch reinforcement learning framework. First, MAPO dynamically concentrates the policy gradient on modality-critical tokens using a modality relevance mask, which is derived from the cross-modal differential entropy between an audio-ablated reference and the multimodal policy. Second, it integrates an auxiliary attention loss branch that applies a targeted, temporally scaled penalty to the model's internal attention distributions. This ensures the model actively sustains cross-modal grounding deep into the reasoning trace. Evaluations on complex audio reasoning benchmarks demonstrate that MAPO substantially improves long-horizon reasoning fidelity and multimodal instruction following, achieving highly competitive performance and setting new state-of-the-art results on several key benchmarks among open-weight models. By relying strictly on native statistical signals rather than domain-specific inductive biases, MAPO offers a promising foundation for mitigating epistemic collapse across diverse multimodal systems.

  • Especially valuable: Papers that propose a new reward function, a new scalable RL training recipe, or a new data-generation / self-improvement loop for post-training LLMs.
  • Usually not relevant: Generic reinforcement learning papers, RL for robotics/control/games, prompt optimization without RL, supervised finetuning only, inference-time search without a training contribution, or alignment papers with no substantive RL component.

2. Agents for LLMs

We care specifically about self-evolving agents rather than static agent systems. The central question is whether the agent can improve itself over time through interaction, reflection, tool-use traces, self-generated supervision, or automated curriculum/data generation.

  • Strong match: Self-improving agent frameworks, reflection/self-critique loops that feed back into learning, trajectory-driven continual improvement, automatic tool-use policy improvement, environment interaction that produces new supervision, and long-horizon agent adaptation.
  • Especially valuable: Closed-loop systems showing measurable capability gain over time, reliable agent learning from interaction traces, or agent architectures that turn execution experience into better future performance.
  • Usually not relevant: Vanilla ReAct-style agents, workflow orchestration, multi-agent communication only, tool calling systems without learning, benchmark papers, memory systems without self-improvement, or application-focused agent papers.

3. Latent CoT/Reasoning

We care about methods that move reasoning away from verbose explicit text chains into compact latent computation while preserving or improving reasoning quality.

  • Strong match: Latent reasoning architectures, hidden-state reasoning, implicit CoT, recurrent depth / recurrent latent computation, pause or thinking tokens for internal computation, explicit-to-latent distillation, and methods that reduce reasoning token cost or KV-cache use while retaining accuracy.
  • Especially valuable: New architectures or objectives for latent reasoning, hidden-state alignment/distillation, compression of reasoning traces, or methods that materially improve reasoning efficiency on math and logic tasks.
  • Usually not relevant: Standard explicit CoT prompting, generic reasoning finetuning, test-time scaling without latent computation, reasoning benchmarks, or papers whose main contribution is evaluation rather than a latent reasoning mechanism.
