💡 Daily Research Summary

### Self-Improving Agents & Embodied AI The field of agentic AI is moving decisively toward closed-loop, self-evolutionary systems. A clear trend is the development of frameworks that enable agents to improve autonomously through interaction, often without external supervision. This is exemplified by test-time self-evolution via variational synthesis (TTVS), iterative refinement using pairwise comparisons (3DrawAgent), and modular capability evolution in embodied systems (Learning Without Losing Identity). These approaches are unified by a focus on creating agents that can execute, self-assess, and refine their policies in a continuous loop, effectively cultivating meta-cognitive abilities and moving beyond static, one-time training.

Reinforcement Learning for LLMs

Research in RL for LLMs is maturing beyond foundational algorithms to address critical stability and efficiency challenges inherent in aligning large models. A major focus is on mitigating the training-inference mismatch, with novel methods like QaRL aligning quantized rollouts and StableOPD stabilizing on-policy distillation to prevent failure modes. Concurrently, there is significant innovation in reward design, moving from simple outcome-based signals to sophisticated process rewards that guide intermediate reasoning (SubSearch) and enforce logical consistency (Faithful GRPO). This reflects a broader push toward more sample-efficient, stable, and theoretically grounded RL training regimes, as analyzed in unified frameworks for post-training.

Latent & Implicit Reasoning

A powerful paradigm is emerging that shifts complex reasoning from the token space to learned latent representations, aiming for greater efficiency and compositional generalization. Frameworks like Pearl and SeLaR demonstrate this by learning predictive embeddings or selectively activating soft embeddings for multi-step reasoning, effectively creating a "latent chain-of-thought." This direction is closely tied to architectural innovations for efficiency, such as recurrent-depth transformers for implicit multi-hop reasoning (Loop, Think, & Generalize) and novel state-space models that achieve constant inference cache via computational phase transitions (MIPT-SSM). The goal is to compress reasoning trajectories and enable more sophisticated, internal computation.

Multimodal & Domain-Specialized RL

RL techniques are being aggressively tailored to solve concrete challenges in multimodal and specialized domains, particularly where high-stakes accuracy and faithful grounding are paramount. In visual domains, methods are enforcing visual-spatial consistency (Faithful GRPO) and developing latent reasoning policies for VLMs (Decompose, Look, and Reason). For scientific and medical applications, such as fundus reading (Fundus-R1) or drug discovery (LLM-Guided Action Spaces), frameworks integrate verifiable rewards and process supervision to ensure reliable, annotation-free learning. This trend highlights RL's role not just as a general alignment tool, but as a precise instrument for instilling domain-specific reasoning capabilities and trustworthiness.

Tool Use & Long-Horizon Planning

Agent frameworks are increasingly sophisticated in their orchestration of external tools and management of long-horizon tasks. Research focuses on meta-cognitive arbitration, conditioning tool-use efficiency on task success (Act Wisely), and using curriculum RL to evolve tool-using CAD agents (TOOLCAD). For planning, new methods like GIRL introduce latent world models with controls to prevent imagination drift, enabling more reliable long-term reasoning. This is complemented by work on active memory systems (MemReader) that use RL to optimize selective memory writing, creating a self-improving loop for managing long-term context and information in complex tasks.

arXiv:2604.08468v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Despite significant advances in Large Reasoning Models (LRMs) driven by reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards (RLVR), this paradigm is fundamentally limited in specialized or novel domains where such supervision is prohibitively expensive or unavailable, posing a key challenge for test-time adaptation. While existing test-time methods offer a potential solution, they are constrained by learning from static query sets, risking overfitting to textual patterns. Extensive experiments show TTVS yields superior performance across eight model architectures. Notably, using only unlabeled test-time data, TTVS not only surpasses other test-time adaptation methods but also outperforms state-of-the-art supervised RL-based techniques trained on vast, high-quality labeled data.

🏫 Affiliations: Unknown Institution

💡 Highlight: Proposes Dataset Policy Gradient (DPG), a novel RL primitive for optimizing synthetic data generators to precisely shape LLM behavior via SFT, directly matching RL for LLMs.
View Abstract

arXiv:2604.08423v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: What are the limits of controlling language models via synthetic training data? We develop a reinforcement learning (RL) primitive, the Dataset Policy Gradient (DPG), which can precisely optimize synthetic data generators to produce a dataset of targeted examples. When used for supervised fine-tuning (SFT) of a target model, these examples cause the target model to do well on a differentiable metric of our choice. Our approach achieves this by taking exact data attribution via higher-order gradients and using those scores as policy gradient rewards. We prove that this procedure closely approximates the true, intractable gradient for the synthetic data generator. To illustrate the potential of DPG, we show that, using only SFT on generated examples, we can cause the target model's LM head weights to (1) embed a QR code, (2) embed the pattern $\texttt{67}$, and (3) have lower $\ell^2$ norm. We additionally show that we can cause the generator to (4) rephrase inputs in a new language and (5) produce a specific UUID, even though neither of these objectives is conveyed in the generator's input prompts. These findings suggest that DPG is a powerful and flexible technique for shaping model properties using only synthetic training examples.

💡 Highlight: Proposes QaRL, a novel RL method for LLMs that aligns quantized rollouts with training to address the training-inference mismatch and stabilize optimization.
View Abstract

arXiv:2604.07853v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Large language model (LLM) reinforcement learning (RL) pipelines are often bottlenecked by rollout generation, making end-to-end training slow. Recent work mitigates this by running rollouts with quantization to accelerate decoding, which is the most expensive stage of the RL loop. However, these setups destabilize optimization by amplifying the training-inference gap: rollouts are operated at low precision, while learning updates are computed at full precision. To address this challenge, we propose QaRL (Rollout Alignment Quantization-Aware RL), which aligns training-side forward with the quantized rollout to minimize mismatch. We further identify a failure mode in quantized rollouts: long-form responses tend to produce repetitive, garbled tokens (error tokens). To mitigate these problems, we introduce TBPO (Trust-Band Policy Optimization), a sequence-level objective with dual clipping for negative samples, aimed at keeping updates within the trust region. On Qwen3-30B-A3B MoE for math problems, QaRL outperforms quantized-rollout training by +5.5 while improving stability and preserving low-bit throughput benefits.

🏫 Affiliations: Unknown Institution

💡 Highlight: Proposes a training-free latent reasoning framework (SeLaR) that selectively activates soft embeddings for low-confidence steps, directly addressing core challenges in latent CoT.
View Abstract

arXiv:2604.08299v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Chain-of-Thought (CoT) has become a cornerstone of reasoning in large language models, yet its effectiveness is constrained by the limited expressiveness of discrete token sampling. Recent latent reasoning approaches attempt to alleviate this limitation by replacing discrete tokens with soft embeddings (probability-weighted mixtures of token embeddings) or hidden states, but they commonly suffer from two issues: (1) global activation injects perturbations into high-confidence steps, impairing reasoning stability; and (2) soft embeddings quickly collapse toward the highest-probability token, limiting exploration of alternative trajectories. To address these challenges, we propose SeLaR (Selective Latent Reasoning), a lightweight and training-free framework. SeLaR introduces an entropy-gated mechanism that activates soft embeddings only at low-confidence steps, while preserving discrete decoding at high-confidence steps. Additionally, we propose an entropy-aware contrastive regularization that pushes soft embeddings away from the dominant (highest-probability) token's direction, encouraging sustained exploration of multiple latent reasoning paths. Experiments on five reasoning benchmarks demonstrate that SeLaR consistently outperforms standard CoT and state-of-the-art training-free methods.

View Abstract

arXiv:2604.07165v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Reinforcement learning for Large Language Model agents is often hindered by sparse rewards in multi-step reasoning tasks. Existing approaches like Group Relative Policy Optimization treat sampled trajectories as independent chains, assigning uniform credit to all steps in each chain and ignoring the existence of critical steps that may disproportionally impact reasoning outcome. In this paper, we propose T-STAR(Tree-structured Self-Taught Agent Rectification), a framework that recovers the latent correlated reward structure across seemingly independent trajectories. Specifically, we consolidate trajectories into a unified Cognitive Tree by identifying and merging functionally similar steps/nodes. It enables an Introspective Valuation mechanism that back-propagates trajectory-level rewards through the tree to obtain a new notion of variance-reduced relative advantage at step-level. Using the Cognitive Tree, we also develop In-Context Thought Grafting to synthesize corrective reasoning by contrasting successful and failed branches at critical divergence points/steps. Our proposed Surgical Policy Optimization then capitalizes on the rich policy gradient information concentrated at these critical points/steps through a Bradley-Terry type of surgical loss. Extensive experiments across embodied, interactive, reasoning, and planning benchmarks demonstrate that T-STAR achieves consistent improvements over strong baselines, with gains most pronounced on tasks requiring extended reasoning chains.

arXiv:2604.08527v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: On-policy distillation (OPD) trains student models under their own induced distribution while leveraging supervision from stronger teachers. We identify a failure mode of OPD: as training progresses, on-policy rollouts can undergo abrupt length inflation, causing truncated trajectories to dominate the training data. This truncation collapse coincides with abrupt repetition saturation and induces biased gradient signals, leading to severe training instability and sharp degradation in validation performance. We attribute this problem to the interaction between student-induced data collection and the distillation objective, which implicitly favors long and repetitive rollouts. arXiv:2604.08476v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Multimodal reasoning models (MRMs) trained with reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards (RLVR) show improved accuracy on visual reasoning benchmarks. However, we observe that accuracy gains often come at the cost of reasoning quality: generated Chain-of-Thought (CoT) traces are frequently inconsistent with the final answer and poorly grounded in the visual evidence. We systematically study this phenomenon across seven challenging real-world spatial reasoning benchmarks and find that it affects contemporary MRMs such as ViGoRL-Spatial, TreeVGR as well as our own models trained with standard Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO). We evaluate FGRPO on Qwen2.5-VL-7B and 3B backbones across seven spatial datasets. 👥 Authors: Harsh Kohli, Srinivasan Parthasarathy, Huan Sun, Yuekun Yao

🏫 Affiliations: Unknown Institution

💡 Highlight: Proposes and analyzes recurrent-depth transformers for implicit multi-hop reasoning, directly addressing latent reasoning and compositional generalization.
View Abstract

arXiv:2604.07822v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: We study implicit reasoning, i.e. the ability to combine knowledge or rules within a single forward pass. While transformer-based large language models store substantial factual knowledge and rules, they often fail to compose this knowledge for implicit multi-hop reasoning, suggesting a lack of compositional generalization over their parametric knowledge. To address this limitation, we study recurrent-depth transformers, which enables iterative computation over the same transformer layers. We investigate two compositional generalization challenges under the implicit reasoning scenario: systematic generalization, i.e. combining knowledge that is never used for compositions during training, and depth extrapolation, i.e. generalizing from limited reasoning depth (e.g. training on up to 5-hop) to deeper compositions (e.g. 10-hop). Through controlled studies with models trained from scratch, we show that while vanilla transformers struggle with both generalization challenges, recurrent-depth transformers can effectively make such generalization. For systematic generalization, we find that this ability emerges through a three-stage grokking process, transitioning from memorization to in-distribution generalization and finally to systematic generalization, supported by mechanistic analysis. For depth extrapolation, we show that generalization beyond training depth can be unlocked by scaling inference-time recurrence, with more iterations enabling deeper reasoning. We further study how training strategies affect extrapolation, providing guidance on training recurrent-depth transformers, and identify a key limitation, overthinking, where excessive recurrence degrades predictions and limits generalization to very deep compositions.

arXiv:2604.07716v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: We present MIPT-SSM, a neural sequence architecture built on the physics of Measurement-Induced Phase Transitions (MIPT). The central idea is a learned measurement rate $p_{t}\in(0,1)$ that routes computation between two regimes: wave phase $(p_{t}\rightarrow0)$, where information propagates as distributed complex-phase interference; and particle phase $(p_{t}\rightarrow1)$ where the state collapses onto the current token, enabling precise local storage. These two regimes are provably incompatible in a single linear operator one of the few "no-go theorems" in sequence modeling and $p_{t}$ is our way around it. The model is predicted to exhibit a phase transition at critical sequence length $N^{*}\approx1024$, where the information density ratio $N/D$ crosses unity, consistent with our memory scaling observations. On AG News (four-class classification), MIPT achieves 0.905 accuracy versus Transformer's 0.736 (+16.6%), stable across 3 seeds. At $N=8192$ MIPT requires 810 MB versus Transformer's 34,651 MB a 42.8x memory reduction. On exact-recall ("needle-in-a-haystack"), our causal sparse KV cache achieves 0.968 accuracy. Remarkably, under unbounded cache capacity, the $p_{t}$ gate autonomously learns to store only the single critical token (averaging $1.0/512$ slots used), filtering out all noise and achieving a 99.8% sparsity rate. On language modeling (WikiText-103, 31M parameters), MIPT-LM with $K=64$ cache reaches PPL 92.1 versus Transformer's 90.5 (gap: 1.8%) while inference KV cache shrinks from $O(N)$ to $O(64)$.

ArXiv ID: 2604.07941 | Relevance Score: 7/10 | Novelty Score: 7/10

👥 Authors: Shiwan Zhao, Zhihu Wang, Xuyang Zhao, Jiaming Zhou, Caiyue Xu, Chenfei Liu, Liting Zhang, Yuhang Jia, Yanzhe Zhang, Hualong Yu, Zichen Xu, Qicheng Li, Yong Qin

🏫 Affiliations: Unknown Institution

💡 Highlight: Provides a unified theoretical framework for LLM post-training, explicitly analyzing RL and on-policy/off-policy learning regimes central to RL for LLMs.
View Abstract

arXiv:2604.07941v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Post-training has become central to turning pretrained large language models (LLMs) into aligned and deployable systems. Recent progress spans supervised fine-tuning (SFT), preference optimization, reinforcement learning (RL), process supervision, verifier-guided methods, distillation, and multi-stage pipelines. Yet these methods are often discussed in fragmented ways, organized by labels or objective families rather than by the behavioral bottlenecks they address. This survey argues that LLM post-training is best understood as structured intervention on model behavior. We organize the field first by trajectory provenance, which defines two primary learning regimes: off-policy learning on externally supplied trajectories, and on-policy learning on learner-generated rollouts. We then interpret methods through two recurring roles -- effective support expansion, which makes useful behaviors more reachable, and policy reshaping, which improves behavior within already reachable regions -- together with a complementary systems-level role, behavioral consolidation, which preserves, transfers, and amortizes behavior across stages and model transitions. This perspective yields a unified reading of major paradigms. SFT may serve either support expansion or policy reshaping, whereas preference-based methods are usually off-policy reshaping. On-policy RL often improves behavior on learner-generated states, though under stronger guidance it can also make hard-to-reach reasoning paths reachable. Distillation is often best understood as consolidation rather than only compression, and hybrid pipelines emerge as coordinated multi-stage compositions. Overall, the framework helps diagnose post-training bottlenecks and reason about stage composition, suggesting that progress in LLM post-training increasingly depends on coordinated system design rather than any single dominant objective.

arXiv:2604.07666v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR) has become a prominent method for post-training Large Language Models (LLMs). However, verifiers are rarely error-free; even deterministic checks can be inaccurate, and the growing dependence on model-based judges exacerbates the issue. The extent to which RLVR is robust to such noise and the verifier accuracy required for effective training remain unresolved questions. Research Interest Criteria

Please evaluate papers using a strict filter. We only want a small set of strongly aligned papers, not a broad survey of adjacent work.

Global Selection Principle

  • Mark a paper as relevant only if one of the three topics below is a central contribution of the paper, not a side application, benchmark, or discussion point.
  • Be conservative by default: if the connection is indirect, partial, or ambiguous, treat it as not relevant or score it low.
  • Prefer papers that introduce new methods, training frameworks, reward designs, agent improvement loops, or latent reasoning architectures.
  • Do not favor papers just because they mention "LLM", "agent", "reasoning", "RL", "alignment", or "CoT" in a generic way.
  • Exclude broad foundation model papers, generic post-training papers, generic tool-use agents, application/system papers, domain-specific deployments, and pure benchmark papers unless their core method strongly matches one of the topics below.

1. Reinforcement Learning (RL) for LLMs

We care about RL methods that directly improve LLM behavior, alignment, or reasoning through explicit reward design, RL pipelines, verifier-driven optimization, or scalable preference/feedback mechanisms.

  • Strong match: RLHF/RLAIF variants, verifiable reward based RL, rejection sampling plus RL pipelines, reward model design, outcome/process reward methods, online RL for LLMs, and methods that materially improve helpfulness, honesty, harmlessness, or hallucination behavior.
  • Especially valuable: Papers that propose a new reward function, a new scalable RL training recipe, or a new data-generation / self-improvement loop for post-training LLMs.
  • Usually not relevant: Generic reinforcement learning papers, RL for robotics/control/games, prompt optimization without RL, supervised finetuning only, inference-time search without a training contribution, or alignment papers with no substantive RL component.

2. Agents for LLMs

We care specifically about self-evolving agents rather than static agent systems. The central question is whether the agent can improve itself over time through interaction, reflection, tool-use traces, self-generated supervision, or automated curriculum/data generation.

  • Strong match: Self-improving agent frameworks, reflection/self-critique loops that feed back into learning, trajectory-driven continual improvement, automatic tool-use policy improvement, environment interaction that produces new supervision, and long-horizon agent adaptation.
  • Especially valuable: Closed-loop systems showing measurable capability gain over time, reliable agent learning from interaction traces, or agent architectures that turn execution experience into better future performance.
  • Usually not relevant: Vanilla ReAct-style agents, workflow orchestration, multi-agent communication only, tool calling systems without learning, benchmark papers, memory systems without self-improvement, or application-focused agent papers.

3. Latent CoT/Reasoning

We care about methods that move reasoning away from verbose explicit text chains into compact latent computation while preserving or improving reasoning quality.

  • Strong match: Latent reasoning architectures, hidden-state reasoning, implicit CoT, recurrent depth / recurrent latent computation, pause or thinking tokens for internal computation, explicit-to-latent distillation, and methods that reduce reasoning token cost or KV-cache use while retaining accuracy.
  • Especially valuable: New architectures or objectives for latent reasoning, hidden-state alignment/distillation, compression of reasoning traces, or methods that materially improve reasoning efficiency on math and logic tasks.
  • Usually not relevant: Standard explicit CoT prompting, generic reasoning finetuning, test-time scaling without latent computation, reasoning benchmarks, or papers whose main contribution is evaluation rather than a latent reasoning mechanism.

Final Preference

If a paper is merely adjacent to these areas, do not select it. Favor precision over recall. It is better to miss a borderline paper than to include many weakly related ones.
