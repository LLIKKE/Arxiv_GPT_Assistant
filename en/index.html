### Daily Research Summary

Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT)

Today's research underscores a significant trend toward refining the behavioral calibration of large language models through advanced SFT protocols. Moving beyond simple instruction-following, the focus is on instilling nuanced, human-like epistemic reasoning. The proposed frameworks demonstrate a shift from passive response generation to active decision-making, where models are trained to dynamically choose between answering, seeking clarification, or abstaining based on their confidence. This represents a maturation of SFT from a tool for capability enhancement to a precise method for aligning model behavior with complex, real-world interaction principles, often leveraging sophisticated synthetic data generation to teach these meta-cognitive skills.

Reinforcement Learning for LLMs

The frontier of LLM alignment is being expanded through the development of unified, multi-stage frameworks that seamlessly integrate SFT, preference optimization, and reinforcement learning. The key insight is treating post-training not as a series of isolated steps but as a cohesive pipeline, particularly for complex model families like diffusion-based LLMs. This holistic approach aims to efficiently navigate the trade-offs between capability preservation, alignment with human preferences, and the enforcement of safety constraints. It signals a move towards more systematic and executable roadmaps for the end-to-end refinement of advanced generative models, pushing the boundaries of what can be achieved through sequential and reinforcement-based learning techniques.

1. PassiveQA: A Three-Action Framework for Epistemically Calibrated Question Answering via Supervised Finetuning

ArXiv ID: 2604.04565 |

👥 Authors: Madhav S Baidya

🏫 Affiliations: Unknown Institution

💡 Highlight: Proposes a supervised fine-tuning (SFT) framework to teach LLMs epistemic awareness for deciding when to answer, ask, or abstain, directly aligning with SFT research on data-synthesis and fine-tuning protocols.
View Abstract

arXiv:2604.04565v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Large Language Models (LLMs) have achieved strong performance in question answering and retrieval-augmented generation (RAG), yet they implicitly assume that user queries are fully specified and answerable. In real-world settings, queries are often incomplete, ambiguous, or missing critical variables, leading models to produce overconfident or hallucinated responses. In this work, we study decision-aware query resolution under incomplete information, where a model must determine whether to Answer, Ask for clarification, or Abstain. We show that standard and enhanced RAG systems do not reliably exhibit such epistemic awareness, defaulting to answer generation even when information is insufficient. To address this, we propose PassiveQA, a three-action framework that aligns model behaviour with information sufficiency through supervised finetuning. Our approach integrates structured information-state representations, knowledge graph-grounded context, and a finetuned planner that explicitly models missing variables and decision reasoning. Experiments across multiple QA datasets show that the finetuned planner achieves significant improvements in macro F1 and abstention recall while reducing hallucination rates, under a compute-constrained training regime. These results provide strong empirical evidence that epistemic decision-making must be learned during training rather than imposed at inference time.

2. DARE: Diffusion Large Language Models Alignment and Reinforcement Executor

ArXiv ID: 2604.04215 |

👥 Authors: Jingyi Yang, Yuxian Jiang, Xuhao Hu, Shuang Cheng, Biqing Qi, Jing Shao

🏫 Affiliations: Unknown Institution

💡 Highlight: Introduces a unified framework (DARE) for post-training diffusion LLMs, covering supervised fine-tuning, preference optimization, and reinforcement learning, which is highly relevant to SFT and RL for LLMs research.
View Abstract

arXiv:2604.04215v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Diffusion large language models (dLLMs) are emerging as a compelling alternative to dominant autoregressive models, replacing strictly sequential token generation with iterative denoising and parallel generation dynamics. However, their open-source ecosystem remains fragmented across model families and, in particular, across post-training pipelines, where reinforcement learning objectives, rollout implementations and evaluation scripts are often released as paper-specific codebases. This fragmentation slows research iteration, raises the engineering burden of reproduction, and makes fair comparison across algorithms difficult. We present \textbf{DARE} (\textbf{d}LLMs \textbf{A}lignment and \textbf{R}einforcement \textbf{E}xecutor), an open framework for post-training and evaluating dLLMs. Built on top of verl~\cite{sheng2024hybridflow} and OpenCompass~\cite{2023opencompass}, DARE unifies supervised fine-tuning, parameter-efficient fine-tuning, preference optimization, and dLLM-specific reinforcement learning under a shared execution stack for both masked and block diffusion language models. Across representative model families including LLaDA, Dream, SDAR, and LLaDA2.x, DARE provides broad algorithmic coverage, reproducible benchmark evaluation, and practical acceleration. Extensive empirical results position that DARE serves as a reusable research substrate for developing, comparing, and deploying post-training methods for current and emerging dLLMs.

Research Interest Criteria

Please evaluate the papers based on the following three core areas:

1. Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) for LLMs

Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) is a critical technique for adapting pre-trained Large Language Models (LLMs) to specific downstream tasks or domains. This process involves further training the model on a curated dataset of high-quality, labeled examples that reflect the target behavior. Our research focuses on optimizing the SFT process to enhance model performance, alignment, and efficiency.

  • Key Direction 1: Investigating novel data-sourcing and data-synthesis strategies to construct high-quality, diverse, and instruction-rich SFT datasets.
  • Key Direction 2: Developing advanced SFT algorithms and optimization techniques to improve the efficiency and effectiveness of fine-tuning on specialized tasks.
  • Potential Contribution: Establishing best practices for curating SFT data and designing fine-tuning protocols that lead to more capable and reliable LLMs.

2. Reinforcement Learning (RL) for LLMs

Reinforcement Learning, particularly from human or AI-generated feedback, offers a powerful paradigm for aligning LLM behavior with complex human preferences and objectives. This research area explores methods beyond simple instruction-following, aiming to imbue models with more nuanced capabilities. Our work centers on creating robust RL pipelines and novel reward mechanisms to steer LLM development.

  • Relevance: We explore cutting-edge RL techniques, including rejection sampling and unsupervised reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards, to build sophisticated data pipelines that continuously enhance LLM capabilities.
  • Key Direction: Designing novel reward functions that directly target improvements in key metrics such as helpfulness, honesty, harmlessness, and reductions in hallucination.
  • Potential Contribution: Pioneering new research directions in RL for LLMs that inspire further investigation and bridge the gap between theoretical advancements and practical, large-scale model improvements.

3. Agents for LLMs

Autonomous agents built upon LLMs are a key step towards creating general-purpose problem-solving systems. Our emphasis is on self-evolving agents: agents that can iteratively improve their policies, tools, and internal knowledge through experience. This direction studies the mechanisms that make agent behavior not only autonomous at inference time, but continually improving over long horizons via self-generated feedback and data. The goal is to design agent architectures that translate self-evolution into measurable capability gains and strong real-world deployability.

  • Relevance: Centering on self-evolving agents—agent systems that autonomously generate supervision signals, curate experience, and improve themselves over time rather than remaining static after deployment.
  • Key Direction: Building closed-loop improvement frameworks, e.g., self-reflection and self-critique, automated curriculum generation, and learning from interaction traces (including tool-use trajectories) to drive continual adaptation.
  • Potential Contribution: Delivering practical, deployable self-evolving agents that demonstrate sustained improvements in reasoning, planning, and tool use, with clear evaluation protocols that quantify long-horizon learning progress and reliability.
