### Daily Research Summary

RL for LLMs

Today's research continues to refine the critical challenge of reward design in reinforcement learning for language models. A key advancement addresses the sparsity of response-level rewards by developing novel frameworks for token-level credit assignment. This approach, exemplified by rubric-to-token methods, aims to provide more granular and informative feedback signals during training, which is essential for improving the alignment and instruction-following capabilities of LLMs beyond what is achievable with standard supervised fine-tuning alone.

Agents for LLMs

The architecture and efficiency of LLM-based agent systems are under active scrutiny. A significant trend is the move towards more integrated, controllable reasoning frameworks that incorporate mechanisms like self-correction within a single model, moving beyond rigid multi-agent debates. Concurrently, empirical analysis is challenging the assumed efficiency of multi-agent systems, suggesting that under constrained computational budgets, sophisticated single-agent architectures can match or outperform their multi-agent counterparts on complex reasoning tasks. This points to a research direction favoring deeper, more controllable reasoning within unified models over distributed debate systems.

SFT (Supervised Fine-Tuning)

While not the primary focus of today's highlighted papers, supervised fine-tuning remains a foundational technique for instilling specific capabilities. It is notably employed as a core component within larger, more complex frameworks—such as those designed for controllable reasoning—to learn specialized sub-modules like correction mechanisms. This underscores SFT's role as a versatile and essential tool for initializing model behaviors before applying more advanced training paradigms like reinforcement learning.

1. Rubrics to Tokens: Bridging Response-level Rubrics and Token-level Rewards in Instruction Following Tasks

ArXiv ID: 2604.02795 |

👥 Authors: Tianze Xu, Yanzhao Zheng, Pengrui Lu, Lyumanshan Ye, Yong Wu, Zhentao Zhang, Yuanqiang Yu, Chao Ma, Jihuai Zhu, Pengfei Liu, Baohua Dong, Hangcheng Zhu, Ruohui Huang, Gang Yu

🏫 Affiliations: Unknown Institution

💡 Highlight: Directly addresses RL for LLMs by proposing a novel rubric-based RL framework (RTT) that tackles reward sparsity via token-level credit assignment, aligning with the key direction of designing novel reward functions.
View Abstract

arXiv:2604.02795v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Rubric-based Reinforcement Learning (RL) has emerged as a promising approach for aligning Large Language Models (LLMs) with complex, open-domain instruction following tasks. However, existing methods predominantly rely on response-level rewards, introducing severe reward sparsity and reward ambiguity problems. To address these issues, we propose Rubrics to Tokens (RTT), a novel rubric-based RL framework that bridges coarse response-level scores and fine-grained token-level credit assignment. RTT introduces a Token-Level Relevance Discriminator to predict which tokens in the response are responsible for a specific constraint, and optimizes the policy model via RTT-GRPO, which integrates response-level and token-level advantages within a unified framework. Furthermore, when transitioning from one-dimensional, outcome-level reward to three-dimensional reward space in the token-level rubric-based RL, we propose a novel group normalization method, called Intra-sample Token Group Normalization, to accommodate this shift. Extensive experiments and benchmarks demonstrate that RTT consistently outperforms other baselines in both instruction- and rubric-level accuracy across different models.

2. NeuReasoner: Towards Explainable, Controllable, and Unified Reasoning via Mixture-of-Neurons

👥 Authors: Haonan Dong, Kehan Jiang, Haoran Ye, Wenhao Zhu, Zhaolu Kang, Guojie Song

🏫 Affiliations: Unknown Institution

💡 Highlight: Relevant to Agents for LLMs as it introduces a controllable reasoning framework (NeuReasoner) with self-correction mechanisms, and to SFT as it uses SFT to learn the correction mechanism.
View Abstract

arXiv:2604.02972v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Large Reasoning Models (LRMs) have recently achieved remarkable success in complex reasoning tasks. However, closer scrutiny reveals persistent failure modes compromising performance and cost: I) Intra-step level, marked by calculation or derivation errors; II) Inter-step level, involving oscillation and stagnation; and III) Instance level, causing maladaptive over-thinking. Existing endeavors target isolated levels without unification, while their black-box nature and reliance on RL hinder explainability and controllability. To bridge these gaps, we conduct an in-depth white-box analysis, identifying key neurons (Mixture of Neurons, MoN) and their fluctuation patterns associated with distinct failures. Building upon these insights, we propose NeuReasoner, an explainable, controllable, and unified reasoning framework driven by MoN. Technically, NeuReasoner integrates lightweight MLPs for failure detection with a special token-triggered self-correction mechanism learned via SFT. During inference, special tokens are inserted upon failure detection to actuate controllable remedial behaviors. Extensive evaluations across six benchmarks, six backbone models (8B~70B) against nine competitive baselines, demonstrate that NeuReasoner achieves performance gains of up to 27.0% while reducing token consumption by 19.6% ~ 63.3%.

3. Single-Agent LLMs Outperform Multi-Agent Systems on Multi-Hop Reasoning Under Equal Thinking Token Budgets

ArXiv ID: 2604.02460 |

👥 Authors: Dat Tran, Douwe Kiela

🏫 Affiliations: Unknown Institution

💡 Highlight: Directly relevant to Agents for LLMs, providing a critical analysis of multi-agent vs. single-agent LLM systems under controlled compute budgets, which informs the design of efficient agent architectures.
View Abstract

arXiv:2604.02460v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Recent work reports strong performance from multi-agent LLM systems (MAS), but these gains are often confounded by increased test-time computation. When computation is normalized, single-agent systems (SAS) can match or outperform MAS, yet the theoretical basis and evaluation methodology behind this comparison remain unclear. We present an information-theoretic argument, grounded in the Data Processing Inequality, suggesting that under a fixed reasoning-token budget and with perfect context utilization, single-agent systems are more information-efficient. This perspective further predicts that multi-agent systems become competitive when a single agent's effective context utilization is degraded, or when more compute is expended. We test these predictions in a controlled empirical study across three model families (Qwen3, DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Llama, and Gemini 2.5), comparing SAS with multiple MAS architectures under matched budgets. We find that SAS consistently match or outperform MAS on multi-hop reasoning tasks when reasoning tokens are held constant. Beyond aggregate performance, we conduct a detailed diagnostic analysis of system behavior and evaluation methodology. We identify significant artifacts in API-based budget control (particularly in Gemini 2.5) and in standard benchmarks, both of which can inflate apparent gains from MAS. Overall, our results suggest that, for multi-hop reasoning tasks, many reported advantages of multi-agent systems are better explained by unaccounted computation and context effects rather than inherent architectural benefits, and highlight the importance of understanding and explicitly controlling the trade-offs between compute, context, and coordination in agentic systems.

Research Interest Criteria

Please evaluate the papers based on the following three core areas:

1. Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) for LLMs

Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) is a critical technique for adapting pre-trained Large Language Models (LLMs) to specific downstream tasks or domains. This process involves further training the model on a curated dataset of high-quality, labeled examples that reflect the target behavior. Our research focuses on optimizing the SFT process to enhance model performance, alignment, and efficiency.

  • Key Direction 1: Investigating novel data-sourcing and data-synthesis strategies to construct high-quality, diverse, and instruction-rich SFT datasets.
  • Key Direction 2: Developing advanced SFT algorithms and optimization techniques to improve the efficiency and effectiveness of fine-tuning on specialized tasks.
  • Potential Contribution: Establishing best practices for curating SFT data and designing fine-tuning protocols that lead to more capable and reliable LLMs.

2. Reinforcement Learning (RL) for LLMs

Reinforcement Learning, particularly from human or AI-generated feedback, offers a powerful paradigm for aligning LLM behavior with complex human preferences and objectives. This research area explores methods beyond simple instruction-following, aiming to imbue models with more nuanced capabilities. Our work centers on creating robust RL pipelines and novel reward mechanisms to steer LLM development.

  • Relevance: We explore cutting-edge RL techniques, including rejection sampling and unsupervised reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards, to build sophisticated data pipelines that continuously enhance LLM capabilities.
  • Key Direction: Designing novel reward functions that directly target improvements in key metrics such as helpfulness, honesty, harmlessness, and reductions in hallucination.
  • Potential Contribution: Pioneering new research directions in RL for LLMs that inspire further investigation and bridge the gap between theoretical advancements and practical, large-scale model improvements.

3. Agents for LLMs

Autonomous agents built upon LLMs are a key step towards creating general-purpose problem-solving systems. Our emphasis is on self-evolving agents: agents that can iteratively improve their policies, tools, and internal knowledge through experience. This direction studies the mechanisms that make agent behavior not only autonomous at inference time, but continually improving over long horizons via self-generated feedback and data. The goal is to design agent architectures that translate self-evolution into measurable capability gains and strong real-world deployability.

  • Relevance: Centering on self-evolving agents—agent systems that autonomously generate supervision signals, curate experience, and improve themselves over time rather than remaining static after deployment.
  • Key Direction: Building closed-loop improvement frameworks, e.g., self-reflection and self-critique, automated curriculum generation, and learning from interaction traces (including tool-use trajectories) to drive continual adaptation.
  • Potential Contribution: Delivering practical, deployable self-evolving agents that demonstrate sustained improvements in reasoning, planning, and tool use, with clear evaluation protocols that quantify long-horizon learning progress and reliability.
