💡 Daily Research Summary

### Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT)

Today's research underscores a sophisticated maturation of SFT methodologies, moving beyond simple instruction-response pairs to systematic, data-centric engineering. A clear trend is the use of progressive, multi-stage training pipelines, often integrated with synthetic data generation, to build specialized capabilities—from theorem proving in small models to domain-specific expertise in areas like radiology and document parsing. Key innovations include novel data selection strategies, where findings suggest that even random sampling can be surprisingly effective for modern LLMs in online DPO, and the explicit disentanglement of reasoning trace features to guide more targeted fine-tuning. The overarching theme is a shift towards treating SFT not as a one-off step but as a carefully orchestrated, iterative process of data curation and model refinement, often serving as a critical foundation for subsequent reinforcement learning.

Reinforcement Learning for LLMs

The frontier of RL for LLMs is being aggressively expanded along multiple axes: improving exploration, ensuring robustness, and scaling application. A major focus is on refining policy optimization algorithms, with novel frameworks like AsymGRPO modulating entropy to sustain exploration in reasoning tasks and methods like SignCert-PO being introduced to certify advantage sign robustness and mitigate reward hacking. There is significant work on designing better reward mechanisms, including process reward models (PRMs) for step-level guidance and the use of self-generated internal rewards. Furthermore, research is scaling RL to new domains, such as visual reasoning with open recipes like Vero, and deeply integrating it with other techniques like self-distillation to stabilize training. The collective direction points towards more sample-efficient, stable, and verifiably aligned RL pipelines that can handle the long-horizon, compositional nature of advanced reasoning and agentic tasks.

Agents & Self-Evolving Systems

Agent research is experiencing a Cambrian explosion, with today's papers converging on architectures for autonomous, self-improving systems. The core paradigm is one of closed-loop evolution: agents that collect data through interaction or simulation, learn from it (via SFT, RL, or prompt learning), and iteratively refine their policies, skills, or even internal structures. This is enabled by several key components:evolutionary algorithms(e.g., Elo-guided, co-evolutionary self-play) for optimizing agent code or strategies under tight budgets;automated skill library construction(SkillX, SkillFoundry) for continual knowledge accumulation and reuse; andbiologically-inspired memory systemsthat implement forgetting, retrieval, and lifecycle dynamics. The work moves beyond single-agent capabilities to study multi-agent collaboration, Theory-of-Mind emergence, and formal operational definitions of agency itself. The ultimate goal is creating agents that can autonomously navigate complex, open-ended environments and improve their own competence over time.

Multimodal & Latent Reasoning

Research in multimodal and latent reasoning is focused on making models more active, efficient, and structurally sound. A prominent trend is moving models from passive observers toactive interrogatorsof perceptual data, as seen in frameworks that use dynamic latent state probes to query visual features. Efficiency is pursued throughreasoning compression, dynamically condensing verbose chains-of-thought into compact latent representations to reduce overhead. There is also a strong push forstructured reasoning pipelines, such as hierarchical planning with latent world models or multi-stage alignment for causal video reasoning, which impose beneficial inductive biases. Concurrently, foundational work investigates the latent mechanisms of steering (e.g., function vectors) and the trustworthiness trade-offs of compressing reasoning traces. The field is thus advancing on both the architectural front, building more capable and efficient systems, and the analytical front, deepening our understanding of how reasoning is implemented under the hood.

Compression & Efficiency

Efficiency research is increasingly sophisticated, targeting not just model size but the computational footprint of complex cognitive processes like reasoning and multi-agent inference. A key insight is thereuse of computational states: frameworks propose having large models leverage reasoning tokens generated by smaller models, or reusing rollout prefixes in agentic search training to improve data efficiency. This aligns with the broader theme ofdynamic resource management, where systems like LightThinker++ aim to compress reasoning traces on-the-fly and memory systems implement cognitive quantization. The goal is to decouple performance from raw computational cost, enabling the deployment of advanced agentic and reasoning capabilities in more practical, scalable settings by minimizing redundant computation and intelligently allocating latent representational capacity.

arXiv:2604.03098v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Large language model (LLM) agents learn by interacting with environments, but long-horizon training remains fundamentally bottlenecked by sparse and delayed rewards. Existing methods typically address this challenge through post-hoc credit assignment or external reward models, which provide limited guidance at inference time and often separate reward improvement from policy improvement. 3. LLM Reasoning with Process Rewards for Outcome-Guided Steps

ArXiv ID: 2604.02341 |

👥 Authors: Mohammad Rezaei, Jens Lehmann, Sahar Vahdati

🏫 Affiliations: Unknown Institution

💡 Highlight: Introduces PROGRS, a framework using process reward models (PRMs) and outcome-conditioned RL (GRPO) to improve mathematical reasoning in LLMs, directly relevant to RL for LLMs and latent reasoning.
View Abstract

arXiv:2604.02341v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Mathematical reasoning in large language models has improved substantially with reinforcement learning using verifiable rewards, where final answers can be checked automatically and converted into reliable training signals. Most such pipelines optimize outcome correctness only, which yields sparse feedback for long, multi-step solutions and offers limited guidance on intermediate reasoning errors. Recent work therefore introduces process reward models (PRMs) to score intermediate steps and provide denser supervision. In practice, PRM scores are often imperfectly aligned with final correctness and can reward locally fluent reasoning that still ends in an incorrect answer. When optimized as absolute rewards, such signals can amplify fluent failure modes and induce reward hacking. We propose PROGRS, a framework that leverages PRMs while keeping outcome correctness dominant. PROGRS treats process rewards as relative preferences within outcome groups rather than absolute targets. We introduce outcome-conditioned centering, which shifts PRM scores of incorrect trajectories to have zero mean within each prompt group. It removes systematic bias while preserving informative rankings. PROGRS combines a frozen quantile-regression PRM with a multi-scale coherence evaluator. We integrate the resulting centered process bonus into Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) without auxiliary objectives or additional trainable components. Across MATH-500, AMC, AIME, MinervaMath, and OlympiadBench, PROGRS consistently improves Pass@1 over outcome-only baselines and achieves stronger performance with fewer rollouts. These results show that outcome-conditioned centering enables safe and effective use of process rewards for mathematical reasoning.

View Abstract

arXiv:2604.04157v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Theory of Mind (ToM) -- the ability to model others' mental states -- is fundamental to human social cognition. Whether large language models (LLMs) can develop ToM has been tested exclusively through static vignettes, leaving open whether ToM-like reasoning can emerge through dynamic interaction. Here we report that autonomous LLM agents playing extended sessions of Texas Hold'em poker progressively develop sophisticated opponent models, but only when equipped with persistent memory. In a 2x2 factorial design crossing memory (present/absent) with domain knowledge (present/absent), each with five replications (N = 20 experiments, ~6,000 agent-hand observations), we find that memory is both necessary and sufficient for ToM-like behavior emergence (Cliff's delta = 1.0, p = 0.008). Agents with memory reach ToM Level 3-5 (predictive to recursive modeling), while agents without memory remain at Level 0 across all replications. Strategic deception grounded in opponent models occurs exclusively in memory-equipped conditions (Fisher's exact p < 0.001). Domain expertise does not gate ToM-like behavior emergence but enhances its application: agents without poker knowledge develop equivalent ToM levels but less precise deception (p = 0.004). Agents with ToM deviate from game-theoretically optimal play (67% vs. 79% TAG adherence, delta = -1.0, p = 0.008) to exploit specific opponents, mirroring expert human play. All mental models are expressed in natural language and directly readable, providing a transparent window into AI social cognition. Cross-model validation with GPT-4o yields weighted Cohen's kappa = 0.81 (almost perfect agreement). These findings demonstrate that functional ToM-like behavior can emerge from interaction dynamics alone, without explicit training or prompting, with implications for understanding artificial social intelligence and biological social cognition.

arXiv:2604.03189v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Generally capable agents must learn from experience in ways that generalize across tasks and environments. The fundamental problems of learning, including credit assignment, overfitting, forgetting, local optima, and high-variance learning signals, persist whether the learned object lies in parameter space or context space. While these challenges are well understood in classical machine learning optimization, they remain underexplored in context space, leading current methods to be fragmented and ad hoc. We present Reflective Context Learning (RCL), a unified framework for agents that learn through repeated interaction, reflection on behavior and failure modes, and iterative updates to context. In RCL, reflection converts trajectories and current context into a directional update signal analogous to gradients, while mutation applies that signal to improve future behavior in context space. We recast recent context-optimization approaches as instances of this shared learning problem and systematically extend them with classical optimization primitives, including batching, improved credit-assignment signal, auxiliary losses, failure replay, and grouped rollouts for variance reduction. On AppWorld, BrowseComp+, and RewardBench2, these primitives improve over strong baselines, with their relative importance shifting across task regimes. We further analyze robustness to initialization, the effects of batch size, sampling and curriculum strategy, optimizer-state variants, and the impact of allocating stronger or weaker models to different optimization components. Our results suggest that learning through context updates should be treated not as a set of isolated algorithms, but as an optimization problem whose mechanisms can be studied systematically and improved through transferable principles.

Please evaluate the papers based on the following three core areas:

1. Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) for LLMs

Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) is a critical technique for adapting pre-trained Large Language Models (LLMs) to specific downstream tasks or domains. This process involves further training the model on a curated dataset of high-quality, labeled examples that reflect the target behavior. Our research focuses on optimizing the SFT process to enhance model performance, alignment, and efficiency.

  • Key Direction 1: Investigating novel data-sourcing and data-synthesis strategies to construct high-quality, diverse, and instruction-rich SFT datasets.
  • Key Direction 2: Developing advanced SFT algorithms and optimization techniques to improve the efficiency and effectiveness of fine-tuning on specialized tasks.
  • Potential Contribution: Establishing best practices for curating SFT data and designing fine-tuning protocols that lead to more capable and reliable LLMs.

2. Reinforcement Learning (RL) for LLMs

Reinforcement Learning, particularly from human or AI-generated feedback, offers a powerful paradigm for aligning LLM behavior with complex human preferences and objectives. This research area explores methods beyond simple instruction-following, aiming to imbue models with more nuanced capabilities. Our work centers on creating robust RL pipelines and novel reward mechanisms to steer LLM development.

  • Relevance: We explore cutting-edge RL techniques, including rejection sampling and unsupervised reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards, to build sophisticated data pipelines that continuously enhance LLM capabilities.
  • Key Direction: Designing novel reward functions that directly target improvements in key metrics such as helpfulness, honesty, harmlessness, and reductions in hallucination.
  • Potential Contribution: Pioneering new research directions in RL for LLMs that inspire further investigation and bridge the gap between theoretical advancements and practical, large-scale model improvements.

3. Agents for LLMs

Autonomous agents built upon LLMs are a key step towards creating general-purpose problem-solving systems. Our emphasis is on self-evolving agents: agents that can iteratively improve their policies, tools, and internal knowledge through experience. This direction studies the mechanisms that make agent behavior not only autonomous at inference time, but continually improving over long horizons via self-generated feedback and data. The goal is to design agent architectures that translate self-evolution into measurable capability gains and strong real-world deployability.

  • Relevance: Centering on self-evolving agents—agent systems that autonomously generate supervision signals, curate experience, and improve themselves over time rather than remaining static after deployment.
  • Key Direction: Building closed-loop improvement frameworks, e.g., self-reflection and self-critique, automated curriculum generation, and learning from interaction traces (including tool-use trajectories) to drive continual adaptation.
  • Potential Contribution: Delivering practical, deployable self-evolving agents that demonstrate sustained improvements in reasoning, planning, and tool use, with clear evaluation protocols that quantify long-horizon learning progress and reliability.

4. Latent CoT/Reasoning

Latent Chain-of-Thought (CoT) or implicit reasoning aims to shift the reasoning process of Large Language Models (LLMs) from explicit, step-by-step natural language text generation into a continuous latent representation space (hidden vectors or states). While traditional explicit CoT significantly enhances multi-step reasoning capabilities, it incurs high computational costs, severe inference latency, and redundant "overthinking". This research area explores methods beyond standard autoregressive text generation, allowing models to reason "silently" and efficiently internally, retaining the power of CoT while drastically reducing the overhead of generating intermediate steps. Our work centers on building efficient latent reasoning frameworks and alignment mechanisms to steer models to complete complex tasks in compact latent spaces.

  • Relevance: We explore cutting-edge latent reasoning techniques, including compressing explicit reasoning trajectories into continuous vectors via self-distillation, applying reinforcement learning (RL) without explicit CoT data supervision, introducing special placeholder tokens (such as <pause> or <st> tokens) for extended computation, and utilizing recurrent depth architectures to unroll reasoning in latent space. These methods aim to build sophisticated data and training pipelines that continuously enhance computational efficiency and reasoning capabilities.
  • Key Direction: Designing novel reasoning networks and objective functions, such as transferring knowledge from teacher to student models through hidden state alignment, combining dynamic compression factors with probabilistic latent heads to encourage exploration and exploitation of shorter reasoning paths, and leveraging spatial inductive biases (e.g., rendering CoT into images) to preserve global reasoning structures. These directions directly target improvements in key metrics, such as drastically reducing inference latency, minimizing KV-Cache memory footprint, and boosting accuracy on math and complex logic tasks.
  • Potential Contribution: Pioneering new research directions in LLM reasoning architectures that bridge the gap between the high accuracy of explicit CoT and the low inference cost of direct generation. By breaking away from the reliance on verbose and costly text generation, this not only provides a viable alternative for real-world, large-scale model deployments that are resource-constrained and latency-sensitive, but also inspires further exploration into high-dimensional continuous space reasoning beyond linguistic forms (e.g., spatial and physical intuitions).
