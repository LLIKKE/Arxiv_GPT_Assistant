### Daily Research Summary

RL for LLMs

A significant focus today is on refining the reward optimization process for large language models. The key advancement is the development of novel methods that directly address the persistent challenge of vanishing gradients on failure cases during policy training. By introducing techniques like privileged self-distillation, new frameworks aim to stabilize learning and improve the model's ability to learn from negative outcomes, moving beyond simple reward maximization to more nuanced policy optimization.

Agents & Multimodal

Research is converging on sophisticated agent architectures that leverage large language models as central orchestrators. The trend is toward creating robust, adaptable systems by integrating frozen, pre-trained vision-language-action models with dynamic, memory-augmented components. This approach enables in-context adaptation and strategic planning, allowing agents to reason over past experiences and adjust their behavior for improved robustness in complex, multimodal environments without retraining the core perceptual models.

1. HDPO: Hybrid Distillation Policy Optimization via Privileged Self-Distillation

ArXiv ID: 2603.23871 |

👥 Authors: Ken Ding

🏫 Affiliations: Unknown Institution

💡 Highlight: Proposes HDPO, a novel RL method for LLMs that uses privileged self-distillation to address vanishing gradients on failure cases, directly targeting RL for LLMs and reward function design.
View Abstract

arXiv:2603.23871v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Large language models trained with reinforcement learning (RL) for mathematical reasoning face a fundamental challenge: on problems the model cannot solve at all - "cliff" prompts - the RL gradient vanishes entirely, preventing any learning signal from reaching these failure modes. We introduce Hybrid Distillation Policy Optimization (HDPO), which augments standard RL with privileged self-distillation targeting cliff prompts. On each training step, HDPO identifies prompts where all rollouts fail, generates privileged rollouts by providing the model with ground-truth information, filters for correct solutions, and distills the teacher's token-level distribution into the student. Because teacher and student share the same weights - differing only in their input - the realizability gap is provably bounded, unlike cross-model distillation. We prove that R=1 filtered privileged generation recovers the optimal KL-regularized RL policy in the hard-threshold limit. Experiments on OpenMathInstruct-2 with Qwen2.5-Math-1.5B-Instruct show that HDPO consistently improves coverage metrics (pass@4 by +0.8-1.1%, pass@8 by +0.4-1.7%) while maintaining greedy accuracy, with the distillation weight lambda providing direct control over the exploration-exploitation tradeoff.

2. SOMA: Strategic Orchestration and Memory-Augmented System for Vision-Language-Action Model Robustness via In-Context Adaptation

ArXiv ID: 2603.24060 |

👥 Authors: Zhuoran Li, Zhiyang Li, Kaijun Zhou, Jinyu Gu

🏫 Affiliations: Unknown Institution

💡 Highlight: Proposes SOMA, a system that upgrades frozen VLA policies via an LLM orchestrator and memory-augmented in-context adaptation, aligning with interests in agents for LLMs and multimodal models.
View Abstract

arXiv:2603.24060v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Despite the promise of Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models as generalist robotic controllers, their robustness against perceptual noise and environmental variations in out-of-distribution (OOD) tasks remains fundamentally limited by the absence of long-term memory, causal failure attribution, and dynamic intervention capability. To address this, we propose SOMA, a Strategic Orchestration and Memory-Augmented System that upgrades frozen VLA policies for robust in-context adaptation without parameter fine-tuning. Specifically, SOMA operates through an online pipeline of contrastive Dual-Memory Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG), an Attribution-Driven Large-Language-Model (LLM) Orchestrator, and extensible Model Context Protocol (MCP) interventions, while an offline Memory Consolidation module continuously distills the execution traces into reliable priors. Experimental evaluations across three backbone models (pi0, pi0.5, and SmolVLA) on LIBERO-PRO and our proposed LIBERO-SOMA benchmarks demonstrate that SOMA achieves an average absolute success rate gain of 56.6%. This includes a significant absolute improvement of 89.1% in long-horizon task chaining. Project page and source code are available at: https://github.com/LZY-1021/SOMA.

Research Interest Criteria

Please evaluate the papers based on the following three core areas:

1. Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) for LLMs

Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) is a critical technique for adapting pre-trained Large Language Models (LLMs) to specific downstream tasks or domains. This process involves further training the model on a curated dataset of high-quality, labeled examples that reflect the target behavior. Our research focuses on optimizing the SFT process to enhance model performance, alignment, and efficiency.

  • Key Direction 1: Investigating novel data-sourcing and data-synthesis strategies to construct high-quality, diverse, and instruction-rich SFT datasets.
  • Key Direction 2: Developing advanced SFT algorithms and optimization techniques to improve the efficiency and effectiveness of fine-tuning on specialized tasks.
  • Potential Contribution: Establishing best practices for curating SFT data and designing fine-tuning protocols that lead to more capable and reliable LLMs.

2. Reinforcement Learning (RL) for LLMs

Reinforcement Learning, particularly from human or AI-generated feedback, offers a powerful paradigm for aligning LLM behavior with complex human preferences and objectives. This research area explores methods beyond simple instruction-following, aiming to imbue models with more nuanced capabilities. Our work centers on creating robust RL pipelines and novel reward mechanisms to steer LLM development.

  • Relevance: We explore cutting-edge RL techniques, including rejection sampling and unsupervised reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards, to build sophisticated data pipelines that continuously enhance LLM capabilities.
  • Key Direction: Designing novel reward functions that directly target improvements in key metrics such as helpfulness, honesty, harmlessness, and reductions in hallucination.
  • Potential Contribution: Pioneering new research directions in RL for LLMs that inspire further investigation and bridge the gap between theoretical advancements and practical, large-scale model improvements.

3. Agents for LLMs

Autonomous agents built upon LLMs are a key step towards creating general-purpose problem-solving systems. Our emphasis is on self-evolving agents: agents that can iteratively improve their policies, tools, and internal knowledge through experience. This direction studies the mechanisms that make agent behavior not only autonomous at inference time, but continually improving over long horizons via self-generated feedback and data. The goal is to design agent architectures that translate self-evolution into measurable capability gains and strong real-world deployability.

  • Relevance: Centering on self-evolving agents—agent systems that autonomously generate supervision signals, curate experience, and improve themselves over time rather than remaining static after deployment.
  • Key Direction: Building closed-loop improvement frameworks, e.g., self-reflection and self-critique, automated curriculum generation, and learning from interaction traces (including tool-use trajectories) to drive continual adaptation.
  • Potential Contribution: Delivering practical, deployable self-evolving agents that demonstrate sustained improvements in reasoning, planning, and tool use, with clear evaluation protocols that quantify long-horizon learning progress and reliability.
