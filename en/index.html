💡 Daily Research Summary

### On-Policy Distillation for LLMs A clear trend emerges with multiple papers advancing on-policy distillation (OPD) as a core methodology for improving LLMs. The field is moving beyond simple imitation learning toward more sophisticated techniques that address the fundamental training-inference mismatch. PowerOPD introduces a bounded power-transformation reward that stabilizes training dynamics and boosts reasoning accuracy over vanilla OPD. Zone of Proximal Policy Optimization (ZPPO) takes a novel approach by keeping the teacher in the prompt rather than the gradient, reformulating prompts to guide student learning without direct gradient interference. For diffusion language models, Learning from the Self-future (d-OPSD) provides the first on-policy self-distillation framework with step-level supervision, while Self-Generated Error Training enables token editors to fix their own draft errors. The quality-aware self-distillation method for GUI grounding demonstrates how correctness-aware gating can improve teacher signal quality. These works collectively suggest that OPD is maturing into a family of principled techniques that can stabilize training, improve sample efficiency, and enable continuous self-improvement without external supervision.

Reinforcement Learning for Reasoning and Efficiency

Reinforcement learning continues to drive breakthroughs in LLM reasoning, with several papers tackling fundamental limitations of current approaches. Dynamic Epistemic Entropy Orchestrated Erasable Reinforcement Learning (E3RL) directly confronts the autoregressive curse by using dynamic entropy thresholds to enable self-healing reasoning—excising early logical defects and reusing KV cache for long-horizon tasks. SuCo introduces a two-stage framework combining alignment fine-tuning with sufficiency-aware policy optimization to dynamically control reasoning length, directly targeting the efficiency-reliability trade-off. Dynamic Rollout Editing (DRE) addresses the overthinking problem in RL-trained models by editing rollout trajectories during GRPO-style training to improve credit assignment. Rethinking Groups in Critic-Free RLVR proposes negative token filtering for stable single-rollout training, eliminating data inefficiency from group synchronization. The theoretical framework in From Reasoning Traces to Reusable Modules provides crucial foundations, showing how SFT and RL play complementary roles in compositional generalization. These advances collectively indicate a shift toward more adaptive, efficient, and theoretically grounded RL methods that can handle the unique challenges of language model reasoning.

Latent Reasoning and Deep Looped Architectures

A emerging paradigm focuses on latent reasoning within transformer architectures, moving beyond explicit chain-of-thought. LoopCoder-v2 introduces a parallel loop transformer for latent reasoning, carefully studying the gain-cost trade-off of loop count and achieving strong code reasoning results. Fixed-Point Reasoners propose a looped transformer with guaranteed fixed-point convergence as an end-to-end halting mechanism, directly addressing signal propagation challenges in deep looped architectures. Learning to Refine Hidden States (ReLAR) uses reinforcement-guided latent refinement to iteratively update hidden representations before decoding, applying policy gradients at the representation level rather than token level. These approaches share a common insight: reasoning can happen in the latent space of transformer layers, potentially offering more efficient and controllable computation than autoregressive token generation. The convergence guarantees and adaptive halting mechanisms suggest this direction is moving toward practical, stable implementations.

Multimodal and Agentic Systems

The integration of RL with multimodal and agentic systems shows significant progress. See First, Answer Later introduces sufficiency-driven RL (via GRPO) for visual evidence pre-alignment in MLLMs, directly improving visual grounding and reasoning by ensuring models attend to relevant visual evidence before generating answers. MaineCoon pushes toward real-time audio-visual world models using reinforced online-policy distillation (ROPD) as a core technique, demonstrating how OPD can be adapted for multimodal autoregressive models. STAR provides spatiotemporally adaptive reward allocation for text-to-image RL post-training, using attention maps to focus policy updates on relevant latent regions—a critical advance for diffusion model fine-tuning. On the agentic front, EnvRL augments agentic RL with auxiliary environment dynamics learning objectives (state prediction and inverse dynamics), showing clear improvements on long-horizon tasks by helping models understand environment transitions. OPD-Evolver proposes a slow-fast co-evolution framework using on-policy self-distillation to cultivate holistic agent evolvers, suggesting that OPD can serve as a unifying mechanism for agent improvement across multiple dimensions.

1. Continual Self-Improvement with Lightweight Experiential Latent Memories

ArXiv ID: 2606.17803 | Relevance Score: 9/10 | Novelty Score: 9/10

👥 Authors: Vaggelis Dorovatas, Nancy Kalaj, Rahaf Aljundi

🏫 Affiliations: Unknown Institution

💡 Highlight: Proposes an online method that distills inference-time reasoning traces into compact latent memories using self-generated rewards, enabling continual self-improvement without external supervision.
View Abstract

arXiv:2606.17803v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Large language models achieve strong reasoning performance by scaling inference-time compute, yet remain fundamentally stateless, discarding the rich, self-produced reasoning traces generated during this process. We investigate whether models can instead learn online from this experience, converting transient computation (reasoning traces) into persistent reusable knowledge, and without external supervision or access to future data. We show that In-Context Learning (ICL) over raw reasoning traces fails to generalize, reflecting a fundamental limitation of token-level reuse: individual traces lack the abstraction needed for transfer, even after refinement (e.g. self-reflection). In contrast, drawing inspiration from recent works on unsupervised reinforcement learning, we find that lightweight per-instance training with self-generated test-time signals (majority voting) as rewards yields substantial gains, often surpassing full-dataset offline training, motivating a shift from raw traces to learned latent representations. Building on this insight, we propose an online method that distills inference-time compute spent on encountered problems into compact modular latent memories capturing the underlying reasoning structure. These memories are stored and retrieved for future inputs, enabling continual improvement while avoiding catastrophic forgetting through modular design. Importantly, our method is highly efficient, parametrized as extremely lightweight soft prompt memories (~0.001% of model parameters) and trained with only a few gradient steps, yet achieving performance competitive with full parametric updates and offline training. Across challenging mathematical reasoning benchmarks, our approach significantly outperforms zero-shot and raw data ICL baselines, while transferring effectively across datasets.

Existing OPSD methods are inherently autoregressive-centric. 3. PowerOPD: Stabilizing On-Policy Distillation with Bounded Power Transformation

ArXiv ID: 2606.17199 | Relevance Score: 9/10 | Novelty Score: 8/10

👥 Authors: Anhao Zhao, Junlong Tong, Yingqi Fan, Ping Nie, Wenjie Li, Xiaoyu Shen

🏫 Affiliations: Unknown Institution

💡 Highlight: Proposes PowerOPD, a bounded power-transformation reward for on-policy distillation that stabilizes training and improves reasoning accuracy over vanilla OPD.
View Abstract

arXiv:2606.17199v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Standard on-policy distillation (OPD) for large language models estimates the reverse-KL objective using student-sampled tokens, yielding an unbiased single-sample Monte Carlo estimator that avoids vocabulary-wide computation. However, we show that this estimator suffers from severe training pathologies in practice: sample inefficiency, unstable generation dynamics, and a substantial performance gap compared to exact full-vocabulary OPD. Reward-level diagnosis traces these pathologies to the log-ratio reward, which is unbounded by construction, producing extremely high-variance gradients concentrated at early positions and persisting throughout training; standard post-hoc scaling fail as they operate only after this distortion occurs. To solve this problem, we propose PowerOPD: a family of natively bounded, sign-consistent rewards from the Box-Cox power transformation, parameterized by alpha > 0, of which the log-ratio is the degenerate alpha -> 0 limit. Across six mathematical reasoning benchmarks and four Qwen3 teacher-student pairs, PowerOPD achieves benchmark-averaged Avg@8/Pass@8 gains of up to +6.37/+5.71 over vanilla OPD, +3.01/+3.54 over post-hoc stabilization, and +2.59/+8.90 over full-vocabulary OPD, while reducing wall-clock time by 59.2% and peak GPU memory by 23.1%. Larger alpha generally improves accuracy, consistently shortens responses, and keeps gradient norms more than 3,000x smaller than vanilla OPD.

🏫 Affiliations: Unknown Institution

💡 Highlight: Introduces a parallel loop Transformer architecture for latent reasoning and studies the gain-cost trade-off of loop count, achieving strong code reasoning results.
View Abstract

arXiv:2606.18023v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Looped Transformers scale latent computation by repeatedly applying shared blocks, but sequential looping increases latency and KV-cache memory with the loop count. Parallel loop Transformers (PLT) alleviate this cost through cross-loop position offsets (CLP) and shared-KV gated sliding-window attention, making loop count a practical design choice. We therefore study PLT loop-count selection through a gain--cost view: an extra loop may refine representations, but CLP also introduces a positional mismatch at each loop boundary. We instantiate this study by training LoopCoder-v2, a family of 7B PLT coders with different loop counts, from scratch on 18T tokens, followed by matched instruction tuning and evaluation. Empirically, the two-loop variant delivers broad gains over the non-looped baseline across code generation, code reasoning, agentic software engineering, and tool-use benchmarks, improving SWE-bench Verified from 43.0 to 64.4 points and Multi-SWE from 14.0 to 31.0 points. In contrast, variants with three or more loops regress, revealing a strongly non-monotonic loop-count effect. Our diagnostics show that loop 2 provides the main productive refinement, while later loops yield diminishing, oscillatory updates and reduced representational diversity. Because the CLP-induced mismatch remains roughly fixed as refinement gains shrink, the offset cost increasingly dominates. This gain--cost trade-off explains PLT's saturation at two loops and provides diagnostics for loop-count selection.

5. Zone of Proximal Policy Optimization: Teacher in Prompts, Not Gradients

ArXiv ID: 2606.18216 | Relevance Score: 9/10 | Novelty Score: 8/10

👥 Authors: Byung-Kwan Lee, Ximing Lu, Shizhe Diao, Minki Kang, Saurav Muralidharan, Karan Sapra, Andrew Tao, Pavlo Molchanov, Yejin Choi, Yu-Chiang Frank Wang, Ryo Hachiuma

🏫 Affiliations: Unknown Institution

💡 Highlight: Proposes ZPPO, a novel on-policy distillation method that keeps the teacher in the prompt rather than the gradient, using reformulated prompts and a replay buffer to improve small student models.
View Abstract

arXiv:2606.18216v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Knowledge distillation transfers a teacher's competence to a small student but is brittle in the small-student regime: forcing the student to imitate logits from a much larger teacher concentrates it on the teacher's sharpest modes, hurting generalization on benchmark families beyond the training corpus. Reinforcement learning (RL) avoids logit imitation by training on the student's own rollouts. However, on questions where every rollout fails-yielding zero advantage and being silently discarded-injecting a stronger teacher's response into the policy gradient breaks the on-policy assumption and induces drift. We introduce Zone of Proximal Policy Optimization (ZPPO), inspired by Vygotsky's zone of proximal development, which keeps the teacher inside the prompt rather than the policy gradient. On hard questions, ZPPO constructs two reformulated prompts: a Binary Candidate-included Question (BCQ) pairs one correct teacher response with one incorrect student response as anonymized candidates the student must discriminate, and a Negative Candidate-included Question (NCQ) aggregates the student's wrong rollouts into a single prompt to surface their shared failure modes. A prompt replay buffer recirculates each hard question until it either graduates-the student's mean rollout accuracy on it reaches half- or is FIFO-evicted under finite capacity, amplifying BCQ and NCQ inside the student's current zone of proximal development. On the Qwen3.5 family at four student scales (0.8B-9B) with a 27B teacher, post-trained as vision-language models and evaluated on a 31-benchmark suite (16 VLM, 10 LLM, 5 Video), ZPPO outperforms off/on-policy distillation and GRPO, with the largest gains at the smallest scale.

arXiv:2606.17735v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Although reinforcement learning (RL) has expanded the cognitive boundaries of large language models (LLMs), it often remains vulnerable to the autoregressive curse in long-horizon logical reasoning: small epistemic perturbations introduced early in generation can propagate irreversibly along the Markov decision process flow, triggering cascading failures that drive the reasoning trajectory toward collapse. 👥 Authors: Lingjing Kong, Xin Liu, Guangyi Chen, Martin Q. Ma, Xiangchen Song, Yuekai Sun, Mikhail Yurochkin, Taylor W. Killian, Ruslan Salakhutdinov, Kun Zhang, Eric P. Xing, Zhengzhong Liu

💡 Highlight: Provides a theoretical framework and controlled experiments showing how SFT and RL play complementary roles in compositional generalization for LLM reasoning, directly relevant to RL-for-LLMs.
View Abstract

arXiv:2606.18089v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Post-training pipelines that combine supervised fine-tuning (SFT) with reinforcement learning (RL) have emerged as the key recipe for transforming large language models (LLMs) into robust reasoners. We argue that this combined success is driven by compositional generalization, which we formalize through a hierarchical latent selection model. In this framework, reasoning traces are generated by a cascade of discrete latent selection variables corresponding to reusable atomic modules, including both skills (local operations) and routing mechanisms (how intermediate information is selected, reused, and composed). Within this model, we theoretically show that SFT and RL play asymmetric, complementary roles: SFT supplies the raw module materials in compositional traces, and RL decomposes those traces to identify the latent atomic modules and enable compositional generalization. We design controlled experiments to validate this theory. Our results demonstrate that RL can extract atomic modules from compound traces supplied by SFT and recombine them to solve new configurations. Moreover, we find that training on compound traces yields stronger generalization than training on isolated atomic modules. Finally, we investigate the relationship between SFT and RL data and identify an effective protocol in which SFT ensures coverage of all atomic modules through compositional traces, while RL focuses on novel compositions outside the SFT support to drive exploration.

10. Dynamic Rollout Editing for Reducing Overthinking in RL-Trained Reasoning Models

ArXiv ID: 2606.17890 | Relevance Score: 8/10 | Novelty Score: 8/10

👥 Authors: Zihao Wei, Wenjie Shi, Liang Pang, Jingcheng Deng, Shicheng Xu, Shasha Guo, Zenghao Duan, Jiahao Liu, Jingang Wang, Huawei Shen, Xueqi Cheng

🏫 Affiliations: Unknown Institution

💡 Highlight: Proposes Dynamic Rollout Editing (DRE), a training-time intervention for GRPO-style RL that edits overthinking rollouts to improve credit assignment and reduce unnecessary reasoning.
View Abstract

arXiv:2606.17890v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Long-form chain-of-thought reasoning can improve LLM performance on complex tasks, but models often continue generating unnecessary reasoning after a correct answer has emerged. We refer to this behavior as overthinking. We study this phenomenon from the perspective of GRPO-style reinforcement learning (RL) post-training, framing it as a training-time credit-assignment problem rather than merely a decoding-time stopping problem. In rollouts sampled at the onset of GRPO training, we observe that successful trajectories can exhibit a slightly higher degree of overthinking than unsuccessful trajectories for the same prompts. This early imbalance provides a starting point for an undesirable feedback loop: because GRPO assigns sequence-level credit, it cannot distinguish the solution-reaching prefix from the unnecessary continuation that lengthens a successful trajectory. Both receive positive update signal, allowing the initial imbalance to grow into more severe overthinking during training. To address this issue, we introduce Dynamic Rollout Editing (DRE), a training-time intervention for successful trajectories that continue thinking after answer emergence. DRE preserves the accepted verified prefix, edits the remaining thinking, and prefers the edited trajectory within the same RL group, weakening the preference signal for unnecessary thinking without penalizing the reasoning needed to reach the answer. Experiments across diverse tasks show the effectiveness of DRE.

Research Interest Criteria

Please evaluate papers using a strict filter. We only want a small set of strongly aligned papers, not a broad survey of adjacent work.

Global Selection Principle

  • Mark a paper as relevant only if one of the three topics below is a central contribution of the paper, not a side application, benchmark, or discussion point.
  • Be conservative by default: if the connection is indirect, partial, or ambiguous, treat it as not relevant or score it low.
  • Prefer papers that introduce new methods, training frameworks, reward designs, scalable self-improvement loops, or latent reasoning architectures with clear technical substance.
  • Do not favor papers just because they mention "LLM", "agent", "reasoning", "RL", "alignment", or "CoT" in a generic way.
  • Exclude broad foundation model papers, generic post-training papers, generic tool-use agents, application/system papers, domain-specific deployments, and pure benchmark papers unless their core method strongly matches one of the topics below.

1. Reinforcement Learning (RL) for LLMs

We care about RL methods that directly improve LLM behavior, alignment, or reasoning through explicit reward design, RL pipelines, verifier-driven optimization, or scalable preference/feedback mechanisms.

  • Strong match: RLHF/RLAIF variants, verifiable reward based RL, rejection sampling plus RL pipelines, reward model design, outcome/process reward methods, online RL for LLMs, and methods that materially improve helpfulness, honesty, harmlessness, or hallucination behavior.
  • Especially valuable: Papers that propose a new reward function, a new scalable RL training recipe, or a new data-generation / self-improvement loop for post-training LLMs.
  • Usually not relevant: Generic reinforcement learning papers, RL for robotics/control/games, prompt optimization without RL, supervised finetuning only, inference-time search without a training contribution, or alignment papers with no substantive RL component.

2. Latent CoT/Reasoning

We care about methods that move reasoning away from verbose explicit text chains into compact latent computation while preserving or improving reasoning quality.

  • Strong match: Latent reasoning architectures, hidden-state reasoning, implicit CoT, recurrent depth / recurrent latent computation, pause or thinking tokens for internal computation, explicit-to-latent distillation, and methods that reduce reasoning token cost or KV-cache use while retaining accuracy.
  • Especially valuable: New architectures or objectives for latent reasoning, hidden-state alignment/distillation, compression of reasoning traces, or methods that materially improve reasoning efficiency on math and logic tasks.
  • Usually not relevant: Standard explicit CoT prompting, generic reasoning finetuning, test-time scaling without latent computation, reasoning benchmarks, or papers whose main contribution is evaluation rather than a latent reasoning mechanism.

3. On-Policy Distillation for LLMs

We care about distillation methods that improve LLM capabilities by training on the model's own on-policy outputs, trajectories, preferences, corrections, or interaction data, especially when the student distribution is actively refreshed during training.

  • Strong match: On-policy distillation, self-distillation from sampled rollouts, online teacher-student distillation, policy-improvement distillation, iterative distillation from model-generated reasoning traces, distillation from verifier/reward-selected on-policy samples, and methods that close the gap between training data distribution and the model's current behavior.
  • Especially valuable: Papers that propose a new iterative distillation loop, sampling-and-selection strategy, online data refresh mechanism, teacher/student update schedule, or objective that measurably improves reasoning, instruction-following, alignment, tool use, or general LLM capability.
  • Usually not relevant: Standard offline knowledge distillation, static SFT on teacher-generated data, model compression only, data cleaning only, speculative decoding/distillation for speed only, or generic synthetic data generation without an on-policy or iterative policy-improvement component.

Final Preference

If a paper is merely adjacent to these areas, do not select it. Favor precision over recall. It is better to miss a borderline paper than to include many weakly related ones.
