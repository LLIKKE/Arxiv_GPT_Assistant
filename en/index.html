### Daily Research Summary

Date: April 16, 2024

Today's publications reveal significant advancements in enhancing the reasoning capabilities and operational efficiency of large language models, with a strong emphasis on inference-time algorithms, multimodal integration, and strategic training methodologies.

RL for LLMs & SFT

A clear trend emerges in the strategic combination of Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) and Reinforcement Learning (RL) to instill complex, expert-level reasoning into models. Rather than treating these as separate stages, researchers are developing integrated distillation pipelines. For instance, one paper demonstrates how to directly distill the sophisticated reasoning of a domain expert (e.g., a chess master) into an LLM by leveraging both SFT for behavior cloning and RL for reward-guided refinement. This approach moves beyond simple imitation learning, aiming to capture the underlying strategic decision-making processes, which is a key step toward creating more capable and reliable reasoning agents.

Agents & Reasoning

Research in agent-like planning is shifting focus toward sophisticated, budget-aware inference-time search algorithms. The key breakthrough is the development of tree search methods that are specifically designed for the non-deterministic and computationally expensive nature of LLMs. Today's work introduces algorithms that intelligently allocate a finite computational "budget" (e.g., a limit on token generations) across different reasoning paths. By employing principles like sequential halving and Gumbel-based exploration, these methods allow a model to dynamically prune less promising chains of thought and concentrate resources on the most viable solutions, significantly enhancing reasoning performance without altering the underlying model weights.

Multimodal

The frontier of multimodal reasoning is advancing beyond static image captioning or question-answering toward dynamic, interleaved reasoning processes. The latest frameworks treat visual and linguistic modalities as equal partners in a chain-of-thought. The innovation lies in generating "dynamic visual thoughts"—essentially, iterative and precise visual queries or focused image analyses—that are interleaved with textual reasoning steps. This creates a feedback loop where the model's linguistic reasoning informs what to look at next in the image, and the new visual information guides subsequent reasoning. This represents a move from passive multimodal understanding to active, efficient, and grounded multimodal problem-solving.

Compression & Efficiency

Efficiency research is expanding its scope from model compression (e.g., quantization, pruning) to include performance prediction for intelligent system orchestration. A pivotal development is the creation of models that can predict the expected reward or performance of an LLM on a given task before full execution. This capability is directly applicable to "model routing," where a lightweight predictor can decide which specialized model (or model configuration) in a portfolio is best suited for an incoming query. This paradigm enables the construction of more cost-effective and higher-performing LLM systems by dynamically allocating resources, marrying the goals of computational efficiency with quality optimization.

1. Revisiting Tree Search for LLMs: Gumbel and Sequential Halving for Budget-Scalable Reasoning

ArXiv ID: 2603.21162 |

👥 Authors: Leonid Ugadiarov, Yuri Kuratov, Aleksandr Panov, Alexey Skrynnik

🏫 Affiliations: Unknown Institution

💡 Highlight: This paper is relevant as it proposes a novel inference-time tree search algorithm (ReSCALE) to enhance LLM reasoning, aligning with advanced prompt engineering and agent-like planning strategies.
View Abstract

arXiv:2603.21162v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Neural tree search is a powerful decision-making algorithm widely used in complex domains such as game playing and model-based reinforcement learning. Recent work has applied AlphaZero-style tree search to enhance the reasoning capabilities of Large Language Models (LLMs) during inference, but we find that this approach suffers from a scaling failure: on GSM8K and Game24, accuracy drops as the search budget increases. In this paper, we present ReSCALE, an adaptation of Gumbel AlphaZero MCTS that replaces Dirichlet noise and PUCT selection with Gumbel sampling and Sequential Halving, restoring monotonic scaling without changes to the model or its training. ReSCALE reaches 58.4\% on GSM8K and 85.3\% on Game24 at budgets where the baseline degrades. Ablations confirm that Sequential Halving is the primary driver of the improvement.

2. Let's Think with Images Efficiently! An Interleaved-Modal Chain-of-Thought Reasoning Framework with Dynamic and Precise Visual Thoughts

ArXiv ID: 2603.21754 |

👥 Authors: Xu Liu, Yongheng Zhang, Qiguang Chen, Yao Li, Sheng Wang, Libo Qin

🏫 Affiliations: Unknown Institution

💡 Highlight: Proposes an advanced reasoning framework for multimodal models, aligning with interests in Multimodal Large Models (MLLMs) and advanced prompt/CoT engineering strategies.
View Abstract

arXiv:2603.21754v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Recently, Interleaved-modal Chain-of-Thought (ICoT) reasoning has achieved remarkable success by leveraging both multimodal inputs and outputs, attracting increasing attention. While achieving promising performance, current ICoT methods still suffer from two major limitations: (1) Static Visual Thought Positioning, which statically inserts visual information at fixed steps, resulting in inefficient and inflexible reasoning; and (2) Broken Visual Thought Representation, which involves discontinuous and semantically incoherent visual tokens. To address these limitations, we introduce Interleaved-modal Chain-of-Thought reasoning with Dynamic and Precise Visual Thoughts (DaP-ICoT), which incorporates two key components: (1) Dynamic Visual Thought Integration adaptively introduces visual inputs based on reasoning needs, reducing redundancy and improving efficiency. (2) Precise Visual Thought Guidance ensures visual semantically coherent and contextually aligned representations. Experiments across multiple benchmarks and models demonstrate that DaP-ICoT achieves state-of-the-art performance. In addition, DaP-ICoT significantly reduces the number of inserted images, leading to a 72.6% decrease in token consumption, enabling more efficient ICoT reasoning.

3. Grounded Chess Reasoning in Language Models via Master Distillation

ArXiv ID: 2603.20510 |

👥 Authors: Zhenwei Tang, Qianfeng Wen, Seth Grief-Albert, Yahya Elgabra, Blair Yang, Honghua Dong, Ashton Anderson

🏫 Affiliations: Unknown Institution

💡 Highlight: Directly combines Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) and Reinforcement Learning (RL) to distill expert reasoning into an LLM, addressing core interests in SFT data synthesis and RL for LLMs.
View Abstract

arXiv:2603.20510v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Language models often lack grounded reasoning capabilities in specialized domains where training data is scarce but bespoke systems excel. We introduce a general framework for distilling expert system reasoning into natural language chain-of-thought explanations, enabling compact models to acquire domain expertise and the ability to generate faithful, grounded explanations. Rather than distilling only final outputs, we capture the full reasoning process, transforming opaque expert computations into transparent, step-by-step explanations. We demonstrate this approach in chess, a canonical reasoning domain where language models continue to underperform. Our 4B parameter model, C1, advances from a near-zero baseline to 48.1% accuracy, outperforming all open-source models and most frontier proprietary systems. Notably, C1 surpasses its distillation teacher and generates solutions in two orders of magnitude fewer tokens than baselines. Unlike prior neural chess approaches that predict only best moves, C1 generates explainable solutions revealing strategic reasoning. Our pipeline combines supervised fine-tuning and reinforcement learning with theme-balanced data sampling for comprehensive tactical coverage. Master Distillation demonstrates how to inject expert-level knowledge into compact models for under-optimized domains, offering a recipe for unlocking RLVR where LLMs lack sufficient base capabilities.

4. Expected Reward Prediction, with Applications to Model Routing

ArXiv ID: 2603.20217 |

👥 Authors: Kenan Hasanaliyev, Silas Alberti, Jenny Hamer, Dheeraj Rajagopal, Kevin Robinson, Jasper Snoek, Victor Veitch, Alexander Nicholas D'Amour

🏫 Affiliations: Unknown Institution

💡 Highlight: Focuses on predicting expected reward from LLMs, a core component of RL for LLMs, with a direct application to efficient model routing (related to LLM efficiency).
View Abstract

arXiv:2603.20217v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Reward models are a standard tool to score responses from LLMs. Reward models are built to rank responses to a fixed prompt sampled from a single model, for example to choose the best of n sampled responses. In this paper, we study whether scores from response-level reward models lifted to score a model's suitability for a prompt, prior to seeing responses from that model. Specifically, we show that it is straightforward to predict the expected reward that an LLM would earn from the reward model under repeated sampling. Further, we show that these expected reward predictions are precise and discriminative enough to support an application to a model routing protocol that routes prompts to models at inference time to maximize reward while controlling computational cost. We demonstrate the performance of this routing procedure on the open-perfectblend dataset, using a model pool composed of Llama3.1-Instruct 8B/70B, Gemma2-IT 9B/27B, and Gemma1-IT 7B models. Our simple expected reward prediction--based routing (ERP) outperforms baselines that route prompts to models with the best average performance within each prompt's category, and explains the success of more complex routing protocols that implicitly estimate an expected reward. Our approach has the added advantage of being trivially extensible as new models are added to the pool.

Research Interest Criteria

Please evaluate the papers based on the following three core areas:

1. Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) for LLMs

Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) is a critical technique for adapting pre-trained Large Language Models (LLMs) to specific downstream tasks or domains. This process involves further training the model on a curated dataset of high-quality, labeled examples that reflect the target behavior. Our research focuses on optimizing the SFT process to enhance model performance, alignment, and efficiency.

  • Key Direction 1: Investigating novel data-sourcing and data-synthesis strategies to construct high-quality, diverse, and instruction-rich SFT datasets.
  • Key Direction 2: Developing advanced SFT algorithms and optimization techniques to improve the efficiency and effectiveness of fine-tuning on specialized tasks.
  • Potential Contribution: Establishing best practices for curating SFT data and designing fine-tuning protocols that lead to more capable and reliable LLMs.

2. Reinforcement Learning (RL) for LLMs

Reinforcement Learning, particularly from human or AI-generated feedback, offers a powerful paradigm for aligning LLM behavior with complex human preferences and objectives. This research area explores methods beyond simple instruction-following, aiming to imbue models with more nuanced capabilities. Our work centers on creating robust RL pipelines and novel reward mechanisms to steer LLM development.

  • Relevance: We explore cutting-edge RL techniques, including rejection sampling and unsupervised reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards, to build sophisticated data pipelines that continuously enhance LLM capabilities.
  • Key Direction: Designing novel reward functions that directly target improvements in key metrics such as helpfulness, honesty, harmlessness, and reductions in hallucination.
  • Potential Contribution: Pioneering new research directions in RL for LLMs that inspire further investigation and bridge the gap between theoretical advancements and practical, large-scale model improvements.

3. Agents for LLMs

Autonomous agents built upon LLMs are a key step towards creating general-purpose problem-solving systems. Our emphasis is on self-evolving agents: agents that can iteratively improve their policies, tools, and internal knowledge through experience. This direction studies the mechanisms that make agent behavior not only autonomous at inference time, but continually improving over long horizons via self-generated feedback and data. The goal is to design agent architectures that translate self-evolution into measurable capability gains and strong real-world deployability.

  • Relevance: Centering on self-evolving agents—agent systems that autonomously generate supervision signals, curate experience, and improve themselves over time rather than remaining static after deployment.
  • Key Direction: Building closed-loop improvement frameworks, e.g., self-reflection and self-critique, automated curriculum generation, and learning from interaction traces (including tool-use trajectories) to drive continual adaptation.
  • Potential Contribution: Delivering practical, deployable self-evolving agents that demonstrate sustained improvements in reasoning, planning, and tool use, with clear evaluation protocols that quantify long-horizon learning progress and reliability.
