💡 Daily Research Summary

### RL for LLMs & Alignment Today's research demonstrates a concerted push to address the fundamental instabilities and inefficiencies of applying reinforcement learning to large language models. A key theme is the development of novel RL frameworks that move beyond standard policy gradients to ensure stable, monotonic improvement, as seen in methods like Bounded Ratio RL and Mastery-Consolidated Policy Optimization (MCPO). Concurrently, significant effort is directed at solving the exploration problem in sparse-reward reasoning tasks, with innovations like Poly-EPO for optimistic exploration and HEAL for mitigating entropy collapse. The community is also critically re-examining core RLHF components, proposing new frameworks for step-level reward modeling, addressing length bias in sequence-level comparisons, and even providing new theoretical grounding for the effectiveness of online alignment methods. This collective work signifies a maturation of the field, moving from initial proof-of-concepts to robust, theoretically-sound training paradigms.

Self-Improving & Evolving Agents

The frontier of agent research is dominated by architectures designed for continuous, autonomous improvement without heavy human oversight. A clear trend is the formalization of self-evolution loops, where agents systematically externalize lessons from failures into reusable components—be they skill banks, optimized prompts, executable code, or refined hypotheses—as demonstrated by frameworks like EvoMaster, SkillFlow, and GenericAgent. This evolution is often driven by multi-agent ecosystems or self-play paradigms that generate rich, synthetic interaction data, such as in Agent-World's training arena or the collusive strategy evolution studied in prompt optimization. The underlying mechanisms are increasingly sophisticated, combining reinforcement learning with retrieval, code generation, and dynamic graph-based collaboration to enable agents that not only solve tasks but perpetually enhance their own capabilities and internal knowledge structures.

Latent & Efficient Reasoning

A major thrust aims to compress and accelerate the multi-step reasoning processes of LLMs by operating in latent or compressed spaces. The goal is to achieve the benefits of chain-of-thought with the efficiency of single-step generation. Techniques like LEPO apply RL directly to continuous latent representations, while OneVL and Semantic Step Prediction forecast multi-step reasoning trajectories into compact latent plans. Efficiency is pursued hand-in-hand with this latent reasoning, through dynamic KV-cache management (Neural Garbage Collection), low-rank activation compression (LASER), and internalizing early-exit mechanisms (Step-GRPO). This line of work seeks a fundamental shift from expensive, explicit token-by-token reasoning to efficient, implicit computational processes that maintain or improve performance.

Multimodal & Embodied Agents

Research in multimodal and embodied domains is increasingly focused on verifiable, grounded learning and the challenge of continual skill acquisition. For Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs), frameworks like EVE emphasize verifiable self-evolution through executable visual transformations, ensuring improvements are grounded in reality. In embodied AI, the focus is on creating automated, lifelong learning pipelines, as seen in DexWorldModel's online framework for learning from a stream of physics-grounded trajectories. The integration of reinforcement learning with verifiable, often simulation-based, reward signals is a common thread, driving systems that can interact with and improve from complex, non-textual environments.

Federated & Collaborative Learning

There is growing interest in decentralized and collaborative paradigms for training and improving AI systems. Federation over Text proposes a shared library where agents distill and share reasoning traces, enabling collective intelligence. On the technical front, new algorithms like federated RLHF via zeroth-order optimization aim to make these collaborative training processes scalable and privacy-preserving. This direction points towards a future where improvement is not siloed within single models or institutions but emerges from secure, distributed collaboration and knowledge exchange among multiple agents or entities.

2. Neural Garbage Collection: Learning to Forget while Learning to Reason

ArXiv ID: 2604.18002 | Relevance Score: 9/10 | Novelty Score: 8/10

👥 Authors: Michael Y. Li, Jubayer Ibn Hamid, Emily B. Fox, Noah D. Goodman

🏫 Affiliations: Unknown Institution

💡 Highlight: Introduces Neural Garbage Collection, an RL method for LLMs to learn to manage KV cache via eviction decisions, jointly optimizing reasoning and memory from outcome-based reward.
View Abstract

arXiv:2604.18002v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Chain-of-thought reasoning has driven striking advances in language model capability, yet every reasoning step grows the KV cache, creating a bottleneck to scaling this paradigm further. Current approaches manage these constraints on the model's behalf using hand-designed criteria. A more scalable approach would let end-to-end learning subsume this design choice entirely, following a broader pattern in deep learning. After all, if a model can learn to reason, why can't it learn to forget? We introduce Neural Garbage Collection (NGC), in which a language model learns to forget while learning to reason, trained end-to-end from outcome-based task reward alone. As the model reasons, it periodically pauses, decides which KV cache entries to evict, and continues to reason conditioned on the remaining cache. By treating tokens in a chain-of-thought and cache-eviction decisions as discrete actions sampled from the language model, we can use reinforcement learning to jointly optimize how the model reasons and how it manages its own memory: what the model evicts shapes what it remembers, what it remembers shapes its reasoning, and the correctness of that reasoning determines its reward. Crucially, the model learns this behavior entirely from a single learning signal - the outcome-based task reward - without supervised fine-tuning or proxy objectives. On Countdown, AMC, and AIME tasks, NGC maintains strong accuracy relative to the full-cache upper bound at 2-3x peak KV cache size compression and substantially outperforms eviction baselines. Our results are a first step towards a broader vision where end-to-end optimization drives both capability and efficiency in language models.

3. Latent Phase-Shift Rollback: Inference-Time Error Correction via Residual Stream Monitoring and KV-Cache Steering

ArXiv ID: 2604.18567 | Relevance Score: 8/10 | Novelty Score: 8/10

👥 Authors: Manan Gupta, Dhruv Kumar

🏫 Affiliations: Unknown Institution

💡 Highlight: Introduces a novel inference-time error correction method via latent residual stream monitoring and KV-cache steering, directly targeting latent reasoning and efficiency.
View Abstract

arXiv:2604.18567v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Large language models frequently commit unrecoverable reasoning errors mid-generation: once a wrong step is taken, subsequent tokens compound the mistake rather than correct it. We introduce $\textbf{Latent Phase-Shift Rollback}$ (LPSR): at each generation step, we monitor the residual stream at a critical layer lcrit, detect abrupt directional reversals (phase shifts) via a cosine-similarity $+$ entropy dual gate, and respond by rolling back the KV-cache and injecting a pre-computed steering vector. No fine-tuning, gradient computation, or additional forward passes are required. LPSR achieves $\mathbf{44.0\%}$ on MATH-500 with an 8B model versus $28.8\%$ for standard AR ($+15.2$ pp; McNemar $\chi^2 = 66.96$, $p < 10^{-15}$). Critically, prompted self-correction, the most natural inference-time baseline, scores only $19.8\%$, below standard AR; LPSR exceeds it by $+24.2$ pp ($\chi^2 = 89.4$, $p \approx 0$). LPSR also outperforms Best-of-16 ($+7.8$ pp) at $5.4\times$ lower token cost, and surpasses a standard 70B model ($35.2\%$) with $8.75\times$ fewer parameters at ${\sim}3\times$ the token budget. A 32-layer sweep reveals a novel \textbf{detection-correction dissociation}: error-detection AUC peaks at layer~14 ($0.718$) but task accuracy peaks at layer~16 ($44.0\%$ vs.\ $29.2\%$), demonstrating that optimal monitoring depth differs for detection and correction.

arXiv:2604.18320v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Self-evolution of multimodal large language models (MLLMs) remains a critical challenge: pseudo-label-based methods suffer from progressive quality degradation as model predictions drift, while template-based methods are confined to a static set of transformations that cannot adapt in difficulty or diversity. We contend that robust, continuous self-improvement requires not only deterministic external feedback independent of the model's internal certainty, but also a mechanism to perpetually diversify the training distribution. To this end, we introduce EVE (Executable Visual transformation-based self-Evolution), a novel framework that entirely bypasses pseudo-labels by harnessing executable visual transformations continuously enriched in both variety and complexity. EVE adopts a Challenger-Solver dual-policy architecture. The Challenger maintains and progressively expands a queue of visual transformation code examples, from which it synthesizes novel Python scripts to perform dynamic visual transformations. Executing these scripts yields VQA problems with absolute, execution-verified ground-truth answers, eliminating any reliance on model-generated supervision. A multi-dimensional reward system integrating semantic diversity and dynamic difficulty calibration steers the Challenger to enrich its code example queue while posing progressively more challenging tasks, preventing mode collapse and fostering reciprocal co-evolution between the two policies. Extensive experiments demonstrate that EVE consistently surpasses existing self-evolution methods, establishing a robust and scalable paradigm for verifiable MLLM self-evolution. arXiv:2604.16778v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: LLM-powered agents often reason from scratch when presented with a new problem instance and lack automatic mechanisms to transfer learned skills to other agents. We propose a federated learning-like framework, Federation over Text (FoT), that enables multiple agents solving different tasks to collectively generate a shared library of metacognitive insights by iteratively federating their local reasoning processes. Instead of federation over gradients (e.g., as in distributed training), FoT operates at the semantic level without any gradient optimization or supervision signal. Experiments show that FoT improves reasoning effectiveness and efficiency across a wide range of challenging applications, including mathematical problem solving, cross-domain collaboration, and machine learning research insight discovery. Specifically, it improves average accuracies of downstream tasks by 24% while reducing the reasoning tokens by 28% across the first two applications. In the research insight discovery application, FoT is able to generate insights that cover over 90% of the major contributions in the subsequent papers.

  • Especially valuable: Papers that propose a new reward function, a new scalable RL training recipe, or a new data-generation / self-improvement loop for post-training LLMs.
  • Usually not relevant: Generic reinforcement learning papers, RL for robotics/control/games, prompt optimization without RL, supervised finetuning only, inference-time search without a training contribution, or alignment papers with no substantive RL component.

2. Agents for LLMs

We care specifically about self-evolving agents rather than static agent systems. The central question is whether the agent can improve itself over time through interaction, reflection, tool-use traces, self-generated supervision, or automated curriculum/data generation.

  • Strong match: Self-improving agent frameworks, reflection/self-critique loops that feed back into learning, trajectory-driven continual improvement, automatic tool-use policy improvement, environment interaction that produces new supervision, and long-horizon agent adaptation.
  • Especially valuable: Closed-loop systems showing measurable capability gain over time, reliable agent learning from interaction traces, or agent architectures that turn execution experience into better future performance.
  • Usually not relevant: Vanilla ReAct-style agents, workflow orchestration, multi-agent communication only, tool calling systems without learning, benchmark papers, memory systems without self-improvement, or application-focused agent papers.

3. Latent CoT/Reasoning

We care about methods that move reasoning away from verbose explicit text chains into compact latent computation while preserving or improving reasoning quality.

  • Strong match: Latent reasoning architectures, hidden-state reasoning, implicit CoT, recurrent depth / recurrent latent computation, pause or thinking tokens for internal computation, explicit-to-latent distillation, and methods that reduce reasoning token cost or KV-cache use while retaining accuracy.
  • Especially valuable: New architectures or objectives for latent reasoning, hidden-state alignment/distillation, compression of reasoning traces, or methods that materially improve reasoning efficiency on math and logic tasks.
  • Usually not relevant: Standard explicit CoT prompting, generic reasoning finetuning, test-time scaling without latent computation, reasoning benchmarks, or papers whose main contribution is evaluation rather than a latent reasoning mechanism.
