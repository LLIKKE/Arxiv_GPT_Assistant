1. Revisiting Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards from a Contrastive Perspective

ArXiv ID: 2605.12969 | Relevance Score: 8/10 | Novelty Score: 8/10

👥 Authors: Feng Zhang, Xinhong Ma, Ziqiang Dong, Xi Leng, Jianfei Zhao, Xin Sun, Yang Yang, Guanjun Jiang

🏫 Affiliations: Unknown Institution

💡 Highlight: Introduces ConSPO, a novel contrastive sequence-level policy optimization framework for RLVR that addresses structural limitations of GRPO.
View Abstract

arXiv:2605.12969v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: RLVR has become a widely adopted paradigm for improving LLMs' reasoning capabilities, and GRPO is one of its most representative algorithms. In this paper, we first show that GRPO admits an equivalent discriminative reformulation as a weighted positive-negative score difference. Under this view, GRPO increases sequence-level scores of verified positive rollouts and decreases those of negative rollouts, where the scores are averages of clipped token-level importance sampling ratios. This reformulation reveals two structural limitations of GRPO: likelihood-misaligned scoring, where clipped ratio-based surrogate scores are optimized instead of generation likelihoods, and score-insensitive credit assignment, where rollout-level credit is assigned without accounting for relative score gaps between positive and negative rollouts in the same group. To address these limitations, we propose ConSPO, a framework for Contrastive Sequence-level Policy Optimization in RLVR. ConSPO replaces GRPO's clipped ratio-based scores with length-normalized sequence log-probabilities, aligning the optimized rollout scores with the likelihoods used in autoregressive generation. It then optimizes a group-wise InfoNCE-style objective that contrasts each positive rollout against negative distractors from the same group, enabling credit assignment to depend on their relative scores. This contrastive formulation amplifies updates for poorly separated positives while concentrating suppressive updates on high-scoring negatives. Moreover, ConSPO introduces a curriculum-scheduled margin, guiding optimization from coarse positive-negative ordering in early training toward stronger separation in later stages. Extensive evaluations across diverse backbone models, parameter scales, and training datasets show that ConSPO consistently outperforms several strong RLVR baselines on challenging mathematical reasoning benchmarks.

arXiv:2605.12913v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Long-horizon LM agents learn from multi-turn interaction, where a single early mistake can alter the subsequent state distribution and derail the whole trajectory. The resulting 4B agent reaches 27.3%, outperforming representative published 8B SWE-agent systems, while the 8B agent achieves 29.8%, surpassing SWE-Gym-32B and coming within 5 points of stronger 32B-scale agents. Together with consistent gains on the held-out SWE-Gym split, these results suggest the effectiveness of DAgger for modern long-horizon LM agents.

Overall, our results show that combining outcome-based policy optimization with knowledge distillation in a single objective provides a more stable and effective path to long-context reasoning, while preserving short-context capabilities.

8. Useful Memories Become Faulty When Continuously Updated by LLMs

ArXiv ID: 2605.12978 | Relevance Score: 7/10 | Novelty Score: 8/10

👥 Authors: Dylan Zhang, Yanshan Lin, Zhengkun Wu, Yihang Sun, Bingxuan Li, Dianqi Li, Hao Peng

🏫 Affiliations: Unknown Institution

💡 Highlight: Directly studies self-improving agent memory systems, revealing that LLM-based consolidated memory degrades over time, and proposes episodic-only memory as a robust alternative.
View Abstract

arXiv:2605.12978v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Learning from past experience benefits from two complementary forms of memory: episodic traces -- raw trajectories of what happened -- and consolidated abstractions distilled across many episodes into reusable, schema-like lessons. Recent agentic-memory systems pursue the consolidated form: an LLM rewrites past trajectories into a textual memory bank that it continuously updates with new interactions, promising self-improving agents without parameter updates. Yet we find that such consolidated memories produced by today's LLMs are often faulty even when derived from useful experiences. As consolidation proceeds, memory utility first rises, then degrades, and can fall below the no-memory baseline. More surprisingly, even when consolidating from ground-truth solutions, GPT-5.4 fails on 54% of a set of ARC-AGI problems it had previously solved without memory. We trace the regression to the consolidation step rather than the underlying experience: the same trajectories yield qualitatively different memories under different update schedules, and an episodic-only control that simply retains those trajectories remains competitive with the consolidators we test. In a controlled ARC-AGI Stream environment that exposes Retain, Delete, and Consolidate actions, agents preserve raw episodes by default and double the accuracy of their forced-consolidation counterparts; disabling consolidation entirely (episodic management only) matches this auto regime. Research Interest Criteria

Please evaluate papers using a strict filter. We only want a small set of strongly aligned papers, not a broad survey of adjacent work.

Global Selection Principle

  • Mark a paper as relevant only if one of the three topics below is a central contribution of the paper, not a side application, benchmark, or discussion point.
  • Be conservative by default: if the connection is indirect, partial, or ambiguous, treat it as not relevant or score it low.
  • Prefer papers that introduce new methods, training frameworks, reward designs, agent improvement loops, or latent reasoning architectures.
  • Do not favor papers just because they mention "LLM", "agent", "reasoning", "RL", "alignment", or "CoT" in a generic way.
  • Exclude broad foundation model papers, generic post-training papers, generic tool-use agents, application/system papers, domain-specific deployments, and pure benchmark papers unless their core method strongly matches one of the topics below.

1. Reinforcement Learning (RL) for LLMs

We care about RL methods that directly improve LLM behavior, alignment, or reasoning through explicit reward design, RL pipelines, verifier-driven optimization, or scalable preference/feedback mechanisms.

  • Strong match: RLHF/RLAIF variants, verifiable reward based RL, rejection sampling plus RL pipelines, reward model design, outcome/process reward methods, online RL for LLMs, and methods that materially improve helpfulness, honesty, harmlessness, or hallucination behavior.
  • Especially valuable: Papers that propose a new reward function, a new scalable RL training recipe, or a new data-generation / self-improvement loop for post-training LLMs.
  • Usually not relevant: Generic reinforcement learning papers, RL for robotics/control/games, prompt optimization without RL, supervised finetuning only, inference-time search without a training contribution, or alignment papers with no substantive RL component.

2. Agents for LLMs

We care specifically about self-evolving agents rather than static agent systems. The central question is whether the agent can improve itself over time through interaction, reflection, tool-use traces, self-generated supervision, or automated curriculum/data generation.

  • Strong match: Self-improving agent frameworks, reflection/self-critique loops that feed back into learning, trajectory-driven continual improvement, automatic tool-use policy improvement, environment interaction that produces new supervision, and long-horizon agent adaptation.
  • Especially valuable: Closed-loop systems showing measurable capability gain over time, reliable agent learning from interaction traces, or agent architectures that turn execution experience into better future performance.
  • Usually not relevant: Vanilla ReAct-style agents, workflow orchestration, multi-agent communication only, tool calling systems without learning, benchmark papers, memory systems without self-improvement, or application-focused agent papers.

3. Latent CoT/Reasoning

We care about methods that move reasoning away from verbose explicit text chains into compact latent computation while preserving or improving reasoning quality.

  • Strong match: Latent reasoning architectures, hidden-state reasoning, implicit CoT, recurrent depth / recurrent latent computation, pause or thinking tokens for internal computation, explicit-to-latent distillation, and methods that reduce reasoning token cost or KV-cache use while retaining accuracy.
  • Especially valuable: New architectures or objectives for latent reasoning, hidden-state alignment/distillation, compression of reasoning traces, or methods that materially improve reasoning efficiency on math and logic tasks.
  • Usually not relevant: Standard explicit CoT prompting, generic reasoning finetuning, test-time scaling without latent computation, reasoning benchmarks, or papers whose main contribution is evaluation rather than a latent reasoning mechanism.

Final Preference

If a paper is merely adjacent to these areas, do not select it. Favor precision over recall. It is better to miss a borderline paper than to include many weakly related ones.
