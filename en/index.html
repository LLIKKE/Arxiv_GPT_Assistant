💡 Daily Research Summary

### RL for LLMs The sheer volume of papers today underscores that reinforcement learning (RL) for LLM post-training has fully entered its "engineering maturity" phase, where the focus has shifted from proving the concept to optimizing every component of the pipeline. A dominant theme is the move beyond simplistic, trajectory-level rewards toward more granular and principled credit assignment. **GraphGPO**and**StepOPSD**directly tackle step-level attribution in agentic contexts, while**BASIS**and**RICE-PO**introduce critic-free methods that leverage batchwise or retrieval-based information to localize credit. Concurrently, the design of the reward signal itself is being refined:**Focal Reward**automatically balances multi-dimensional rubric-based rewards,**Tournament-GRPO**handles open-ended generation with group-wise comparisons, and**GeoFaith**jointly optimizes for outcome correctness and process faithfulness. Efficiency is also a key concern, with**Spend Your Rollouts Where It Counts**and**GAC**proposing adaptive allocation and noise-aware mixing for hybrid SFT-RL training. This collective work paints a picture of a field systematically dismantling the bottlenecks of RL for LLMs—credit assignment, reward sparsity, and training instability—to build more reliable and capable reasoning systems.

Self-Evolving Agents & Agentic RL

The concept of autonomous self-improvement is no longer a vision but a rapidly maturing research direction, with multiple papers presenting concrete frameworks for creating agents that learn and adapt over time.MiniMax-M2andSIApropose end-to-end systems where agents iteratively refine their weights and behavior through feedback loops, whileMUSE-Autoskilloffers a more modular approach by formalizing the entire skill lifecycle—creation, memory, management, and evaluation—as a unified process. This is complemented by work on the data and training infrastructure for such systems:AKBEdynamically probes an agent's knowledge boundary to reduce wasteful tool calls, andVerilog-Evolvedemonstrates how domain-specific agents can evolve their skills across sessions by maintaining histories. A critical insight fromNoisyAgentandMAIGOis that robustness and history management are essential for real-world deployment, showing that self-evolution must account for environmental noise and conversational contamination. The field is clearly converging on the idea that the next leap in agent capability will come not from a single architectural innovation, but from the integration of closed-loop learning, sophisticated memory, and adaptive data pipelines.

Latent Reasoning & Recurrent Architectures

A significant breakthrough is emerging in the design of models that can reason in a latent, token-free space, challenging the dominance of explicit chain-of-thought. TheSTARSandLatent Recurrent Transformerpapers both propose novel architectures that reuse hidden states across tokens or loops, enabling implicit reasoning without generating intermediate text. This is a paradigm shift with profound implications for efficiency, asPair-In, Pair-Outdemonstrates by unifying input compression and output speculation through latent multi-token prediction. The application of this idea extends beyond language:Latent Equation Embedding (LEE)applies latent iterative refinement to symbolic regression, proving the concept's generality. However, this new capability also introduces new risks, asConceptual Steganographywarns that latent CoTs can hide malicious content that is robust to paraphrase-based defenses. The field is thus at a pivotal moment: latent reasoning promises massive gains in speed and scalability, but it demands new safety and interpretability mechanisms.

Multimodal & Domain-Specific RL

Reinforcement learning is being aggressively applied to unlock new capabilities in multimodal systems, moving beyond standard language tasks.Touch-R1andMedVol-R1pioneer the use of GRPO with tactile-grounded and volumetric rewards, respectively, demonstrating that RL can effectively integrate non-linguistic signals into reasoning. For video understanding,DynFrameintroduces a sophisticated RL training recipe with role-specific token-level advantages, whileInterSketchuses interleaved visual-textual reasoning with self-correcting sketches. In the audio domain,Learning When to Think While Listening applies RL to optimize the timing of reasoning in large audio-language models. These works collectively show that the RL-for-LLM toolkit is highly transferable: the core challenges of reward design, credit assignment, and training stability are universal, but the specific implementations must be tailored to the unique structure of each modality. This cross-modal validation strengthens the argument that RL is a fundamental algorithmic primitive for building generally capable AI systems.

💡 Highlight: Proposes SAERL, a novel framework using Sparse Autoencoder model internals to guide data engineering (diversity, difficulty, quality) for LLM RL post-training, showing clear gains.
View Abstract

arXiv:2605.27354v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Model internals encode rich information about how a large language model (LLM) processes its training data; however, post-training data engineering largely relies on external signals and ignores rich intrinsic signals lying in model internals. We propose SAERL, a data engineering framework for LLM reinforcement learning (RL). It models three intrinsic data properties: diversity, difficulty, and quality, using model internals extracted with Sparse Autoencoder (SAE), an advanced mechanistic interpretability tool. Each property grounds a concrete data engineering operation: SAE-space clustering with moderate batch mixing for batch diversity control, a difficulty proxy for easy-to-hard curriculum ordering, and a quality probe for data filtering. SAERL improves average accuracy by 3.00% over vanilla GRPO and reaches target accuracy with 20% fewer training steps on Qwen2.5-Math-1.5B, with consistent gains across model scales and RL algorithms. Experiments show that SAE transfers effectively across model families and scales, serving as a lightweight and reusable data engineering tool. These results demonstrate that model internals are a powerful and practical source of signals for post-training data engineering.

🏫 Affiliations: Unknown Institution

💡 Highlight: Proposes STARS, a training framework for stable latent reasoning in looped language models, directly addressing latent CoT/reasoning with a novel stability mechanism.
View Abstract

arXiv:2605.26733v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Looped Language Models (LoopLMs) enable efficient latent reasoning through depth recurrence, yet exhibit unreliable test-time scaling behavior: performance often peaks at a certain iteration depth and then collapses with further recurrence. Through latent dynamics analysis, we find an inherent trade-off between stability and effectiveness in existing architectures and strategies. By conceptualizing reasoning as uncertainty reduction, we propose that convergence toward stable fixed points while preserving effectiveness represents a promising way. To this end, we propose STARS (STAbility-driven Recurrent Scaling), a training framework that constrains latent states to approach asymptotically stable fixed points. This is realized via efficient Jacobian Spectral Radius Regularization with random loop sampling, enabling STARS to maximize effectiveness while ensuring rigorous stability. Experiments on arithmetic tasks show that STARS achieves reliable test-time scaling, and on complex mathematical reasoning it substantially mitigates performance degradation as recurrence depth increases while also improving peak performance.

3. Latent Recurrent Transformer: Architecture Exploration, Training Strategies, and Scaling Behavior

👥 Authors: Zeyi Huang, Xuehai He, LiLiang Ren, Yiping Wang, Baolin Peng, Hao Cheng, Shuohang Wang, Pengcheng He, Jianfeng Gao, Yong Jae Lee, Yelong Shen

🏫 Affiliations: Unknown Institution

💡 Highlight: Proposes a latent recurrent transformer that reuses hidden states across tokens for implicit reasoning, directly matching the latent CoT/reasoning criterion.
View Abstract

arXiv:2605.26797v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: We study Latent Recurrent Transformer (LRT), a lightweight augmentation of autoregressive transformers that reuses a high-level source-layer hidden state from the previous token as recurrent memory for the next token. Because this source state is already computed during ordinary decoding, LRT adds a cross-layer recurrent latent pathway across positions without inserting pause tokens or extra depth loops, and the standard attention mechanism and KV-cache interface are preserved. To pretrain this recurrence at scale without sequentially unrolling the transformer, we introduce interleaved parallel training: a single full-sequence initialization forward pass builds a shared buffer; then disjoint position subsets are refined in parallel and written back, so that all tokens receive recurrent-memory-aware supervision at roughly 2 times baseline compute. Across nanochat style backbones and a wide range of tokens-per-parameter budgets, LRT improves both language-modeling loss and in-context learning under matched effective compute while adding as little as 0.3% parameters.

4. Beyond Trajectory-Level Attribution: Graph-Based Credit Assignment for Agentic Reinforcement Learning

ArXiv ID: 2605.26684 | Relevance Score: 8/10 | Novelty Score: 8/10

👥 Authors: Xin Cheng, Shuo He, Lang Feng, HaiYang Xu, Ming Yan, Lei Feng, Bo An

🏫 Affiliations: Unknown Institution

💡 Highlight: Proposes GraphGPO, a novel graph-based credit assignment method for agentic RL that improves step-level attribution over trajectory-level methods, directly addressing RL for LLMs and agent improvement.
View Abstract

arXiv:2605.26684v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Group-based reinforcement learning (RL) methods have achieved remarkable success in improving the performance of large language models (LLMs) and have been rapidly extended to agentic tasks. However, their credit assignment relies heavily on coarse-grained trajectory-level attribution according to final outcomes, making it difficult to capture the contribution of individual steps, such as valuable steps obscured within failed trajectories. To uncover latent information and enable more faithful step-level credit assignment, we propose Graph-based Group Policy Optimization (GraphGPO), which first aggregates all rollout trajectories into a unified state-transition graph and then estimates the distance from each state to the task goal using the global information encoded in the graph. Finally, GraphGPO assigns credit to each edge by estimating a graph-based advantage, based on how much the transition reduces the distance to the task goal. In this way, GraphGPO significantly improves training efficiency and achieves state-of-the-art performance across a range of challenging benchmarks.

Global Selection Principle

  • Mark a paper as relevant only if one of the three topics below is a central contribution of the paper, not a side application, benchmark, or discussion point.
  • Be conservative by default: if the connection is indirect, partial, or ambiguous, treat it as not relevant or score it low.
  • Prefer papers that introduce new methods, training frameworks, reward designs, agent improvement loops, or latent reasoning architectures.
  • Do not favor papers just because they mention "LLM", "agent", "reasoning", "RL", "alignment", or "CoT" in a generic way.
  • Exclude broad foundation model papers, generic post-training papers, generic tool-use agents, application/system papers, domain-specific deployments, and pure benchmark papers unless their core method strongly matches one of the topics below.

1. Reinforcement Learning (RL) for LLMs

We care about RL methods that directly improve LLM behavior, alignment, or reasoning through explicit reward design, RL pipelines, verifier-driven optimization, or scalable preference/feedback mechanisms.

  • Strong match: RLHF/RLAIF variants, verifiable reward based RL, rejection sampling plus RL pipelines, reward model design, outcome/process reward methods, online RL for LLMs, and methods that materially improve helpfulness, honesty, harmlessness, or hallucination behavior.
  • Especially valuable: Papers that propose a new reward function, a new scalable RL training recipe, or a new data-generation / self-improvement loop for post-training LLMs.
  • Usually not relevant: Generic reinforcement learning papers, RL for robotics/control/games, prompt optimization without RL, supervised finetuning only, inference-time search without a training contribution, or alignment papers with no substantive RL component.

2. Agents for LLMs

We care specifically about self-evolving agents rather than static agent systems. The central question is whether the agent can improve itself over time through interaction, reflection, tool-use traces, self-generated supervision, or automated curriculum/data generation.

  • Strong match: Self-improving agent frameworks, reflection/self-critique loops that feed back into learning, trajectory-driven continual improvement, automatic tool-use policy improvement, environment interaction that produces new supervision, and long-horizon agent adaptation.
  • Especially valuable: Closed-loop systems showing measurable capability gain over time, reliable agent learning from interaction traces, or agent architectures that turn execution experience into better future performance.
  • Usually not relevant: Vanilla ReAct-style agents, workflow orchestration, multi-agent communication only, tool calling systems without learning, benchmark papers, memory systems without self-improvement, or application-focused agent papers.

3. Latent CoT/Reasoning

We care about methods that move reasoning away from verbose explicit text chains into compact latent computation while preserving or improving reasoning quality.

  • Strong match: Latent reasoning architectures, hidden-state reasoning, implicit CoT, recurrent depth / recurrent latent computation, pause or thinking tokens for internal computation, explicit-to-latent distillation, and methods that reduce reasoning token cost or KV-cache use while retaining accuracy.
  • Especially valuable: New architectures or objectives for latent reasoning, hidden-state alignment/distillation, compression of reasoning traces, or methods that materially improve reasoning efficiency on math and logic tasks.
  • Usually not relevant: Standard explicit CoT prompting, generic reasoning finetuning, test-time scaling without latent computation, reasoning benchmarks, or papers whose main contribution is evaluation rather than a latent reasoning mechanism.

Final Preference

If a paper is merely adjacent to these areas, do not select it. Favor precision over recall. It is better to miss a borderline paper than to include many weakly related ones.
