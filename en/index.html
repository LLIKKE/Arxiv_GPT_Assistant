💡 Daily Research Summary

### RL for LLMs A major wave of innovation is targeting the fundamental limitations of Reinforcement Learning (RL) for Large Language Models, particularly in reasoning. Several works address the credit assignment problem in multi-step reasoning. Frameworks like **FlowTracer**and**3SPO**move beyond scalar rewards by tracing attention-induced information flow or using state scores to provide step-wise, token-level credit. A second critical trend is the integration of RL with latent reasoning, with**Dropout-GRPO**introducing structured stochasticity to enable Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) on continuous latent chains-of-thought. Furthermore, the field is actively addressing training stability and data efficiency:**CPPO**proposes a token-level trust region to mitigate cumulative prefix drift, while**TD-Grokking**and**TRACE**tackle sparse rewards and rollout budget inefficiency by decomposing problems and tree-structured allocation, respectively. Finally, critical vulnerabilities are being exposed, such as the ability of one-shot GRPO training to override alignment (**It Takes One to Bias Them All**) and the systematic alignment drift observed in reasoning models post-RL (**Does Reasoning Preserve Alignment?**).

On-Policy Distillation and Self-Improvement

A dominant theme is the refinement of on-policy distillation, moving beyond simple imitation to more sophisticated, structurally aligned feedback mechanisms.Anchored Residual On-Policy Distillation (AR-OPD)andSocraticPOboth leverage privileged teacher guidance during the student's own rollouts to mitigate hindsight bias, whileThe Role of Feedback Alignment in Self-Distillationexplicitly demonstrates that step-aligned critique feedback is a key driver of effectiveness, outperforming binary rewards. This principle is applied across modalities, from bridging paralinguistic perception in speech models (ParaBridge) to using visual feedback for code generation (Visual-SDPO). The approach is also being extended to train specialized agents, as seen in pipelines that distill from incentive-aligned arena trajectories (Bittensor Agent Arenas) and in the**Cross-Modal Multi-Teacher On-Policy Distillation (MOPD)**for multimodal LLMs. A novel framework,Role-Agent, bootstraps this self-improvement by having a single LLM act as both agent and environment, using predicted state alignment as a process reward.

Agentic RL and Exploration

Advances in agentic RL are centered on improving exploration and credit assignment in complex, multi-turn environments.DiRLintroduces a direction-aware exploration strategy that uses model representations to distinguish reasoning from memorization, whileN-GRPOfosters semantic diversity via embedding-level neighbor mixing. For practical agent deployment,GUI-ACenhances continual learning in GUI agents by using grounding certainty for adaptive advantage estimation. The challenge of sparse rewards in agentic search is addressed byquery recyclingfor zero-variance queries, which improves training efficiency. A unified framework,TRACE, specifically targets the rollout budget allocation problem to enhance reward contrast in multi-turn agentic RL.

Alignment and Safety

Research on alignment is expanding from post-training to data selection and defense. A novelcoupled task-reference selectionframework uses a minimax view to ensure safe fine-tuning, directly improving alignment via data curation. On the defense front, alignment-based methods like DPO and GRPO are shown to be effective against property inference attacks. A critical audit reveals that the RL and distillation processes used to create reasoning models can systematically degrade trustworthiness, causing alignment drift (Does Reasoning Preserve Alignment?). This underscores the need for robust safety evaluations throughout the RL pipeline.

Multimodal and Specialized Architectures

For multimodal models, RL is being applied to optimize task-level objectives, as seen in theARMautoregressive model which uses RL for cross-task synergy in generation and editing. A specialized application,SD-GRPO, tackles long-form vision-language generation by assigning per-segment advantages instead of a single scalar reward. In the speech domain,Multi-Faceted Interactivity Alignmentuses axis-specific reward functions to optimize full-duplex speech model behavior. Finally, a new distillation framework,PADD, specifically addresses the challenge of training Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) students from dense teachers using path-refined policy optimization.

🏫 Affiliations: Unknown Institution

💡 Highlight: Proposes FlowTracer, a novel RL framework that uses attention-induced information flow on a DAG to assign token-level credit for reasoning tasks, directly improving RL for LLMs.
View Abstract

arXiv:2606.10646v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Token-level credit assignment remains a key obstacle for reinforcement learning (RL) in large language models (LLMs), where RL recipes typically treat all tokens equally, failing to distinguish decisive reasoning steps from routine formatting or fluent filler. Recent attempts leverage model-internal signals to assign finer-grained credit, but these are often point-wise heuristics that ignore the global structure of information propagation. We propose FlowTracer, an RL framework that traces answer-targeted reasoning flow on an attention-induced directed acyclic graph in which nodes correspond to tokens and edge capacities come from aggregated attention weights and derives token credit from this global structure. The edge capacities are reweighted to retain only the influence that can reach the answer region, while enforcing local flow conservation so intermediate tokens neither lose nor gain effective mass due to path length or irrelevant branches. On this graph, FlowTracer extracts an information-flow backbone connecting the question to the answer and scores tokens by flow throughput, revealing high-impact hubs and aggregation checkpoints that mediate long-range dependencies. These derived importances are used to shape token-level rewards, enabling learning signals to focus precisely on the tokens that route information toward (or away from) correct answers and delivering consistent performance gains across a range of reasoning tasks.

2. Dropout-GRPO: Variational Stochasticity for Continuous Latent Reasoning

ArXiv ID: 2606.10184 | Relevance Score: 9/10 | Novelty Score: 8/10

👥 Authors: Wooil Jung

🏫 Affiliations: Unknown Institution

💡 Highlight: Proposes Dropout-GRPO, a novel method to introduce stochasticity into latent reasoning models via structured dropout, enabling GRPO to work on continuous latent reasoning and directly addressing a key limitation of RL for latent CoT.
View Abstract

arXiv:2606.10184v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) relies on the diversity of $K$ rollouts within each group; otherwise, the group-mean advantage $A^{(k)} = r^{(k)} - \mu_r$ collapses to zero. This presents a structural challenge for latent-reasoning models like Coconut, which feed continuous hidden states recurrently in place of discrete chain-of-thought tokens. Because the latent phase is inherently deterministic given the parameters and prompt, multiple rollouts produce identical trajectories, stalling GRPO's progress. Consequently, applying group-relative reinforcement learning to continuous latent reasoning has proven difficult. To address this, we propose sourcing the necessary stochasticity through structured dropout. By applying a single Bernoulli mask held constant across all latent recurrence steps for a given rollout, we generate essential trajectory variance. This shared mask effectively treats each rollout as a posterior sample from a variational distribution over parameters, allowing GRPO to optimize the expected reward of a Bayesian model-average policy. We provide both theoretical justification for this method -- including unbiasedness, variance reduction, and the well-definedness of the latent gradient -- and empirical validation. On GSM8K, dropout-GRPO improves a Coconut baseline from $27.29\%$ to $29.01\%$ pass@1, demonstrating the viability of GRPO learning for latent-reasoning models. Our work positions this as a practical, theoretically grounded approach for post-training latent-reasoning LLMs.

💡 Highlight: Introduces Anchored Residual On-Policy Distillation (AR-OPD), a dual-view framework that disentangles privileged supervision in on-policy distillation to mitigate hindsight bias and improve reasoning in LLMs.
View Abstract

arXiv:2606.10385v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: On-policy distillation (OPD) has demonstrated strong empirical gains in enhancing complex reasoning in LLMs by aligning a student model with a teacher's predictive distribution over the student's own trajectories. An emerging variant, Privileged OPD, further strengthens this paradigm by employing a self-teacher model augmented with privileged information, such as oracle traces, to mitigate teacher-student capacity gaps while providing dense, answer-directed supervision. However, current methods treat privileged information as a monolithic imitation target, failing to disentangle locally reachable reasoning steps from future-conditioned oracle signals. Consequently, the student is encouraged to match a hindsight-biased distribution that often falls outside its local predictive support. This reachability mismatch incentivizes the student model to skip valid intermediate reasoning in favor of locally unsupported shortcuts. To resolve this, we introduce Anchored Residual On-Policy Distillation (AR-OPD), a dual-view framework that disentangles privileged supervision. Rather than enforcing strict full-view imitation, AR-OPD establishes a locally compatible anchor using a partially privileged teacher, isolating and injecting oracle foresight as a controlled residual to provide destination-directed guidance. Across diverse reasoning tasks, AR-OPD outperforms full privileged OPD by 2.3 points and SFT by 7.9 points. Crucially, this anchored residual mechanism reduces hindsight leakage by 21.7% and mitigates late-stage drift, yielding up to a 7.2-point advantage on challenging long-horizon trajectories exceeding 768 tokens.

View Abstract

arXiv:2606.10346v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Reinforcement learning has become a key paradigm for eliciting reasoning abilities in large language models, where exploration is crucial for discovering effective solution trajectories. Existing exploration methods typically encourage diversity in semantic or gradient spaces, without distinguishing what drives this diversity. A trajectory may appear novel because it follows a new reasoning process, or because it varies memorized patterns and shortcuts. Rewarding both cases equally may steer exploration toward memorization rather than genuine reasoning improvement. In this paper, we propose DiRL, a Direction-Aware Reinforcement Learning framework that anchors exploration to an internal reasoning-memorization direction of the policy. DiRL integrates seamlessly into standard Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO). Extensive experiments on mathematical and general reasoning benchmarks demonstrate the effectiveness of DiRL, showing significant improvements over various existing exploration methods.

View Abstract

arXiv:2606.09856v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Post-training Large Language Models (LLMs) for reasoning typically focuses on deductive tasks such as mathematics and coding where correctness is verifiable. Yet, many real-world reasoning problems are inductive: agents must infer uncertain beliefs from sparse, ambiguous observations. There are challenges to using standard fine-tuning methods for inductive reasoning, including difficulties in curating large-scale, high-quality labeled datasets and in handling targets that are inherently distributional. In this work, we introduce a novel approach, called Program-based Posterior Training (PPT), to address these limitations: we use an LLM to generate diverse open-world scenarios as probabilistic programs, run probabilistic inference to produce distributional target responses to queries, and then fine-tune on these probabilistic soft labels. Additionally, the gains in raw calibration are not subsumed by post-hoc temperature scaling, showing that the models have more deeply internalized uncertainty compared to output rescaling. Together, these results suggest that probabilistic-program-mediated fine-tuning is a promising approach for post-training LLMs to reliably perform approximate inductive inference.

arXiv:2606.09883v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Large language models (LLMs) have made remarkable progress in reasoning tasks, largely driven by post-training paradigms, especially reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards (RLVR). However, a critical bottleneck persists: RLVR fails on highly challenging zero-reward problems, where all sampled reasoning trajectories yield uniformly failed outcomes, providing no optimization signal to drive model improvement. Prior efforts to address this limitation, such as dense process supervision, partial reward assignment, or prefix-guided exploration, suffer from inherent task constraints or do not fully equip the policy model with the capabilities necessary to solve the original intractable problems. To address this, we propose TD-Grokking, a training-time decomposition framework for zero-reward problems. It recursively decomposes intractable root problems into self-contained, verifiable subproblems, forming hierarchical trees where solvable leaves provide non-zero rewards. Evaluations on mathematical and medical tasks show that TD-Grokking outperforms vanilla GRPO as well as all baseline approaches. Together with detailed analysis, these results confirm that training-time decomposition effectively converts zero-reward examples into usable training signals, enabling consistent performance gains. Our code and datasets are available at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/TD-Grokking-6567/.

Research Interest Criteria

Please evaluate papers using a strict filter. We only want a small set of strongly aligned papers, not a broad survey of adjacent work.

Global Selection Principle

  • Mark a paper as relevant only if one of the three topics below is a central contribution of the paper, not a side application, benchmark, or discussion point.
  • Be conservative by default: if the connection is indirect, partial, or ambiguous, treat it as not relevant or score it low.
  • Prefer papers that introduce new methods, training frameworks, reward designs, scalable self-improvement loops, or latent reasoning architectures with clear technical substance.
  • Do not favor papers just because they mention "LLM", "agent", "reasoning", "RL", "alignment", or "CoT" in a generic way.
  • Exclude broad foundation model papers, generic post-training papers, generic tool-use agents, application/system papers, domain-specific deployments, and pure benchmark papers unless their core method strongly matches one of the topics below.

1. Reinforcement Learning (RL) for LLMs

We care about RL methods that directly improve LLM behavior, alignment, or reasoning through explicit reward design, RL pipelines, verifier-driven optimization, or scalable preference/feedback mechanisms.

  • Strong match: RLHF/RLAIF variants, verifiable reward based RL, rejection sampling plus RL pipelines, reward model design, outcome/process reward methods, online RL for LLMs, and methods that materially improve helpfulness, honesty, harmlessness, or hallucination behavior.
  • Especially valuable: Papers that propose a new reward function, a new scalable RL training recipe, or a new data-generation / self-improvement loop for post-training LLMs.
  • Usually not relevant: Generic reinforcement learning papers, RL for robotics/control/games, prompt optimization without RL, supervised finetuning only, inference-time search without a training contribution, or alignment papers with no substantive RL component.

2. Latent CoT/Reasoning

We care about methods that move reasoning away from verbose explicit text chains into compact latent computation while preserving or improving reasoning quality.

  • Strong match: Latent reasoning architectures, hidden-state reasoning, implicit CoT, recurrent depth / recurrent latent computation, pause or thinking tokens for internal computation, explicit-to-latent distillation, and methods that reduce reasoning token cost or KV-cache use while retaining accuracy.
  • Especially valuable: New architectures or objectives for latent reasoning, hidden-state alignment/distillation, compression of reasoning traces, or methods that materially improve reasoning efficiency on math and logic tasks.
  • Usually not relevant: Standard explicit CoT prompting, generic reasoning finetuning, test-time scaling without latent computation, reasoning benchmarks, or papers whose main contribution is evaluation rather than a latent reasoning mechanism.

3. On-Policy Distillation for LLMs

We care about distillation methods that improve LLM capabilities by training on the model's own on-policy outputs, trajectories, preferences, corrections, or interaction data, especially when the student distribution is actively refreshed during training.

  • Strong match: On-policy distillation, self-distillation from sampled rollouts, online teacher-student distillation, policy-improvement distillation, iterative distillation from model-generated reasoning traces, distillation from verifier/reward-selected on-policy samples, and methods that close the gap between training data distribution and the model's current behavior.
  • Especially valuable: Papers that propose a new iterative distillation loop, sampling-and-selection strategy, online data refresh mechanism, teacher/student update schedule, or objective that measurably improves reasoning, instruction-following, alignment, tool use, or general LLM capability.
  • Usually not relevant: Standard offline knowledge distillation, static SFT on teacher-generated data, model compression only, data cleaning only, speculative decoding/distillation for speed only, or generic synthetic data generation without an on-policy or iterative policy-improvement component.

Final Preference

If a paper is merely adjacent to these areas, do not select it. Favor precision over recall. It is better to miss a borderline paper than to include many weakly related ones.
