💡 Daily Research Summary

### Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT)

Today's research demonstrates a sophisticated evolution of SFT, moving beyond simple instruction-response pairs towards highly specialized, process-oriented data synthesis. A key trend is the construction of SFT datasets that teach specific cognitive behaviors, such as epistemically calibrated decision-making (PassiveQA) or structured reasoning patterns for spatial and visual tasks (EgoMind, CLEAR). There is a strong emphasis on data-centric engineering, with pipelines for automatically generating challenging problems from error analysis and distilling domain-specific knowledge (e.g., from biological literature) into reasoning-ready formats. Furthermore, SFT is increasingly used as a foundational step to establish robust reasoning patterns before applying reinforcement learning, forming a critical part of multi-stage training pipelines for complex tasks like theorem proving and multilingual translation.

Reinforcement Learning (RL) for LLMs

The frontier of RL for LLMs is characterized by a dual focus on novel algorithmic efficiency and deeper reward engineering. On the algorithmic front, significant progress is made in off-policy and group-relative optimization methods (OP-GRPO, GRPO variants) that improve sample efficiency for flow-matching and diffusion models. Concurrently, researchers are designing more sophisticated reward mechanisms, including process reward models integrated with outcome signals and multi-objective alignment for structured reasoning. A critical sub-theme is the investigation and mitigation of RL's fundamental limitations, such as reward hacking vulnerabilities exposed by token-space attacks and the empirical study of RLHF's generalization boundaries. The integration of RL with verifiable rewards and self-distillation also emerges as a powerful paradigm for improving mathematical and coding capabilities.

Agents (Self-Evolving)

The field of autonomous agents is rapidly maturing, with today's work showcasing comprehensive frameworks for long-term, memory-centric, and skill-based evolution. A dominant theme is the systematic construction and refinement of reusable skill libraries, achieved through automated mining from heterogeneous resources, hierarchical experience distillation, and iterative verification. This is coupled with advanced memory architectures that feature biologically-inspired forgetting, multi-channel retrieval, and persistent, evolvable case-based storage, enabling agents to maintain long-term coherence and adaptivity. The paradigm of test-time learning and reflection is strongly emphasized, with agents learning to optimize their own context, plan based on uncertainty, and recover from failures through cascade correction. These components converge towards the vision of persistent, ambiently self-perceptive agent runtimes capable of complex, long-horizon task orchestration.

Multimodal

Multimodal research is advancing through structured training pipelines that tightly couple reasoning with generation. A clear pattern involves using SFT to establish a foundational reasoning chain (e.g., for spatial or causal understanding) followed by RL-based post-training to refine and align the final output. This is evident in frameworks for video reasoning, degraded image understanding, and causal video analysis. There is also a push for scalable, general RL recipes that work across diverse visual tasks and for using RL as a tool not just for alignment but for the active discovery of model failure modes, thereby improving robustness and diagnostic capabilities.

Latent CoT / Reasoning Compression

Efficiency in long-context reasoning is being tackled through innovative compression and early termination strategies. A major breakthrough direction is the dynamic management of the reasoning process itself, including methods to compress intermediate reasoning steps into compact semantic representations for later reuse and to halt generation early based on confidence dynamics, drastically reducing computational overhead. Research also delves into the architecture level, proposing efficient KV cache compression via trigonometric functions to alleviate memory bottlenecks. Furthermore, studies investigate the fundamental trade-offs in compressing explicit reasoning traces and explore decoupling core reasoning from structured output requirements to mitigate "format tax" degradation.

Compression & Efficient Inference

Beyond reasoning-specific compression, novel inference-time techniques are emerging to optimize knowledge delivery and model architecture. A standout method proposes zero-token knowledge injection via direct manipulation of the Key-Value cache, bypassing the need for prompt engineering or additional context tokens. This represents a significant step towards more efficient, targeted knowledge utilization during inference, complementing the broader efforts in model and reasoning pathway compression.

💡 Highlight: Directly addresses SFT and RL for LLMs, detailing a three-stage training pipeline (SFT, RL, reasoning cache) to teach a small model complex theorem proving, perfectly matching the specified research directions.

arXiv:2604.04898v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Proprietary AI systems have recently demonstrated impressive capabilities on complex proof-based problems, with gold-level performance reported at the 2025 International Mathematical Olympiad (IMO). However, the training pipelines behind these systems remain largely undisclosed, and their reliance on large "internal" models and scaffolds makes them expensive to run, difficult to reproduce, and hard to study or improve upon. This raises a central question: can small, open models also be trained to achieve competitive reasoning performance on difficult Olympiad-level math? In this paper, we answer this question by building QED-Nano, a 4B model post-trained for Olympiad-level proofs. QED-Nano surpasses the proof-generation performance of much larger open models, including Nomos-1 and GPT-OSS-120B, and approaches the performance of proprietary models like Gemini 3 Pro, at a fraction of the inference cost. To support further research on open mathematical reasoning, we release the full QED-Nano pipeline, including the QED-Nano and QED-Nano-SFT models, the FineProofs-SFT and FineProofs-RL datasets, and the training and evaluation code.

arXiv:2604.03253v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: A promising research direction in enabling LLMs to generate consistently correct code involves addressing their inability to properly estimate program execution, particularly for code they generate. In this work, we demonstrate that Code LLMs can be trained to simulate program execution in a step-by-step manner and that this capability can be leveraged to improve competitive programming performance. View Abstract

arXiv:2604.04142v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Post training via GRPO has demonstrated remarkable effectiveness in improving the generation quality of flow-matching models. However, GRPO suffers from inherently low sample efficiency due to its on-policy training paradigm. To address this limitation, we present OP-GRPO, the first Off-Policy GRPO framework tailored for flow-matching models. First, we actively select high-quality trajectories and adaptively incorporate them into a replay buffer for reuse in subsequent training iterations. Across image and video generation benchmarks, OP-GRPO achieves comparable or superior performance to Flow-GRPO with only 34.2% of the training steps on average, yielding substantial gains in training efficiency while maintaining generation quality.

🏫 Affiliations: Unknown Institution

💡 Highlight: This paper directly addresses the 'Agents for LLMs' and 'Reinforcement Learning (RL) for LLMs' criteria by proposing vocabulary dropout to sustain diversity in a co-evolutionary self-play loop for autonomous curriculum learning in mathematical reasoning.
View Abstract

arXiv:2604.03472v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Co-evolutionary self-play, where one language model generates problems and another solves them, promises autonomous curriculum learning without human supervision. In practice, the proposer quickly converges to a narrow distribution of problems that satisfy the reward function. This diversity collapse renders the curriculum uninformative for the solver, stalling the co-evolutionary loop. We introduce vocabulary dropout, a random mask applied to the proposer's output logits during both policy training and curriculum generation, as a lightweight mechanism to sustain diversity. The mask is hard and non-stationary, preventing the proposer from locking into fixed token sequences. Training Qwen3-4B and Qwen3-8B on mathematical reasoning via R-Zero, we find that vocabulary dropout sustains proposer diversity across lexical, semantic, and functional metrics throughout training, and yields solver improvements averaging +4.4 points at 8B, with the largest gains on competition-level benchmarks. Our findings suggest that explicit action-space constraints, analogous to the structural role that game rules play in classical self-play, can help sustain productive co-evolution in language. Vocabulary dropout is one simple instantiation of this principle.

arXiv:2604.03098v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Large language model (LLM) agents learn by interacting with environments, but long-horizon training remains fundamentally bottlenecked by sparse and delayed rewards. Existing methods typically address this challenge through post-hoc credit assignment or external reward models, which provide limited guidance at inference time and often separate reward improvement from policy improvement. We propose Self-Guide, a self-generated internal reward for language agents that supports both inference-time guidance and training-time supervision. Specifically, the agent uses Self-Guide as a short self-guidance signal to steer the next action during inference, and converts the same signal into step-level internal reward for denser policy optimization during training. This creates a co-evolving loop: better policy produces better guidance, and better guidance further improves policy as internal reward. Across three agent benchmarks, inference-time self-guidance already yields clear gains, while jointly evolving policy and internal reward with GRPO brings further improvements (8\%) over baselines trained solely with environment reward. Overall, our results suggest that language agents can improve not only by collecting more experience, but also by learning to generate and refine their own internal reward during acting and learning.

6. Self-Distilled RLVR

ArXiv ID: 2604.03128 |

👥 Authors: Chenxu Yang, Chuanyu Qin, Qingyi Si, Minghui Chen, Naibin Gu, Dingyu Yao, Zheng Lin, Weiping Wang, Jiaqi Wang, Nan Duan

🏫 Affiliations: Unknown Institution

💡 Highlight: Proposes RLSD, a method combining RL with verifiable rewards and self-distillation, directly addressing core challenges in RL for LLMs and SFT optimization.
View Abstract

arXiv:2604.03128v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: On-policy distillation (OPD) has become a popular training paradigm in the LLM community. This paradigm selects a larger model as the teacher to provide dense, fine-grained signals for each sampled trajectory, in contrast to reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards (RLVR), which only obtains sparse signals from verifiable outcomes in the environment. Recently, the community has explored on-policy self-distillation (OPSD), where the same model serves as both teacher and student, with the teacher receiving additional privileged information such as reference answers to enable self-evolution. This paper demonstrates that learning signals solely derived from the privileged teacher result in severe information leakage and unstable long-term training. Accordingly, we identify the optimal niche for self-distillation and propose \textbf{RLSD} (\textbf{RL}VR with \textbf{S}elf-\textbf{D}istillation). Specifically, we leverage self-distillation to obtain token-level policy differences for determining fine-grained update magnitudes, while continuing to use RLVR to derive reliable update directions from environmental feedback (e.g., response correctness). This enables RLSD to simultaneously harness the strengths of both RLVR and OPSD, achieving a higher convergence ceiling and superior training stability.

8. Readable Minds: Emergent Theory-of-Mind-Like Behavior in LLM Poker Agents

ArXiv ID: 2604.04157 |

👥 Authors: Hsieh-Ting Lin, Tsung-Yu Hou

🏫 Affiliations: Unknown Institution

💡 Highlight: This paper is highly relevant to Agents for LLMs, specifically self-evolving agents, as it demonstrates autonomous LLM agents developing sophisticated Theory-of-Mind and strategic deception through persistent memory and dynamic interaction.
View Abstract

arXiv:2604.04157v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Theory of Mind (ToM) -- the ability to model others' mental states -- is fundamental to human social cognition. Whether large language models (LLMs) can develop ToM has been tested exclusively through static vignettes, leaving open whether ToM-like reasoning can emerge through dynamic interaction. Here we report that autonomous LLM agents playing extended sessions of Texas Hold'em poker progressively develop sophisticated opponent models, but only when equipped with persistent memory. In a 2x2 factorial design crossing memory (present/absent) with domain knowledge (present/absent), each with five replications (N = 20 experiments, ~6,000 agent-hand observations), we find that memory is both necessary and sufficient for ToM-like behavior emergence (Cliff's delta = 1.0, p = 0.008). Agents with memory reach ToM Level 3-5 (predictive to recursive modeling), while agents without memory remain at Level 0 across all replications. Strategic deception grounded in opponent models occurs exclusively in memory-equipped conditions (Fisher's exact p < 0.001). Domain expertise does not gate ToM-like behavior emergence but enhances its application: agents without poker knowledge develop equivalent ToM levels but less precise deception (p = 0.004). Agents with ToM deviate from game-theoretically optimal play (67% vs. 79% TAG adherence, delta = -1.0, p = 0.008) to exploit specific opponents, mirroring expert human play. All mental models are expressed in natural language and directly readable, providing a transparent window into AI social cognition. Cross-model validation with GPT-4o yields weighted Cohen's kappa = 0.81 (almost perfect agreement). These findings demonstrate that functional ToM-like behavior can emerge from interaction dynamics alone, without explicit training or prompting, with implications for understanding artificial social intelligence and biological social cognition.

Please evaluate the papers based on the following three core areas:

1. Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) for LLMs

Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) is a critical technique for adapting pre-trained Large Language Models (LLMs) to specific downstream tasks or domains. This process involves further training the model on a curated dataset of high-quality, labeled examples that reflect the target behavior. Our research focuses on optimizing the SFT process to enhance model performance, alignment, and efficiency.

  • Key Direction 1: Investigating novel data-sourcing and data-synthesis strategies to construct high-quality, diverse, and instruction-rich SFT datasets.
  • Key Direction 2: Developing advanced SFT algorithms and optimization techniques to improve the efficiency and effectiveness of fine-tuning on specialized tasks.
  • Potential Contribution: Establishing best practices for curating SFT data and designing fine-tuning protocols that lead to more capable and reliable LLMs.

2. Reinforcement Learning (RL) for LLMs

Reinforcement Learning, particularly from human or AI-generated feedback, offers a powerful paradigm for aligning LLM behavior with complex human preferences and objectives. This research area explores methods beyond simple instruction-following, aiming to imbue models with more nuanced capabilities. Our work centers on creating robust RL pipelines and novel reward mechanisms to steer LLM development.

  • Relevance: We explore cutting-edge RL techniques, including rejection sampling and unsupervised reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards, to build sophisticated data pipelines that continuously enhance LLM capabilities.
  • Key Direction: Designing novel reward functions that directly target improvements in key metrics such as helpfulness, honesty, harmlessness, and reductions in hallucination.
  • Potential Contribution: Pioneering new research directions in RL for LLMs that inspire further investigation and bridge the gap between theoretical advancements and practical, large-scale model improvements.

3. Agents for LLMs

Autonomous agents built upon LLMs are a key step towards creating general-purpose problem-solving systems. Our emphasis is on self-evolving agents: agents that can iteratively improve their policies, tools, and internal knowledge through experience. This direction studies the mechanisms that make agent behavior not only autonomous at inference time, but continually improving over long horizons via self-generated feedback and data. The goal is to design agent architectures that translate self-evolution into measurable capability gains and strong real-world deployability.

  • Relevance: Centering on self-evolving agents—agent systems that autonomously generate supervision signals, curate experience, and improve themselves over time rather than remaining static after deployment.
  • Key Direction: Building closed-loop improvement frameworks, e.g., self-reflection and self-critique, automated curriculum generation, and learning from interaction traces (including tool-use trajectories) to drive continual adaptation.
  • Potential Contribution: Delivering practical, deployable self-evolving agents that demonstrate sustained improvements in reasoning, planning, and tool use, with clear evaluation protocols that quantify long-horizon learning progress and reliability.

4. Latent CoT/Reasoning

Latent Chain-of-Thought (CoT) or implicit reasoning aims to shift the reasoning process of Large Language Models (LLMs) from explicit, step-by-step natural language text generation into a continuous latent representation space (hidden vectors or states). While traditional explicit CoT significantly enhances multi-step reasoning capabilities, it incurs high computational costs, severe inference latency, and redundant "overthinking". This research area explores methods beyond standard autoregressive text generation, allowing models to reason "silently" and efficiently internally, retaining the power of CoT while drastically reducing the overhead of generating intermediate steps. Our work centers on building efficient latent reasoning frameworks and alignment mechanisms to steer models to complete complex tasks in compact latent spaces.

  • Relevance: We explore cutting-edge latent reasoning techniques, including compressing explicit reasoning trajectories into continuous vectors via self-distillation, applying reinforcement learning (RL) without explicit CoT data supervision, introducing special placeholder tokens (such as <pause> or <st> tokens) for extended computation, and utilizing recurrent depth architectures to unroll reasoning in latent space. These methods aim to build sophisticated data and training pipelines that continuously enhance computational efficiency and reasoning capabilities.
  • Key Direction: Designing novel reasoning networks and objective functions, such as transferring knowledge from teacher to student models through hidden state alignment, combining dynamic compression factors with probabilistic latent heads to encourage exploration and exploitation of shorter reasoning paths, and leveraging spatial inductive biases (e.g., rendering CoT into images) to preserve global reasoning structures. These directions directly target improvements in key metrics, such as drastically reducing inference latency, minimizing KV-Cache memory footprint, and boosting accuracy on math and complex logic tasks.
  • Potential Contribution: Pioneering new research directions in LLM reasoning architectures that bridge the gap between the high accuracy of explicit CoT and the low inference cost of direct generation. By breaking away from the reliance on verbose and costly text generation, this not only provides a viable alternative for real-world, large-scale model deployments that are resource-constrained and latency-sensitive, but also inspires further exploration into high-dimensional continuous space reasoning beyond linguistic forms (e.g., spatial and physical intuitions).
