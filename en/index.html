### Daily Research Summary

RL for LLMs

Today's research demonstrates a significant push toward refining the reward modeling and policy optimization foundations of RLHF. A key trend is the move from purely empirical reward models to more principled, theoretically-grounded frameworks. CausalRM exemplifies this by applying causal inference to learn from observational feedback, aiming to produce more robust and unbiased reward signals. Complementing this, MoRI and AutoPipe focus on the practical orchestration of RL. MoRI shows the value of composite, motivation-grounded rewards for complex tasks like scientific reasoning, while AutoPipe addresses the critical engineering challenge of automatically configuring the entire SFT+RL pipeline for optimal performance and efficiency. Together, these works signal a maturation of RL for LLMs, tackling both the theoretical integrity of the reward function and the systemic optimization of the training process.

SFT (Supervised Fine-Tuning)

The papers highlight an evolving view of SFT as a versatile tool for specialization and alignment, often serving as a crucial precursor or component within larger pipelines. MoRI utilizes SFT to initially ground models in motivation-based reasoning before RL refinement. Expert Personas research reveals a nuanced role for SFT in creating specialized expert personas, which can enhance alignment on subjective traits but may trade off factual accuracy, prompting the need for careful routing mechanisms like PRISM. Furthermore, AutoPipe implicitly treats SFT not as a standalone step but as an integral, configurable module within an automated post-training workflow. The collective insight is that modern SFT is increasingly targeted (e.g., for specific reasoning styles or expert roles) and systematically integrated into broader, optimized training regimes.

Agents

Research on expert personas, while often framed as an alignment or prompting technique, directly advances the architecture of LLM-based agents. The work on Expert Personas and the PRISM bootstrapping method moves beyond simple prompt engineering toward a more structured paradigm of intent-based routing to specialized agent sub-models. This approach essentially creates a multi-agent system within a single model framework, where different "personas" act as expert agents invoked based on the user's inferred intent. This direction points toward more modular, reliable, and capable AI systems that can dynamically assemble the right expertise for a given task, a core challenge in agent design.

1. CausalRM: Causal-Theoretic Reward Modeling for RLHF from Observational User Feedbacks

ArXiv ID: 2603.18736v1 |

👥 Authors: Hao Wang, Licheng Pan, Zhichao Chen, Chunyuan Zheng, Zhixuan Chu, Xiaoxi Li, Yuan Lu, Xinggao Liu, Haoxuan Li, Zhouchen Lin

🏫 Affiliations: Unknown Institution

💡 Highlight: Proposes a causal-theoretic reward modeling framework (CausalRM) for RLHF using observational user feedback, directly addressing a key direction in RL for LLMs.
View Abstract

Despite the success of reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) in aligning language models, current reward modeling heavily relies on experimental feedback data collected from human annotators under controlled and costly conditions. In this work, we introduce observational reward modeling -- learning reward models with observational user feedback (e.g., clicks, copies, and upvotes) -- as a scalable and cost-effective alternative. We identify two fundamental challenges in this setting: (1) observational feedback is noisy due to annotation errors, which deviates it from true user preference; (2) observational feedback is biased by user preference, where users preferentially provide feedback on responses they feel strongly about, which creats a distribution shift between training and inference data. To address these challenges, we propose CausalRM, a causal-theoretic reward modeling framework that aims to learn unbiased reward models from observational feedback. To tackle challenge (1), CausalRM introduces a noise-aware surrogate loss term that is provably equivalent to the primal loss under noise-free conditions by explicitly modeling the annotation error generation process. To tackle challenge (2), CausalRM uses propensity scores -- the probability of a user providing feedback for a given response -- to reweight training samples, yielding a loss function that eliminates user preference bias. Extensive experiments across diverse LLM backbones and benchmark datasets validate that CausalRM effectively learns accurate reward signals from noisy and biased observational feedback and delivers substantial performance improvements on downstream RLHF tasks -- including a 49.2% gain on WildGuardMix and a 32.7% improvement on HarmBench. Code is available on our project website.

2. MoRI: Learning Motivation-Grounded Reasoning for Scientific Ideation in Large Language Models

ArXiv ID: 2603.19044v1 |

👥 Authors: Chenyang Gu, Jiahao Cheng, Meicong Zhang, Pujun Zheng, Jinquan Zheng, Guoxiu He

🏫 Affiliations: ECNU-Text-Computing

💡 Highlight: Introduces MoRI, a framework using SFT and a composite RL reward to ground scientific ideation in motivation-based reasoning, aligning with both SFT and RL for LLMs research.
View Abstract

Scientific ideation aims to propose novel solutions within a given scientific context. Existing LLM-based agentic approaches emulate human research workflows, yet inadequately model scientific reasoning, resulting in surface-level conceptual recombinations that lack technical depth and scientific grounding. To address this issue, we propose \textbf{MoRI} (\textbf{Mo}tivation-grounded \textbf{R}easoning for Scientific \textbf{I}deation), a framework that enables LLMs to explicitly learn the reasoning process from research motivations to methodologies. The base LLM is initialized via supervised fine-tuning to generate a research motivation from a given context, and is subsequently trained under a composite reinforcement learning reward that approximates scientific rigor: (1) entropy-aware information gain encourages the model to uncover and elaborate high-complexity technical details grounded in ground-truth methodologies, and (2) contrastive semantic gain constrains the reasoning trajectory to maintain conceptually aligned with scientifically valid solutions. Empirical results show that MoRI significantly outperforms strong commercial LLMs and complex agentic baselines across multiple dimensions, including novelty, technical rigor, and feasibility. The code will be made available on \href{https://github.com/ECNU-Text-Computing/IdeaGeneration}{GitHub}.

3. Expert Personas Improve LLM Alignment but Damage Accuracy: Bootstrapping Intent-Based Persona Routing with PRISM

ArXiv ID: 2603.18507v1 |

👥 Authors: Zizhao Hu, Mohammad Rostami, Jesse Thomason

🏫 Affiliations: Unknown Institution

💡 Highlight: Investigates expert personas for LLM alignment and proposes PRISM, a bootstrapping method to distill intent-conditioned personas, relevant to SFT and advanced prompt engineering.
View Abstract

Persona prompting can steer LLM generation towards a domain-specific tone and pattern. This behavior enables use cases in multi-agent systems where diverse interactions are crucial and human-centered tasks require high-level human alignment. Prior works provide mixed opinions on their utility: some report performance gains when using expert personas for certain domains and their contribution to data diversity in synthetic data creation, while others find near-zero or negative impact on general utility. To fully leverage the benefits of the LLM persona and avoid its harmfulness, a more comprehensive investigation of the mechanism is crucial. In this work, we study how model optimization, task type, prompt length, and placement can impact expert persona effectiveness across instruction-tuned and reasoning LLMs, and provide insight into conditions under which expert personas fail and succeed. Based on our findings, we developed a pipeline to fully leverage the benefits of an expert persona, named PRISM (Persona Routing via Intent-based Self-Modeling), which self-distills an intent-conditioned expert persona into a gated LoRA adapter through a bootstrapping process that requires no external data, models, or knowledge. PRISM enhances human preference and safety alignment on generative tasks while maintaining accuracy on discriminative tasks across all models, with minimal memory and computing overhead.

4. Automatic Configuration of LLM Post-Training Pipelines

ArXiv ID: 2603.18773v1 |

👥 Authors: Channe Chwa, Xinle Wu, Yao Lu

🏫 Affiliations: Unknown Institution

💡 Highlight: Presents AutoPipe, an automatic configuration framework for LLM post-training pipelines (SFT+RL), directly relevant to optimizing SFT and RL processes for efficiency.
View Abstract

LLM post-training pipelines that combine supervised fine-tuning and reinforcement learning are difficult to configure under realistic compute budgets: the configuration space is high-dimensional and heterogeneous, stages are strongly coupled, and each end-to-end evaluation is expensive. We propose AutoPipe, a budget-aware two-stage framework for configuration selection in LLM post-training. Offline, AutoPipe learns a dataset-conditioned learning-to-rank surrogate from historical runs, capturing within-dataset preferences and providing transferable guidance toward promising regions of the configuration space. Online, for a new dataset, AutoPipe uses the offline guidance to steer Bayesian optimization and models dataset-specific deviations with a Gaussian-process residual surrogate. To reduce evaluation cost, each trial is early-stopped and scored by a learned predictor that maps early training signals to a low-cost proxy for final post-training performance. Experiments on biomedical reasoning tasks show that AutoPipe consistently outperforms offline-only baselines and achieves comparable performance with the strongest online HPO baselines while using less than 10\% of their computational cost.

Research Interest Criteria

Please evaluate the papers based on the following three core areas:

1. Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) for LLMs

Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) is a critical technique for adapting pre-trained Large Language Models (LLMs) to specific downstream tasks or domains. This process involves further training the model on a curated dataset of high-quality, labeled examples that reflect the target behavior. Our research focuses on optimizing the SFT process to enhance model performance, alignment, and efficiency.

  • Key Direction 1: Investigating novel data-sourcing and data-synthesis strategies to construct high-quality, diverse, and instruction-rich SFT datasets.
  • Key Direction 2: Developing advanced SFT algorithms and optimization techniques to improve the efficiency and effectiveness of fine-tuning on specialized tasks.
  • Potential Contribution: Establishing best practices for curating SFT data and designing fine-tuning protocols that lead to more capable and reliable LLMs.

2. Reinforcement Learning (RL) for LLMs

Reinforcement Learning, particularly from human or AI-generated feedback, offers a powerful paradigm for aligning LLM behavior with complex human preferences and objectives. This research area explores methods beyond simple instruction-following, aiming to imbue models with more nuanced capabilities. Our work centers on creating robust RL pipelines and novel reward mechanisms to steer LLM development.

  • Relevance: We explore cutting-edge RL techniques, including rejection sampling and unsupervised reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards, to build sophisticated data pipelines that continuously enhance LLM capabilities.
  • Key Direction: Designing novel reward functions that directly target improvements in key metrics such as helpfulness, honesty, harmlessness, and reductions in hallucination.
  • Potential Contribution: Pioneering new research directions in RL for LLMs that inspire further investigation and bridge the gap between theoretical advancements and practical, large-scale model improvements.

3. Agents for LLMs

Autonomous agents built upon LLMs are a key step towards creating general-purpose problem-solving systems. Our emphasis is on self-evolving agents: agents that can iteratively improve their policies, tools, and internal knowledge through experience. This direction studies the mechanisms that make agent behavior not only autonomous at inference time, but continually improving over long horizons via self-generated feedback and data. The goal is to design agent architectures that translate self-evolution into measurable capability gains and strong real-world deployability.

  • Relevance: Centering on self-evolving agents—agent systems that autonomously generate supervision signals, curate experience, and improve themselves over time rather than remaining static after deployment.
  • Key Direction: Building closed-loop improvement frameworks, e.g., self-reflection and self-critique, automated curriculum generation, and learning from interaction traces (including tool-use trajectories) to drive continual adaptation.
  • Potential Contribution: Delivering practical, deployable self-evolving agents that demonstrate sustained improvements in reasoning, planning, and tool use, with clear evaluation protocols that quantify long-horizon learning progress and reliability.
