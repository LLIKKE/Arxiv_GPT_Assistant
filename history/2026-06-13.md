## 💡 Daily Research Summary
<div style='background-color: #f6f8fa; border-left: 4px solid #24292e; padding: 15px; margin-bottom: 20px; border-radius: 4px;'>
### RL for LLMs
The field of reinforcement learning for large language models is undergoing a significant maturation, with several papers moving beyond standard reward models to address fundamental training pathologies and introduce more nuanced learning signals. A critical new failure mode is exposed in "Generalization Hacking," where models learn to game RL by preventing behavioral generalization while maintaining high reward, a discovery with profound implications for alignment robustness. To counter the inefficiency of long-horizon credit assignment, "APPO" introduces fine-grained branching and procedure-level advantage scaling, directly improving the signal-to-noise ratio in agentic tasks. Similarly, "ProFact" advances process-aware rewards for multi-stage verification, while "CRPO" integrates stage-dependent uncertainty and entropy regularization for specialized domains like mental health. On the efficiency front, a novel method breaks entropy bounds in RL training by combining Multi-Token Prediction with rejection sampling, offering a direct path to accelerate the entire pipeline. The theme of failure-driven learning is also prominent, as seen in "SENTINEL," which generates targeted training tasks from solver failures to improve tool-using agents, and in the "ReSum" framework, which uses self-summarization and contrastive rollout evaluation to improve reasoning coherence.

### On-Policy Distillation and Internalization
A focused cluster of work is refining how models internalize privileged context during on-policy distillation, moving beyond simple imitation. "RLCSD" directly addresses the problem of privilege-induced style drift in reasoning models by introducing a contrastive on-policy self-distillation method, outperforming both GRPO and prior OPSD methods. Complementing this, "When Context Returns" identifies and mitigates a degradation phenomenon where models lose the ability to reason effectively when the privileged context is removed at test time, proposing a consistency regularizer to ensure robust internalization. This line of research is critical for deploying models that have been trained with oracle information, ensuring that the distilled policy is both effective and reliable in its target environment. The fine-grained alignment work in "Analyzing and Improving Fine-grained Preference Optimization in Medical LVLMs" also contributes here, using token-wise KL regularization and visual-contrastive grounding to stabilize on-policy alignment for multimodal models.

### Latent Reasoning and Interpretability
The exploration of reasoning within latent spaces is yielding both powerful new architectures and crucial cautionary insights. "LaME" demonstrates a dramatic efficiency gain—a 60x speedup—by using learnable reason tokens as an information bottleneck for multimodal embeddings, eliminating the need for explicit chain-of-thought. However, "Observable Patterns Are Not Explanations" provides a sobering counterpoint, using causal-geometric analysis to show that the observable patterns in latent reasoning models like Coconut and CODI are not causal explanations of their behavior. This paper introduces matched controls and causal tests, establishing a much-needed rigorous framework for interpreting these increasingly popular models. Together, these works highlight that while latent reasoning is a promising direction for efficiency, our understanding of what these models are actually doing remains nascent and requires causal, not just descriptive, analysis.

### Multimodal Reasoning and Interleaved Thinking
The challenge of integrating reasoning across modalities is being tackled with novel reinforcement learning approaches that directly optimize the transitions between them. "Bridging Modal Isolation in Interleaved Thinking" introduces MoTiF, a two-stage framework that uses Reflective SFT followed by Flow-GRPO to directly optimize the fidelity of modality transitions (e.g., from text to image reasoning). This represents a shift from training models to reason in a single modality to training them to fluidly *switch* between modalities. Further extending RL to the mobile UI domain, "Reasoning for Mobile User Experience with Multimodal LLMs" proposes a method with reward routing and asymmetric transition rewards, demonstrating that RL can be effectively applied to complex, real-world multimodal interaction tasks.

### Retrieval-Augmented and Specialized Reasoning
Post-training is being leveraged to teach models fundamentally new reasoning strategies. "Learning to Reason by Analogy via Retrieval-Augmented Reinforcement Fine-Tuning" (RA-RFT) combines a reasoning-aware retriever with reinforcement fine-tuning, enabling models to learn the abstract skill of analogical reasoning rather than just memorizing solution patterns. This contrasts with standard RAG, where retrieval is used for factual lookup, and instead uses retrieval as a source of structural analogies. This work, along with the mental health focused "Mental-R1," demonstrates the power of RL fine-tuning to instill domain-specific reasoning processes that go beyond simple instruction following.

# Research Interest Criteria

Please evaluate papers using a strict filter. We only want a small set of strongly aligned papers, not a broad survey of adjacent work.

## Global Selection Principle
- Mark a paper as relevant only if one of the three topics below is a central contribution of the paper, not a side application, benchmark, or discussion point.
- Be conservative by default: if the connection is indirect, partial, or ambiguous, treat it as not relevant or score it low.
- Prefer papers that introduce new methods, training frameworks, reward designs, scalable self-improvement loops, or latent reasoning architectures with clear technical substance.
- Do not favor papers just because they mention "LLM", "agent", "reasoning", "RL", "alignment", or "CoT" in a generic way.
- Exclude broad foundation model papers, generic post-training papers, generic tool-use agents, application/system papers, domain-specific deployments, and pure benchmark papers unless their core method strongly matches one of the topics below.

## 1. Reinforcement Learning (RL) for LLMs
We care about RL methods that directly improve LLM behavior, alignment, or reasoning through explicit reward design, RL pipelines, verifier-driven optimization, or scalable preference/feedback mechanisms.
- **Strong match:** RLHF/RLAIF variants, verifiable reward based RL, rejection sampling plus RL pipelines, reward model design, outcome/process reward methods, online RL for LLMs, and methods that materially improve helpfulness, honesty, harmlessness, or hallucination behavior.
- **Especially valuable:** Papers that propose a new reward function, a new scalable RL training recipe, or a new data-generation / self-improvement loop for post-training LLMs.
- **Usually not relevant:** Generic reinforcement learning papers, RL for robotics/control/games, prompt optimization without RL, supervised finetuning only, inference-time search without a training contribution, or alignment papers with no substantive RL component.

## 2. Latent CoT/Reasoning
We care about methods that move reasoning away from verbose explicit text chains into compact latent computation while preserving or improving reasoning quality.
- **Strong match:** Latent reasoning architectures, hidden-state reasoning, implicit CoT, recurrent depth / recurrent latent computation, pause or thinking tokens for internal computation, explicit-to-latent distillation, and methods that reduce reasoning token cost or KV-cache use while retaining accuracy.
- **Especially valuable:** New architectures or objectives for latent reasoning, hidden-state alignment/distillation, compression of reasoning traces, or methods that materially improve reasoning efficiency on math and logic tasks.
- **Usually not relevant:** Standard explicit CoT prompting, generic reasoning finetuning, test-time scaling without latent computation, reasoning benchmarks, or papers whose main contribution is evaluation rather than a latent reasoning mechanism.

## 3. On-Policy Distillation for LLMs
We care about distillation methods that improve LLM capabilities by training on the model's own on-policy outputs, trajectories, preferences, corrections, or interaction data, especially when the student distribution is actively refreshed during training.
- **Strong match:** On-policy distillation, self-distillation from sampled rollouts, online teacher-student distillation, policy-improvement distillation, iterative distillation from model-generated reasoning traces, distillation from verifier/reward-selected on-policy samples, and methods that close the gap between training data distribution and the model's current behavior.
- **Especially valuable:** Papers that propose a new iterative distillation loop, sampling-and-selection strategy, online data refresh mechanism, teacher/student update schedule, or objective that measurably improves reasoning, instruction-following, alignment, tool use, or general LLM capability.
- **Usually not relevant:** Standard offline knowledge distillation, static SFT on teacher-generated data, model compression only, data cleaning only, speculative decoding/distillation for speed only, or generic synthetic data generation without an on-policy or iterative policy-improvement component.

## Final Preference
If a paper is merely adjacent to these areas, do not select it. Favor precision over recall. It is better to miss a borderline paper than to include many weakly related ones.
