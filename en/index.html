💡 Daily Research Summary

### Reinforcement Learning for LLMs The research landscape today is dominated by sophisticated advancements in reinforcement learning for large language models, moving decisively beyond basic RLHF. A central theme is the development of more stable and effective training objectives that operate at the distribution or marginal level, such as optimizing P(y) in pre-train space or using distribution-level RL (DistRL), to expand reasoning capacity before fine-grained optimization. Concurrently, significant effort is being directed at solving core technical vulnerabilities: new architectures like multi-timescale PPO with target decoupling aim to overcome policy hacking and credit assignment, while a suite of novel methods—from causal reward decomposition and implicit prefix-value models to fine-grained exploration via perplexity disentanglement (DiPO)—are being proposed to create more robust, spurious-cue-free reward signals and address reward sparsity. This collective push represents a maturation of the field, focusing on the foundational stability and quality of the RL optimization process itself.

Self-Improving & Evolving Agents

A powerful parallel trend is the emergence of frameworks for autonomous, self-improving agent systems that operate without external supervision. These systems are increasingly built on principles of self-play and internal distillation, where agents evolve by generating their own solution traces, engaging in multi-agent interactions, or distilling knowledge from their own inference experiences in a training-free loop. This is exemplified by multi-agent privileged self-distillation (π-Play), deterministic geometric co-evolution for spatial intelligence (SpatialEvo), and collaborative peer-predictive self-training. The paradigm extends to embodied domains, where agents employ long short-term reflective optimization for continuous skill improvement. The core innovation is the creation of closed-loop, self-sustaining improvement cycles, moving learning from a data-dependent process to an experience-driven, internally guided evolution.

Agent Frameworks & Tool Use

Research on practical agent frameworks is advancing on two key fronts: improving robustness in long-horizon tasks and enhancing open-world tool use. For complex, extended tasks like robotic manipulation, new architectures feature dual-system planning with adaptive replanning and closed-loop recovery mechanisms, ensuring agents can dynamically adjust to failures. In the realm of tool augmentation, the focus is on decoupled, multi-objective optimization (e.g., via GRPO) to separately but jointly refine skills for proactive tool retrieval and grounded execution. Furthermore, there is a growing emphasis on endowing agents with transferable, structured expertise through case-based learning frameworks, allowing them to extract and reuse procedural knowledge from past successes, thereby accelerating learning and improving performance on novel tasks.

Multimodal & Specialized Reasoning

The application of RL and specialized training paradigms is yielding strong gains in complex, multimodal reasoning domains. Novel RL algorithms are being tailored for specific challenges, such as joint optimization of evidence localization and answer accuracy in long document QA (Evidence-aware Group RPO) or multi-turn RL for visual document understanding. Beyond pure performance, there is a push for alignment with nuanced human processes, using RL to steer models toward human-like justification in sentiment analysis or role-playing fidelity in audio LLMs. This trend highlights a shift from generic model improvement to the design of bespoke training objectives that directly address the structural and evaluative demands of specialized tasks, including critical applications like verifiable, RAG-enhanced deepfake detection.

Model Efficiency & Training Optimization

Efficiency research is targeting critical bottlenecks in both inference and training. For inference, novel KV cache compression methods like Orthogonal Backfill are proposed to preserve information in latent multi-agent communication, directly addressing the scalability of collaborative reasoning systems. On the training side, innovations focus on improving the data efficiency of knowledge transfer, such as token-importance-aware selection for on-policy distillation to make the distillation process more effective. These works reflect a concerted effort to reduce the computational overhead of advanced AI systems without sacrificing capability, ensuring that more complex agentic and multi-model interactions remain practically feasible.

Alignment & Safety

Safety and alignment research is adopting more formal and structured approaches to mitigate risks inherent in RL-based systems. A significant contribution is a unifying survey and framework for understanding reward hacking, providing a critical lens on this core vulnerability. In response, proposed safety mechanisms are becoming more sophisticated, employing Bayesian reward shaping and mentor override protocols ("Golden Handcuffs") to enforce safer agent behavior. Furthermore, alignment techniques are expanding to handle multi-objective scenarios with conflicting goals, as seen in offline RL algorithms using smooth scalarization for Pareto-optimal policy selection. This direction underscores the field's progression from ad-hoc safety patches toward principled, theoretically-grounded frameworks for guaranteed alignment.

1. $\pi$-Play: Multi-Agent Self-Play via Privileged Self-Distillation without External Data

👥 Authors: Yaocheng Zhang, Yuanheng Zhu, Wenyue Chong, Songjun Tu, Qichao Zhang, Jiajun Chai, Xiaohan Wang, Wei Lin, Guojun Yin, Dongbin Zhao

🏫 Affiliations: Unknown Institution

💡 Highlight: Proposes a multi-agent self-evolution framework where agents improve via privileged self-distillation from self-generated solution traces, strongly matching self-improving agents.
View Abstract

arXiv:2604.14054v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Deep search agents have emerged as a promising paradigm for addressing complex information-seeking tasks, but their training remains challenging due to sparse rewards, weak credit assignment, and limited labeled data. Self-play offers a scalable route to reduce data dependence, but conventional self-play optimizes students only through sparse outcome rewards, leading to low learning efficiency. In this work, we observe that self-play naturally produces a question construction path (QCP) during task generation, an intermediate artifact that captures the reverse solution process. This reveals a new source of privileged information for self-distillation: self-play can itself provide high-quality privileged context for the teacher model in a low-cost and scalable manner, without relying on human feedback or curated privileged information. Leveraging this insight, we propose Privileged Information Self-Play ($\pi$-Play), a multi-agent self-evolution framework. In $\pi$-Play, an examiner generates tasks together with their QCPs, and a teacher model leverages QCP as privileged context to densely supervise a student via self-distillation. This design transforms conventional sparse-reward self-play into a dense-feedback self-evolution loop. Extensive experiments show that data-free $\pi$-Play surpasses fully supervised search agents and improves evolutionary efficiency by 2-3$\times$ over conventional self-play.

View Abstract

arXiv:2604.14142v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: While reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards (RLVR) significantly enhances LLM reasoning by optimizing the conditional distribution P(y|x), its potential is fundamentally bounded by the base model's existing output distribution. Optimizing the marginal distribution P(y) in the Pre-train Space addresses this bottleneck by encoding reasoning ability and preserving broad exploration capacity. Yet, conventional pre-training relies on static corpora for passive learning, leading to a distribution shift that hinders targeted reasoning enhancement. In this paper, we introduce PreRL (Pre-train Space RL), which applies reward-driven online updates directly to P(y). We theoretically and empirically validate the strong gradient alignment between log P(y) and log P(y|x), establishing PreRL as a viable surrogate for standard RL. Furthermore, we uncover a critical mechanism: Negative Sample Reinforcement (NSR) within PreRL serves as an exceptionally effective driver for reasoning. NSR-PreRL rapidly prunes incorrect reasoning spaces while stimulating endogenous reflective behaviors, increasing transition and reflection thoughts by 14.89x and 6.54x, respectively. Leveraging these insights, we propose Dual Space RL (DSRL), a Policy Reincarnation strategy that initializes models with NSR-PreRL to expand the reasoning horizon before transitioning to standard RL for fine-grained optimization. Extensive experiments demonstrate that DSRL consistently outperforms strong baselines, proving that pre-train space pruning effectively steers the policy toward a refined correct reasoning subspace.

💡 Highlight: Survey paper that directly and centrally analyzes reward hacking in RLHF/RLAIF, providing a unifying framework for understanding this core vulnerability in RL for LLMs.
View Abstract

arXiv:2604.13602v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) and related alignment paradigms have become central to steering large language models (LLMs) and multimodal large language models (MLLMs) toward human-preferred behaviors. However, these approaches introduce a systemic vulnerability: reward hacking, where models exploit imperfections in learned reward signals to maximize proxy objectives without fulfilling true task intent. As models scale and optimization intensifies, such exploitation manifests as verbosity bias, sycophancy, hallucinated justification, benchmark overfitting, and, in multimodal settings, perception--reasoning decoupling and evaluator manipulation. Recent evidence further suggests that seemingly benign shortcut behaviors can generalize into broader forms of misalignment, including deception and strategic gaming of oversight mechanisms. In this survey, we propose the Proxy Compression Hypothesis (PCH) as a unifying framework for understanding reward hacking. We formalize reward hacking as an emergent consequence of optimizing expressive policies against compressed reward representations of high-dimensional human objectives. Under this view, reward hacking arises from the interaction of objective compression, optimization amplification, and evaluator--policy co-adaptation. This perspective unifies empirical phenomena across RLHF, RLAIF, and RLVR regimes, and explains how local shortcut learning can generalize into broader forms of misalignment, including deception and strategic manipulation of oversight mechanisms. We further organize detection and mitigation strategies according to how they intervene on compression, amplification, or co-adaptation dynamics. By framing reward hacking as a structural instability of proxy-based alignment under scale, we highlight open challenges in scalable oversight, multimodal grounding, and agentic autonomy.

View Abstract

arXiv:2604.13088v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: In sparse termination rewards, intra-group comparisons have become the dominant paradigm for fine-tuning reasoning models via reinforcement learning. However, long-term training often leads to issues like ineffective update accumulation (learning tax), solution probability drift, and entropy collapse. This paper presents a necessary condition for algorithm design from a token-level credit assignment perspective: to prevent reward-irrelevant drift, intra-group objectives must maintain gradient exchangeability across token updates, enabling gradient cancellation on weak-credit/high-frequency tokens. We show that two common mechanisms disrupting exchangeability make "non-cancellation" a structural norm. Based on this, we propose minimal intra-group transformations to restore or approximate the cancellation structure in the shared token space. Experimental results demonstrate that these transformations stabilize training, improve sample efficiency, and enhance final performance, validating the value of this design condition.

6. Unleashing Implicit Rewards: Prefix-Value Learning for Distribution-Level Optimization

ArXiv ID: 2604.13197 | Relevance Score: 8/10 | Novelty Score: 7/10

👥 Authors: Shiping Gao, Hongzhan Chen, Xiaojun Quan, Qifan Wang, Lifu Huang

🏫 Affiliations: Unknown Institution

💡 Highlight: Proposes IPVRM, a novel implicit prefix-value reward model for step-level credit assignment, and DistRL, a distribution-level RL method for improving LLM reasoning.
View Abstract

arXiv:2604.13197v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Process reward models (PRMs) provide fine-grained reward signals along the reasoning process, but training reliable PRMs often requires step annotations or heavy verification pipelines, making them expensive to scale and refresh during online RL. Implicit PRMs mitigate this cost by learning decomposable token- or step-level rewards from trajectory-level outcome labels. However, they suffer from a train-inference mismatch: training only constrains a sequence-level aggregate, whereas inference requires token-level scores to reflect local step quality. As a result, token-level credits are weakly identified and may fail to faithfully reflect which reasoning steps are actually correct. This unreliability undermines a key promise of implicit PRMs: scoring many candidate tokens. In practice, noisy per-token advantages may systematically reinforce incorrect continuations. We address this problem with a novel Implicit Prefix-Value Reward Model (IPVRM), which directly learns a prefix-conditioned value function estimating the probability of eventual correctness, and derives step signals via temporal-difference (TD) differences. IPVRM substantially improves step-verification F1 on ProcessBench. Building on these calibrated prefix values, we further propose Distribution-Level RL (DistRL), which computes TD advantages for both sampled tokens and high-probability candidate tokens, enabling dense counterfactual updates without additional rollouts. While DistRL offers limited gains when powered by miscalibrated implicit rewards, it consistently improves downstream reasoning once paired with IPVRM.

arXiv:2604.12627v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: RLVR improves reasoning in large language models, but its effectiveness is often limited by severe reward sparsity on hard problems. Recent hint-based RL methods mitigate sparsity by injecting partial solutions or abstract templates, yet they typically scale guidance by adding more tokens, which introduce redundancy, inconsistency, and extra training overhead. We propose \textbf{KnowRL} (Knowledge-Guided Reinforcement Learning), an RL training framework that treats hint design as a minimal-sufficient guidance problem. The model, curated training data, and code are publicly available at https://github.com/Hasuer/KnowRL.

arXiv:2604.14144v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Spatial reasoning over three-dimensional scenes is a core capability for embodied intelligence, yet continuous model improvement remains bottlenecked by the cost of geometric annotation. The self-evolving paradigm offers a promising path, but its reliance on model consensus to construct pseudo-labels causes training to reinforce rather than correct the model's own geometric errors. We identify a property unique to 3D spatial reasoning that circumvents this limitation: ground truth is a deterministic consequence of the underlying geometry, computable exactly from point clouds and camera poses without any model involvement. A task-adaptive scheduler endogenously concentrates training on the model's weakest categories, producing a dynamic curriculum without manual design. Experiments across nine benchmarks demonstrate that SpatialEvo achieves the highest average score at both 3B and 7B scales, with consistent gains on spatial reasoning benchmarks and no degradation on general visual understanding.

🏫 Affiliations: Unknown Institution

View Abstract

arXiv:2604.13552v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Large language models (LLMs) demonstrate strong reasoning capabilities, but their performance often degrades under distribution shift. Existing test-time adaptation (TTA) methods rely on gradient-based updates that require white-box access and need substantial overhead, while training-free alternatives are either static or depend on external guidance. In this paper, we propose Training-Free Test-Time Contrastive Learning TF-TTCL, a training-free adaptation framework that enables a frozen LLM to improve online by distilling supervision from its own inference experiences. Specifically, TF-TTCL implements a dynamic "Explore-Reflect-Steer" loop through three core modules: 1) Semantic Query Augmentation first diversifies problem views via multi-agent role-playing to generate different reasoning trajectories; 2) Contrastive Experience Distillation then captures the semantic gap between superior and inferior trajectories, distilling them into explicit textual rules; and 3) Contextual Rule Retrieval finally activates these stored rules during inference to dynamically steer the frozen LLM toward robust reasoning patterns while avoiding observed errors. Extensive experiments on closed-ended reasoning tasks and open-ended evaluation tasks demonstrate that TF-TTCL consistently outperforms strong zero-shot baselines and representative TTA methods under online evaluation. Code is available at https://github.com/KevinSCUTer/TF-TTCL.

This provides a more stable and accurate learning signal for our agents, leading to superior training performance that surpasses GRPO. Our experiments on the MMLongbench-Doc benchmark show that MM-Doc-R1 outperforms previous baselines by 10.4%. Furthermore, SPO demonstrates superior performance over GRPO, boosting results by 5.0% with Qwen3-8B and 6.1% with Qwen3-4B. These results highlight the effectiveness of our integrated framework and novel training algorithm in advancing the state-of-the-art for complex, long-document visual question answering.

Research Interest Criteria

Please evaluate papers using a strict filter. We only want a small set of strongly aligned papers, not a broad survey of adjacent work.

Global Selection Principle

  • Mark a paper as relevant only if one of the three topics below is a central contribution of the paper, not a side application, benchmark, or discussion point.
  • Be conservative by default: if the connection is indirect, partial, or ambiguous, treat it as not relevant or score it low.
  • Prefer papers that introduce new methods, training frameworks, reward designs, agent improvement loops, or latent reasoning architectures.
  • Do not favor papers just because they mention "LLM", "agent", "reasoning", "RL", "alignment", or "CoT" in a generic way.
  • Exclude broad foundation model papers, generic post-training papers, generic tool-use agents, application/system papers, domain-specific deployments, and pure benchmark papers unless their core method strongly matches one of the topics below.

1. Reinforcement Learning (RL) for LLMs

We care about RL methods that directly improve LLM behavior, alignment, or reasoning through explicit reward design, RL pipelines, verifier-driven optimization, or scalable preference/feedback mechanisms.

  • Strong match: RLHF/RLAIF variants, verifiable reward based RL, rejection sampling plus RL pipelines, reward model design, outcome/process reward methods, online RL for LLMs, and methods that materially improve helpfulness, honesty, harmlessness, or hallucination behavior.
  • Especially valuable: Papers that propose a new reward function, a new scalable RL training recipe, or a new data-generation / self-improvement loop for post-training LLMs.
  • Usually not relevant: Generic reinforcement learning papers, RL for robotics/control/games, prompt optimization without RL, supervised finetuning only, inference-time search without a training contribution, or alignment papers with no substantive RL component.

2. Agents for LLMs

We care specifically about self-evolving agents rather than static agent systems. The central question is whether the agent can improve itself over time through interaction, reflection, tool-use traces, self-generated supervision, or automated curriculum/data generation.

  • Strong match: Self-improving agent frameworks, reflection/self-critique loops that feed back into learning, trajectory-driven continual improvement, automatic tool-use policy improvement, environment interaction that produces new supervision, and long-horizon agent adaptation.
  • Especially valuable: Closed-loop systems showing measurable capability gain over time, reliable agent learning from interaction traces, or agent architectures that turn execution experience into better future performance.
  • Usually not relevant: Vanilla ReAct-style agents, workflow orchestration, multi-agent communication only, tool calling systems without learning, benchmark papers, memory systems without self-improvement, or application-focused agent papers.

3. Latent CoT/Reasoning

We care about methods that move reasoning away from verbose explicit text chains into compact latent computation while preserving or improving reasoning quality.

  • Strong match: Latent reasoning architectures, hidden-state reasoning, implicit CoT, recurrent depth / recurrent latent computation, pause or thinking tokens for internal computation, explicit-to-latent distillation, and methods that reduce reasoning token cost or KV-cache use while retaining accuracy.
  • Especially valuable: New architectures or objectives for latent reasoning, hidden-state alignment/distillation, compression of reasoning traces, or methods that materially improve reasoning efficiency on math and logic tasks.
  • Usually not relevant: Standard explicit CoT prompting, generic reasoning finetuning, test-time scaling without latent computation, reasoning benchmarks, or papers whose main contribution is evaluation rather than a latent reasoning mechanism.

Final Preference

If a paper is merely adjacent to these areas, do not select it. Favor precision over recall. It is better to miss a borderline paper than to include many weakly related ones.
