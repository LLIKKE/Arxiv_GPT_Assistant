💡 Daily Research Summary

### RL for LLMs The dominant theme today is the rapid maturation of Reinforcement Learning for Large Language Models (RL4LLMs), moving beyond simple outcome-based rewards to sophisticated, multi-faceted training paradigms. A significant cluster of papers focuses on enhancing process supervision and reward design. For instance, **GRPO-VPS**introduces verifiable process supervision via belief probing to improve reasoning accuracy, while**V-tableR1**proposes a critic-guided policy optimization with step-level feedback for multimodal reasoning.**Rethinking Reinforcement Fine-Tuning in LVLM**provides crucial theoretical grounding, establishing convergence and generalization bounds for RL fine-tuning with verifiable rewards. Furthermore, novel optimization strategies are emerging:**Near-Future Policy Optimization (NPO)**accelerates convergence by using a policy's own later checkpoint as an auxiliary trajectory source, and**Avoiding Overthinking and Underthinking**introduces a curriculum-aware budget scheduling framework. Critically, the field is also exploring how to shape LLM behavior through reward structure, as seen in**The Tool-Overuse Illusion**, which analyzes and mitigates tool overuse by aligning reward structures with epistemic boundaries. This wave of research demonstrates a clear shift toward more granular, theoretically sound, and behaviorally nuanced RL training recipes.

Agents & Self-Improving Systems

A parallel and deeply interconnected trend is the evolution of agents from static tools into self-improving systems that learn from their own trajectories. Several papers propose frameworks where agents meta-learn to optimize their own configurations.Learning to Evolveintroduces a meta-learning approach to optimize a textual parameter graph for multi-agent systems, whileWorkflowGengenerates adaptive workflows from trajectory experience in a closed-loop manner. The concept of "self-play" is being directly applied to agentic learning:Bootstrapping Post-training Signals for Open-ended Tasksuses rubric-based self-play on pretraining text to generate training signals, andSelf-Guided Self-Play (SGS)for theorem proving guides a model away from degeneracy. Practical applications are also prominent, withDR-Venusdemonstrating that frontier-grade deep research agents can be trained from only 10K open data points using agentic SFT and RL, andProMMSearchAgentusing a sim-to-real paradigm with process-oriented rewards. This convergence indicates that the next frontier for agent research is not just building capable systems, but creating the training methodologies that allow them to autonomously refine their skills, strategies, and even their own internal structures.

Multimodal Reasoning & Post-Training

The application of RL to multimodal models is rapidly expanding, with a strong focus on visual reasoning and grounding.SSL-R1breaks new ground by deriving verifiable rewards directly from images for self-supervised visual RL post-training, offering a scalable alternative to human annotation.Hybrid Latent Reasoning (HyLaR)addresses the challenge of reasoning in discrete-continuous action spaces, which is fundamental for embodied agents. For more specific domains,ToolsRLprovides a framework with tool-specific rewards for visual reasoning, andVideo-ToCintroduces a reasoning-demand reward mechanism for video understanding. Even specialized clinical applications are benefiting, asInfection-Reasoneruses GRPO post-training to refine evidence-grounded reasoning for wound classification. This body of work shows that the principles of RL4LLMs are being successfully adapted to the multimodal domain, creating powerful feedback loops that improve both visual perception and the chain-of-thought reasoning built upon it.

Reasoning & Alignment

Beyond general RL, there is a focused effort to refine the reasoning process itself, addressing persistent issues like logical consistency, overthinking, and alignment with user intent.Where Reasoning Breaksproposes a surgical RL objective that selectively optimizes token-level preferences at critical logical pivots, moving beyond whole-sequence optimization.HiPOintroduces hierarchical preference optimization for segment-level reasoning alignment, offering a more granular approach than standard DPO. For instruction following,Self-Guided Plan Extractionuses goal-conditional RL to jointly train planners and agents. The intersection of reasoning and tool use is also being explored:R2IFaligns reasoning with decisions via composite rewards for interpretable function calling, andJTPROiteratively co-optimizes instructions and tool schemas from rollout traces. Finally,Differentiable Conformal Training offers a novel approach to improving reasoning factuality by learning a scorer that optimizes for claim retention under reliability guarantees, a promising direction for open-ended generation. These papers collectively illustrate a move toward more controlled, logical, and faithful reasoning in LLMs, driven by finely-tuned RL signals.

💡 Highlight: Proposes HyLaR, a hybrid latent reasoning framework with decoupled policy optimization (DePO) for RL in discrete-continuous action spaces, directly addressing latent reasoning and RL for LLMs.
View Abstract

arXiv:2604.20328v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Chain-of-Thought (CoT) reasoning significantly elevates the complex problem-solving capabilities of multimodal large language models (MLLMs). However, adapting CoT to vision typically discretizes signals to fit LLM inputs, causing early semantic collapse and discarding fine-grained details. While external tools can mitigate this, they introduce a rigid bottleneck, confining reasoning to predefined operations. Although recent latent reasoning paradigms internalize visual states to overcome these limitations, optimizing the resulting hybrid discrete-continuous action space remains challenging. In this work, we propose HyLaR (Hybrid Latent Reasoning), a framework that seamlessly interleaves discrete text generation with continuous visual latent representations. Specifically, following an initial cold-start supervised fine-tuning (SFT), we introduce DePO (Decoupled Policy Optimization) to enable effective reinforcement learning within this hybrid space. DePO decomposes the policy gradient objective, applying independent trust-region constraints to the textual and latent components, alongside an exact closed-form von Mises-Fisher (vMF) KL regularizer. Extensive experiments demonstrate that HyLaR outperforms standard MLLMs and state-of-the-art latent reasoning approaches across fine-grained perception and general multimodal understanding benchmarks. Code is available at https://github.com/EthenCheng/HyLaR.

5. Rethinking Reinforcement Fine-Tuning in LVLM: Convergence, Reward Decomposition, and Generalization

ArXiv ID: 2604.19857 | Relevance Score: 8/10 | Novelty Score: 8/10

👥 Authors: Carter Adams, Rafael Oliveira, Gabriel Almeida, Sofia Torres

🏫 Affiliations: Unknown Institution

💡 Highlight: Provides theoretical foundations for RL fine-tuning with verifiable rewards in LVLMs, including convergence and generalization bounds, directly matching RL for LLMs.
View Abstract

arXiv:2604.19857v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Reinforcement fine-tuning with verifiable rewards (RLVR) has emerged as a powerful paradigm for equipping large vision-language models (LVLMs) with agentic capabilities such as tool use and multi-step reasoning. Despite striking empirical successes, most notably Visual Agentic Reinforcement Fine-Tuning (Visual-ARFT), the theoretical underpinnings of this paradigm remain poorly understood. In particular, two critical questions lack rigorous answers: (i)~how does the composite structure of verifiable rewards (format compliance, answer accuracy, tool executability) affect the convergence of Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO), and (ii)~why does training on a small set of tool-augmented tasks transfer to out-of-distribution domains? We address these gaps by introducing the \emph{Tool-Augmented Markov Decision Process} (TA-MDP), a formal framework that models multimodal agentic decision-making with bounded-depth tool calls. Within this framework, we establish three main results. First, we prove that GRPO under composite verifiable rewards converges to a first-order stationary point at rate $O(1/\sqrt{T})$ with explicit dependence on the number of reward components and group size (\textbf{Theorem~1}). Second, we derive a \emph{Reward Decomposition Theorem} that bounds the sub-optimality gap between decomposed per-component optimization and joint optimization, providing a precise characterization of when reward decomposition is beneficial (\textbf{Theorem~2}). Third, we establish a PAC-Bayes generalization bound for tool-augmented policies that explains the strong out-of-distribution transfer observed in Visual-ARFT (\textbf{Theorem~3}).

6. Scaling Self-Play with Self-Guidance

👥 Authors: Luke Bailey, Kaiyue Wen, Kefan Dong, Tatsunori Hashimoto, Tengyu Ma

🏫 Affiliations: Unknown Institution

💡 Highlight: Introduces Self-Guided Self-Play (SGS), a novel self-play algorithm where a language model guides the Conjecturer away from degeneracy, enabling scalable learning for formal theorem proving.
View Abstract

arXiv:2604.20209v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: LLM self-play algorithms are notable in that, in principle, nothing bounds their learning: a Conjecturer model creates problems for a Solver, and both improve together. However, in practice, existing LLM self-play methods do not scale well with large amounts of compute, instead hitting learning plateaus. We argue this is because over long training runs, the Conjecturer learns to hack its reward, collapsing to artificially complex problems that do not help the Solver improve. To overcome this, we introduce Self-Guided Self-Play (SGS), a self-play algorithm in which the language model itself guides the Conjecturer away from degeneracy. In SGS, the model takes on three roles: Solver, Conjecturer, and a Guide that scores synthetic problems by their relevance to unsolved target problems and how clean and natural they are, providing supervision against Conjecturer collapse. Our core hypothesis is that language models can assess whether a subproblem is useful for achieving a goal. We evaluate the scaling properties of SGS by running training for significantly longer than prior works and by fitting scaling laws to cumulative solve rate curves. Applying SGS to formal theorem proving in Lean4, we find that it surpasses the asymptotic solve rate of our strongest RL baseline in fewer than 80 rounds of self-play and enables a 7B parameter model, after 200 rounds of self-play, to solve more problems than a 671B parameter model pass@4.

View Abstract

arXiv:2604.20659v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR) has advanced the reasoning capabilities of Large Language Models (LLMs) by leveraging direct outcome verification instead of learned reward models. Building on this paradigm, Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) eliminates the need for critic models but suffers from indiscriminate credit assignment for intermediate steps, which limits its ability to identify effective reasoning strategies and incurs overthinking. In this work, we introduce a model-free and verifiable process supervision via probing the model's belief in the correct answer throughout its reasoning trajectory. By segmenting the generation into discrete steps and tracking the conditional probability of the correct answer appended at each segment boundary, we efficiently compute interpretable segment-wise progress measurements to refine GRPO's trajectory-level feedback. This approach enables more targeted and sample-efficient policy updates, while avoiding the need for intermediate supervision derived from costly Monte Carlo rollouts or auxiliary models. Experiments on mathematical and general-domain benchmarks show consistent gains over GRPO across diverse models: up to 2.6-point accuracy improvements and 13.7% reasoning-length reductions on math tasks, and up to 2.4 points and 4% on general-domain tasks, demonstrating strong generalization.

View Abstract

arXiv:2604.19780v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Scaling test-time compute via extended reasoning has become a key paradigm for improving the capabilities of large language models (LLMs). However, existing approaches optimize reasoning under fixed or uniformly sampled token budgets, ignoring the fundamental mismatch between problem difficulty and allocated compute. This leads to overthinking on easy problems and underthinking on hard ones, resulting in suboptimal token efficiency across diverse reasoning scenarios. In this paper, we propose Budget-Adaptive Curriculum Reasoning (BCAE), a unified framework that jointly optimizes reasoning quality and token efficiency through three synergistic components: (1) a \emph{budget-conditioned unified policy} that embeds the token budget as a continuous conditioning signal, eliminating the need for decoupled thinking and summarization strategies; (2) a \emph{curriculum-aware budget scheduler} that adaptively shifts the training budget distribution from easy to hard problems based on real-time learning progress; and (3) a \emph{truncation-aware dense reward} mechanism that provides fine-grained credit assignment at intermediate reasoning steps via process-level verification. We further introduce \emph{Budget-Conditioned Advantage Estimation} (BCAE), a novel variance reduction technique that conditions the advantage baseline on the sampled budget, yielding more stable policy gradients. Experiments on mathematical reasoning benchmarks (MATH, GSM8K, AIME, and Minerva Math) demonstrate that BACR consistently outperforms other strong baselines across all token budgets, achieving up to 8.3\% accuracy improvement under tight budgets while reducing average token consumption by 34\% compared to unconstrained reasoning.

We propose HiPO (Hierarchical Preference Optimization), an extension of DPO that separates responses into reasoning segments (query clarification and context, reasoning steps, and answer) and computes loss as a weighted sum of the DPO loss for each segment. Our approach enables segment-specific training while maintaining DPO's computational efficiency and training stability. We demonstrate that for multiple 7B LLMs fine-tuned using HiPO and DPO on the Math Stack Exchange preference dataset, the models trained with HiPO outperform the others on a variety of common math benchmarks and achieve greater organization, logical flow, and consistency as measured by GPT-4.1.

Global Selection Principle

  • Mark a paper as relevant only if one of the three topics below is a central contribution of the paper, not a side application, benchmark, or discussion point.

  • Do not favor papers just because they mention "LLM", "agent", "reasoning", "RL", "alignment", or "CoT" in a generic way.
  • Exclude broad foundation model papers, generic post-training papers, generic tool-use agents, application/system papers, domain-specific deployments, and pure benchmark papers unless their core method strongly matches one of the topics below.

1. Reinforcement Learning (RL) for LLMs

We care about RL methods that directly improve LLM behavior, alignment, or reasoning through explicit reward design, RL pipelines, verifier-driven optimization, or scalable preference/feedback mechanisms.

  • Strong match: RLHF/RLAIF variants, verifiable reward based RL, rejection sampling plus RL pipelines, reward model design, outcome/process reward methods, online RL for LLMs, and methods that materially improve helpfulness, honesty, harmlessness, or hallucination behavior.
  • Especially valuable: Papers that propose a new reward function, a new scalable RL training recipe, or a new data-generation / self-improvement loop for post-training LLMs.
  • Usually not relevant: Generic reinforcement learning papers, RL for robotics/control/games, prompt optimization without RL, supervised finetuning only, inference-time search without a training contribution, or alignment papers with no substantive RL component.

2. Agents for LLMs

We care specifically about self-evolving agents rather than static agent systems. The central question is whether the agent can improve itself over time through interaction, reflection, tool-use traces, self-generated supervision, or automated curriculum/data generation.

  • Strong match: Self-improving agent frameworks, reflection/self-critique loops that feed back into learning, trajectory-driven continual improvement, automatic tool-use policy improvement, environment interaction that produces new supervision, and long-horizon agent adaptation.
  • Especially valuable: Closed-loop systems showing measurable capability gain over time, reliable agent learning from interaction traces, or agent architectures that turn execution experience into better future performance.
  • Usually not relevant: Vanilla ReAct-style agents, workflow orchestration, multi-agent communication only, tool calling systems without learning, benchmark papers, memory systems without self-improvement, or application-focused agent papers.

3. Latent CoT/Reasoning

We care about methods that move reasoning away from verbose explicit text chains into compact latent computation while preserving or improving reasoning quality.

  • Strong match: Latent reasoning architectures, hidden-state reasoning, implicit CoT, recurrent depth / recurrent latent computation, pause or thinking tokens for internal computation, explicit-to-latent distillation, and methods that reduce reasoning token cost or KV-cache use while retaining accuracy.
  • Especially valuable: New architectures or objectives for latent reasoning, hidden-state alignment/distillation, compression of reasoning traces, or methods that materially improve reasoning efficiency on math and logic tasks.
  • Usually not relevant: Standard explicit CoT prompting, generic reasoning finetuning, test-time scaling without latent computation, reasoning benchmarks, or papers whose main contribution is evaluation rather than a latent reasoning mechanism.
