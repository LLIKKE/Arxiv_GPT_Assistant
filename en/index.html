💡 Daily Research Summary

### RL for LLMs & Policy Optimization

Today's research demonstrates a concerted push to refine and stabilize the core mechanisms of Reinforcement Learning for Large Language Models, moving beyond simple reward maximization. A central theme is addressing the fundamental challenge of sparse and delayed rewards in reasoning tasks. Novel frameworks like KnowRL and Token-Level Policy Optimization (TEPO) introduce mechanisms—such as minimal-sufficient knowledge guidance and token-level reward aggregation—to provide denser, more informative learning signals. Concurrently, there is a strong focus on ensuring these optimization processes yield well-calibrated and robust models, as seen in Calibration-Aware Policy Optimization (CAPO) and work on mitigating reward hacking through robust optimization against correlated proxies. The systematic investigation into On-Policy Distillation (OPD) dynamics further underscores this maturation, providing recipes to recover from failing distillation and enforce teacher consistency, as exemplified by Lightning OPD.

Self-Evolving & Agentic Systems

The frontier of AI agents is rapidly advancing toward systems capable of genuine, long-term self-improvement and autonomous operation. A key innovation is the integration of sophisticated memory architectures that enable agents to learn from experience and distill successful strategies into reusable knowledge. Frameworks like MolMem and Thought-Retriever explicitly build persistent, evolving memory systems—be it for molecular optimization or general agentic tasks—that grow more capable through interaction. This is complemented by research on autonomous systems for long-horizon challenges, such as the AiScientist for ML research engineering and HypoExplore for neural architecture discovery, which feature hierarchical orchestration and self-improving loops of hypothesis generation and evaluation. The benchmark Frontier-Eng further solidifies this direction by providing a testbed for generative optimization in real-world engineering tasks.

Latent Reasoning & Model Architecture

Significant progress is being made in understanding and architecting the internal computational processes of LLMs, particularly for implicit reasoning and planning. A major thrust involves designing models with latent, recurrent, or controllable internal states to improve efficiency and emergent capability. Proposals like Parcae's stable looped architecture and EVE's variational model with agentic internal control aim to enable more powerful and efficient latent computation. Theoretical and mechanistic studies provide a foundation for this, with analysis showing how Chain-of-Thought supervision reduces sample complexity and evidence that latent planning representations naturally emerge with model scale. The exploration of how multi-token prediction induces robust reasoning circuits further deepens our mechanistic understanding of how these capabilities are learned and represented within transformer architectures.

Multimodal & Specialized Agent Alignment

Research on aligning AI systems is expanding into complex, multimodal domains, requiring novel reward design and optimization techniques that move beyond simple human preferences. A clear trend is the development of annotation-free or self-supervised reward mechanisms that are scalable and fine-grained. PromptEcho constructs rewards for text-to-image models directly from vision-language models, while Visual Preference Optimization (rDPO) leverages instance-specific rubric rewards. For agents operating in structured environments like long documents or the web, methods such as Evidence-aware Group Relative Policy Optimization (DocSeeker) and progressive curricula using ORPO/GRPO (From Imitation to Discrimination) are being developed to jointly optimize for task success and robust, evidence-grounded behavior. The work on Cycle-Consistent Search also exemplifies this trend, creating proxy rewards from question reconstructability to train search agents without gold supervision.

Decentralized & Cross-Lingual Adaptation

Finally, today's papers address practical challenges in deploying and adapting advanced LLM capabilities across diverse, real-world constraints. One line of work tackles the problem of aligning models across decentralized, private data silos, as seen in PubSwap's federated RLVR framework that coordinates via public data. Another addresses the critical need for linguistic inclusivity in reasoning, with ReasonXL demonstrating a pipeline using SFT and RL with verifiable rewards to successfully shift an LLM's reasoning language without sacrificing performance. These efforts highlight the growing focus on making state-of-the-art RL and reasoning techniques operable and equitable in varied deployment scenarios.

1. KnowRL: Boosting LLM Reasoning via Reinforcement Learning with Minimal-Sufficient Knowledge Guidance

ArXiv ID: 2604.12627 | Relevance Score: 9/10 | Novelty Score: 8/10

👥 Authors: Linhao Yu, Tianmeng Yang, Siyu Ding, Renren Jin, Naibin Gu, Xiangzhao Hao, Shuaiyi Nie, Deyi Xiong, Weichong Yin, Yu Sun, Hua Wu

🏫 Affiliations: Unknown Institution

💡 Highlight: Introduces KnowRL, a reinforcement learning framework for LLMs that uses minimal-sufficient knowledge guidance to address reward sparsity in reasoning tasks.
View Abstract

arXiv:2604.12627v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: RLVR improves reasoning in large language models, but its effectiveness is often limited by severe reward sparsity on hard problems. Recent hint-based RL methods mitigate sparsity by injecting partial solutions or abstract templates, yet they typically scale guidance by adding more tokens, which introduce redundancy, inconsistency, and extra training overhead. We propose \textbf{KnowRL} (Knowledge-Guided Reinforcement Learning), an RL training framework that treats hint design as a minimal-sufficient guidance problem. During RL training, KnowRL decomposes guidance into atomic knowledge points (KPs) and uses Constrained Subset Search (CSS) to construct compact, interaction-aware subsets for training. We further identify a pruning interaction paradox -- removing one KP may help while removing multiple such KPs can hurt -- and explicitly optimize for robust subset curation under this dependency structure. We train KnowRL-Nemotron-1.5B from OpenMath-Nemotron-1.5B. Across eight reasoning benchmarks at the 1.5B scale, KnowRL-Nemotron-1.5B consistently outperforms strong RL and hinting baselines. Without KP hints at inference, KnowRL-Nemotron-1.5B reaches 70.08 average accuracy, already surpassing Nemotron-1.5B by +9.63 points; with selected KPs, performance improves to 74.16, establishing a new state of the art at this scale. The model, curated training data, and code are publicly available at https://github.com/Hasuer/KnowRL.

2. Lightning OPD: Efficient Post-Training for Large Reasoning Models with Offline On-Policy Distillation

ArXiv ID: 2604.13010 | Relevance Score: 8/10 | Novelty Score: 7/10

👥 Authors: Yecheng Wu, Song Han, Hai Cai

🏫 Affiliations: Unknown Institution

💡 Highlight: Proposes Lightning OPD, an efficient offline on-policy distillation framework for LLM post-training that enforces teacher consistency to match standard OPD performance.
View Abstract

arXiv:2604.13010v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: On-policy distillation (OPD) has emerged as an efficient post-training paradigm for large language models. However, standard OPD requires a live teacher inference server throughout training, resulting in substantial infrastructure overhead. In this work, we investigate whether on-policy distillation can be performed offline. A natural approach is to precompute teacher log-probabilities once over SFT rollouts and reuse them during training. In practice, however, this offline variant fails to reliably match the performance of standard OPD. To understand this discrepancy, we identify a previously overlooked condition that is critical for any OPD pipeline, which we term teacher consistency. This condition requires that the same teacher model be used for both supervised fine-tuning and OPD. We show that violating teacher consistency introduces an irreducible gradient bias, causing both offline and online OPD to converge to a suboptimal fixed point regardless of training duration. Building on this insight, we propose Lightning OPD, an offline on-policy distillation framework that enforces teacher consistency by precomputing teacher log-probabilities over SFT rollouts. This design eliminates the need for a live teacher server entirely. We further show that, under teacher consistency, Lightning OPD shares the same optimum as standard OPD, with bounded gradient discrepancy and an implicit regularization effect that helps prevent policy drift. Extensive experiments on mathematical reasoning and code generation demonstrate that Lightning OPD achieves state-of-the-art performance with significantly improved efficiency. Starting from an SFT-initialized Qwen3-8B-Base model, Lightning OPD reaches 69.9% on AIME 2024 in just 30 GPU hours, achieving a 4.0x speedup over standard OPD and substantially lowering the barrier to entry for academic research on LLM post-training.

3. Calibration-Aware Policy Optimization for Reasoning LLMs

ArXiv ID: 2604.12632 | Relevance Score: 8/10 | Novelty Score: 7/10

👥 Authors: Ziqi Wang, Xingzhou Lou, Meiqi Wu, Zhengqi Wen, Junge Zhang

🏫 Affiliations: Unknown Institution

💡 Highlight: Proposes a new RL-based policy optimization method (CAPO) for LLMs that jointly improves reasoning accuracy and calibration, directly addressing RL for LLMs.
View Abstract

arXiv:2604.12632v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) enhances LLM reasoning but often induces overconfidence, where incorrect responses yield lower perplexity than correct ones, degrading relative calibration as described by the Area Under the Curve (AUC). Existing approaches either yield limited improvements in calibration or sacrifice gains in reasoning accuracy. We first prove that this degradation in GRPO-style algorithms stems from their uncertainty-agnostic advantage estimation, which inevitably misaligns optimization gradients with calibration. This leads to improved accuracy at the expense of degraded calibration. We then propose Calibration-Aware Policy Optimization (CAPO). It adopts a logistic AUC surrogate loss that is theoretically consistent and admits regret bound, enabling uncertainty-aware advantage estimation. By further incorporating a noise masking mechanism, CAPO achieves stable learning dynamics that jointly optimize calibration and accuracy. Experiments on multiple mathematical reasoning benchmarks show that CAPO-1.5B significantly improves calibration by up to 15% while achieving accuracy comparable to or better than GRPO, and further boosts accuracy on downstream inference-time scaling tasks by up to 5%. Moreover, when allowed to abstain under low-confidence conditions, CAPO achieves a Pareto-optimal precision-coverage trade-off, highlighting its practical value for hallucination mitigation.

arXiv:2604.12500v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Specification gaming under Reinforcement Learning (RL) is known to cause LLMs to develop sycophantic, manipulative, or deceptive behavior, yet the conditions under which this occurs remain unclear. We train 11 instruction-tuned LLMs (0.5B--14B) with on-policy RL across 3 environments and find that model size acts as a safety buffer in some environments but enables greater harmful exploitation in others. 5. Self-Distillation Zero: Self-Revision Turns Binary Rewards into Dense Supervision

ArXiv ID: 2604.12002 | Relevance Score: 8/10 | Novelty Score: 7/10

👥 Authors: Yinghui He, Simran Kaur, Adithya Bhaskar, Yongjin Yang, Jiarui Liu, Narutatsu Ri, Liam Fowl, Abhishek Panigrahi, Danqi Chen, Sanjeev Arora

🏫 Affiliations: Unknown Institution

💡 Highlight: Proposes a novel self-distillation method that uses binary rewards to generate dense token-level supervision for LLM self-improvement, aligning with RL for LLMs and self-evolving agents.
View Abstract

arXiv:2604.12002v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Current post-training methods in verifiable settings fall into two categories. Reinforcement learning (RLVR) relies on binary rewards, which are broadly applicable and powerful, but provide only sparse supervision during training. Distillation provides dense token-level supervision, typically obtained from an external teacher or using high-quality demonstrations. Collecting such supervision can be costly or unavailable. We propose Self-Distillation Zero (SD-Zero), a method that is substantially more training sample-efficient than RL and does not require an external teacher or high-quality demonstrations. SD-Zero trains a single model to play two roles: a Generator, which produces an initial response, and a Reviser, which conditions on that response and its binary reward to produce an improved response. We then perform on-policy self-distillation to distill the reviser into the generator, using the reviser's token distributions conditioned on the generator's response and its reward as supervision. In effect, SD-Zero trains the model to transform binary rewards into dense token-level self-supervision. On math and code reasoning benchmarks with Qwen3-4B-Instruct and Olmo-3-7B-Instruct, SD-Zero improves performance by at least 10% over the base models and outperforms strong baselines, including Rejection Fine-Tuning (RFT), GRPO, and Self-Distillation Fine-Tuning (SDFT), under the same question set and training sample budget. Extensive ablation studies show two novel characteristics of our proposed algorithm: (a) token-level self-localization, where the reviser can identify the key tokens that need to be revised in the generator's response based on reward, and (b) iterative self-evolution, where the improving ability to revise answers can be distilled back into generation performance with regular teacher synchronization.

💡 Highlight: Proposes TEPO, a novel token-level policy optimization framework for RL in LLMs that addresses sparse-reward challenges in CoT reasoning.
View Abstract

arXiv:2604.12736v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) has significantly advanced the reasoning ability of large language models (LLMs), particularly in their mathemat ical reasoning performance. However, GRPO and related entropy regularization methods still struggle with token-level sparse-rewards, which is an inherent chal lenge in chain-of-thought (CoT) reasoning. These approaches often rely on undifferen tiated token-level entropy regularization, which easily leads to entropy collapse or model degradation under sparse token rewards. In this work, we propose TEPO, a novel token-level framework that (1) leverages sequence-level likelihood to link group-level rewards with individual tokens via token-level aggregation, and (2) introduces a token-level KL-Divergence mask constraint that targets tokens with positive advantages and decreasing entropy to mitigate abrupt policy updates. 🏫 Affiliations: REAL-Lab-NU

💡 Highlight: Introduces MolMem, a multi-turn agentic RL framework with a dual-memory system for molecular optimization, explicitly focusing on self-improving agents that distill successful trajectories into reusable strategies.
View Abstract

arXiv:2604.12237v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: In drug discovery, molecular optimization aims to iteratively refine a lead compound to improve molecular properties while preserving structural similarity to the original molecule. However, each oracle evaluation is expensive, making sample efficiency a key challenge for existing methods under a limited oracle budget. Trial-and-error approaches require many oracle calls, while methods that leverage external knowledge tend to reuse familiar templates and struggle on challenging objectives. A key missing piece is long-term memory that can ground decisions and provide reusable insights for future optimizations. To address this, we present MolMem (\textbf{Mol}ecular optimization with \textbf{Mem}ory), a multi-turn agentic reinforcement learning (RL) framework with a dual-memory system. Specifically, MolMem uses Static Exemplar Memory to retrieve relevant exemplars for cold-start grounding, and Evolving Skill Memory to distill successful trajectories into reusable strategies. Built on this memory-augmented formulation, we train the policy with dense step-wise rewards, turning costly rollouts into long-term knowledge that improves future optimization. Extensive experiments show that MolMem achieves 90\% success on single-property tasks (1.5$\times$ over the best baseline) and 52\% on multi-property tasks using only 500 oracle calls. Our code is available at https://github.com/REAL-Lab-NU/MolMem.

arXiv:2604.12513v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: We study whether a variational language model can support a minimal and measurable form of agentic control grounded in its own internal evidence. 10. Parcae: Scaling Laws For Stable Looped Language Models

ArXiv ID: 2604.12946 | Relevance Score: 7/10 | Novelty Score: 8/10

👥 Authors: Hayden Prairie, Zachary Novack, Taylor Berg-Kirkpatrick, Daniel Y. Fu

🏫 Affiliations: Unknown Institution

💡 Highlight: Proposes Parcae, a stable looped architecture for LLMs that enables latent recurrent computation, directly addressing latent reasoning efficiency.
View Abstract

arXiv:2604.12946v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Traditional fixed-depth architectures scale quality by increasing training FLOPs, typically through increased parameterization, at the expense of a higher memory footprint, or data. A potential alternative is looped architectures, which instead increase FLOPs by sending activations through a block of layers in a loop. While promising, existing recipes for training looped architectures can be unstable, suffering from residual explosion and loss spikes. We address these challenges by recasting looping as a nonlinear time-variant dynamical system over the residual stream. Via a linear approximation to this system, we find that instability occurs in existing looped architectures as a result of large spectral norms in their injection parameters. To address these instability issues, we propose Parcae, a novel stable, looped architecture that constrains the spectral norm of the injection parameters via discretization of a negative diagonal parameterization. As a result, Parcae achieves up to 6.3% lower validation perplexity over prior large-scale looped models. Using our stable looped architecture, we investigate the scaling properties of looping as a medium to improve quality by increasing FLOPs in training and test-time. For training, we derive predictable power laws to scale FLOPs while keeping parameter count fixed. Our initial scaling laws suggest that looping and data should be increased in tandem, given a fixed FLOP budget. At test-time, we find that Parcae can use looping to scale compute, following a predictable, saturating exponential decay. When scaled up to 1.3B parameters, we find that Parcae improves CORE and Core-Extended quality by 2.99 and 1.18 points when compared to strong Transformer baselines under a fixed parameter and data budget, achieving a relative quality of up to 87.5% a Transformer twice the size.

We train the approach using group relative policy optimization with a multi-component reward function that jointly optimizes edit-level semantic similarity, fluency, and pattern conformity as well as argument-level appropriateness. In automatic and human evaluation, it outperforms competitive baselines and the state of the art in human-like editing, with multi-round editing achieving appropriateness close to full rewriting.

Research Interest Criteria

Please evaluate papers using a strict filter. We only want a small set of strongly aligned papers, not a broad survey of adjacent work.

Global Selection Principle

  • Mark a paper as relevant only if one of the three topics below is a central contribution of the paper, not a side application, benchmark, or discussion point.
  • Be conservative by default: if the connection is indirect, partial, or ambiguous, treat it as not relevant or score it low.
  • Prefer papers that introduce new methods, training frameworks, reward designs, agent improvement loops, or latent reasoning architectures.
  • Do not favor papers just because they mention "LLM", "agent", "reasoning", "RL", "alignment", or "CoT" in a generic way.
  • Exclude broad foundation model papers, generic post-training papers, generic tool-use agents, application/system papers, domain-specific deployments, and pure benchmark papers unless their core method strongly matches one of the topics below.

1. Reinforcement Learning (RL) for LLMs

We care about RL methods that directly improve LLM behavior, alignment, or reasoning through explicit reward design, RL pipelines, verifier-driven optimization, or scalable preference/feedback mechanisms.

  • Strong match: RLHF/RLAIF variants, verifiable reward based RL, rejection sampling plus RL pipelines, reward model design, outcome/process reward methods, online RL for LLMs, and methods that materially improve helpfulness, honesty, harmlessness, or hallucination behavior.
  • Especially valuable: Papers that propose a new reward function, a new scalable RL training recipe, or a new data-generation / self-improvement loop for post-training LLMs.
  • Usually not relevant: Generic reinforcement learning papers, RL for robotics/control/games, prompt optimization without RL, supervised finetuning only, inference-time search without a training contribution, or alignment papers with no substantive RL component.

2. Agents for LLMs

We care specifically about self-evolving agents rather than static agent systems. The central question is whether the agent can improve itself over time through interaction, reflection, tool-use traces, self-generated supervision, or automated curriculum/data generation.

  • Strong match: Self-improving agent frameworks, reflection/self-critique loops that feed back into learning, trajectory-driven continual improvement, automatic tool-use policy improvement, environment interaction that produces new supervision, and long-horizon agent adaptation.
  • Especially valuable: Closed-loop systems showing measurable capability gain over time, reliable agent learning from interaction traces, or agent architectures that turn execution experience into better future performance.
  • Usually not relevant: Vanilla ReAct-style agents, workflow orchestration, multi-agent communication only, tool calling systems without learning, benchmark papers, memory systems without self-improvement, or application-focused agent papers.

3. Latent CoT/Reasoning

We care about methods that move reasoning away from verbose explicit text chains into compact latent computation while preserving or improving reasoning quality.

  • Strong match: Latent reasoning architectures, hidden-state reasoning, implicit CoT, recurrent depth / recurrent latent computation, pause or thinking tokens for internal computation, explicit-to-latent distillation, and methods that reduce reasoning token cost or KV-cache use while retaining accuracy.
  • Especially valuable: New architectures or objectives for latent reasoning, hidden-state alignment/distillation, compression of reasoning traces, or methods that materially improve reasoning efficiency on math and logic tasks.
  • Usually not relevant: Standard explicit CoT prompting, generic reasoning finetuning, test-time scaling without latent computation, reasoning benchmarks, or papers whose main contribution is evaluation rather than a latent reasoning mechanism.

Final Preference

If a paper is merely adjacent to these areas, do not select it. Favor precision over recall. It is better to miss a borderline paper than to include many weakly related ones.
