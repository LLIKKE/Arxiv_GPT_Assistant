💡 Daily Research Summary

### RL for LLMs and Reward Design The field is experiencing a profound shift toward more principled and mathematically grounded reinforcement learning frameworks. A central theme is the re-examination of KL-regularized RL and its closed-form solutions, with the discovery that power distributions naturally bridge sampling, self-reward, and self-distillation. This theoretical insight is complemented by practical advances addressing core limitations of popular methods like GRPO, including the identification and correction of aggregation bias through balanced aggregation, and the introduction of entropy-progress aligned modulation (EP-GRPO) to fix credit assignment failures. Novel algorithmic variants such as Adaptive Power-Mean Policy Optimization (APMPO) and Free Energy-Driven RL (FREIA) are pushing the boundaries of reasoning performance, while uncertainty-aware exploration methods (UE-DPO) and data-dependent exploration bonuses are improving sample efficiency and robustness. A significant sub-trend is the move beyond simplistic binary or scalar rewards toward richer supervision signals—including online natural language feedback, trajectory-aware process rewards (DTW), and executor-grounded rewards—allowing for more nuanced alignment and improved reasoning trace quality.

Self-Improving Agents and Autonomous Learning

A major breakthrough is the emergence of fully autonomous, self-evolving systems that require minimal to no human annotation. The concept of "self-induced outcome potential" (SIOP) enables turn-level credit assignment for agents without task-specific verifiers by leveraging semantic clustering of final answers as latent outcome states. This is complemented by frameworks like EvoLM, where models generate discriminative rubrics as self-reward signals, and RemoteZero, which uses intrinsic semantic verification for geospatial reasoning. The integration of agentic RL with novel algorithms like fatal-aware GRPO (OpenSearch-VL) is creating fully open-source recipes for training multimodal search agents that can self-improve through interaction. Crucially, the field is also addressing safety through analogical reasoning and controlled benchmark rewriting (ROME/ARISE), while demonstrating that smaller, specialized models (Terminus-4B) can rival frontier LLMs on agentic execution tasks when properly trained with rubric-based RL.

Alignment, Safety, and Preference Optimization

The alignment landscape is being reshaped by a deeper understanding of iterative RLHF dynamics, particularly the identification and prevention of "alignment collapse" through foresighted policy optimization (FPO). Researchers are moving beyond simple KL matching with reward-regularized self-distillation (Preference-Based Self-Distillation) and Bayesian list-wise alignment (BLADE) for recommendation. A critical new direction is the systematic evaluation of reward models for socially undesirable preferences, revealing that misalignment can be inadvertently reinforced by the reward signal itself. For multimodal systems, novel DPO variants (UE-DPO) are correcting visual hallucinations through token-level uncertainty, while game-theoretic approaches (Diff.-NPO) are aligning diffusion models at Nash equilibrium. The field is also tackling federated alignment of heterogeneous vision-language models through Mixture-of-Rewards (MoR), enabling scalable preference alignment without parameter sharing.

Reasoning, Verification, and Process Supervision

There is a clear trend toward moving beyond final-answer correctness to reward the reasoning process itself. Step-level credit assignment is being applied to tool-integrated tasks (FineStep for Text-to-SQL) and medical VQA (trajectory-aware process supervision), while frameworks like TraceLift use executor-grounded rewards to explicitly improve reasoning trace quality. For automated theorem proving, neuro-symbolic approaches combining the Wilf-Zeilberger method with LLM-based proving and DAPO-based RL refinement loops are achieving automated formal proofs. Validity-calibrated reasoning distillation dynamically modulates supervision based on local reasoning quality, moving beyond rigid trajectory imitation. The rollout pass-rate control method (Prefix Sampling) is steering binary-reward RL toward its most informative regime, achieving significant wall-clock speedups on complex benchmarks like SWE-bench.

Exploration, Curiosity, and Intrinsic Motivation

A vibrant sub-field is emerging around intrinsic motivation and curiosity-driven exploration for LLMs and VLMs. The GLANCE framework drives exploration in VLM agents by using the discrepancy between linguistic prediction and visual reality as an intrinsic reward, while data-dependent exploration methods for online RLHF construct uncertainty bonuses from historical data. This is complemented by free-energy-driven RL (FREIA) with adaptive advantage shaping for unsupervised reasoning, and curiosity-driven exploration via visual-linguistic discrepancy. These approaches are enabling agents to actively seek informative experiences rather than passively consuming training data, representing a fundamental shift toward more autonomous and sample-efficient learning paradigms.

1. Power Distribution Bridges Sampling, Self-Reward RL, and Self-Distillation

ArXiv ID: 2605.04542 | Relevance Score: 8/10 | Novelty Score: 8/10

👥 Authors: Akiyoshi Tomihari, Issei Sato

🏫 Affiliations: Unknown Institution

💡 Highlight: Bridges power sampling, self-reward KL-regularized RL, and self-distillation, showing the power distribution is the closed-form optimizer of KL-regularized RL with sequence-level log-probabilities as reward.
View Abstract

arXiv:2605.04542v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Recent analyses question whether reinforcement learning (RL) is responsible for strong reasoning in large language models (LLMs). At the same time, distillation and inference-time sampling, including power sampling, have emerged as effective ways to improve LLM performance. However, the relationship among RL, distillation, and sampling remains unclear. In this study, we focus on the power distribution, the target distribution of power sampling, and show that the power distribution bridges sampling, self-reward KL-regularized RL, and self-distillation. From the sampling perspective, we show that inexpensive local approximations cannot reproduce sequence-level power without information about possible suffixes. From the RL perspective, the power distribution is the closed-form optimizer of KL-regularized RL when the model's sequence-level log-probabilities are used as the reward. This identification leads to power self-distillation, an offline distillation surrogate that shares the same target distribution and amortizes the cost of power sampling into supervised training on teacher samples. We further show that power self-distillation can achieve self-reward sharpening, while improvement in a downstream true reward is governed by the covariance between true reward and self-reward under the power distribution. Experiments on reasoning tasks support our analysis: power sampling raises self-reward, true-reward gains depend on alignment with self-reward, and power self-distillation can match or exceed the performance of power sampling at much lower inference cost.

3. Self-Induced Outcome Potential: Turn-Level Credit Assignment for Agents without Verifiers

ArXiv ID: 2605.04984 | Relevance Score: 8/10 | Novelty Score: 8/10

👥 Authors: Senkang Hu, Yong Dai, Xudong Han, Zhengru Fang, Yuzhi Zhao, Sam Tak Wu Kwong, Yuguang Fang

🏫 Affiliations: Unknown Institution

💡 Highlight: Proposes SIOP, a novel turn-level credit assignment method for LLM agents that uses semantic clustering of final answers as latent outcome states, enabling RL without task-specific verifiers.
View Abstract

arXiv:2605.04984v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Long-horizon LLM agents depend on intermediate information-gathering turns, yet training feedback is usually observed only at the final answer, because process-level rewards require high-quality human annotation. Existing turn-level shaping methods reward turns that increase the likelihood of a gold answer, but they require answer supervision or stable task-specific verifiers. Conversely, label-free RL methods extract self-signals from output distributions, but mainly at the answer or trajectory level and therefore cannot assign credit to intermediate turns. We propose Self-Induced Outcome Potential (SIOP), which treats semantic clusters of final answers as latent future outcome states for potential-based turn-level credit assignment. For each query, SIOP samples multiple rollouts, clusters final answers into semantic outcome modes, and builds a reliability-aware target distribution over these states. It then rewards turns for increasing posterior support for reliable future states using a tractable cluster-level approximation. The objective generalizes information-potential shaping from gold-answer supervision to settings without task-specific gold verifiers while avoiding the broadcasted rollout-level advantages used by standard GRPO. We formalize the framework, characterize its supervised gold-answer limit, and show that SIOP improves average performance over verifier-free outcome-level baselines on seven search-augmented agentic reasoning benchmarks while approaching a gold-supervised outcome baseline. Code is available at https://github.com/dl-m9/SIOP.git.

4. Explaining and Preventing Alignment Collapse in Iterative RLHF

ArXiv ID: 2605.04266 | Relevance Score: 8/10 | Novelty Score: 8/10

👥 Authors: Etienne Gauthier, Francis Bach, Michael I. Jordan

🏫 Affiliations: Unknown Institution

💡 Highlight: Proposes foresighted policy optimization (FPO) to prevent alignment collapse in iterative RLHF by restoring a missing steering term in the policy gradient.
View Abstract

arXiv:2605.04266v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) typically assumes a static or non-strategic reward model (RM). In iterative deployment, however, the policy generates the data on which the RM is retrained, creating a feedback loop. Building on the Stackelberg game formulation of this interaction, we derive an analytical decomposition of the policy's true optimization gradient into a standard policy gradient and a parameter-steering term that captures the policy's influence on the RM's future parameters. We show that standard iterative RLHF, which drops this steering term entirely, suffers from alignment collapse: the policy systematically exploits the RM's blind spots, producing low-quality, high-reward outputs whose feedback reinforces the very errors it exploits. To mitigate this, we propose foresighted policy optimization (FPO), a mechanism-design intervention that restores the missing steering term by regularizing the policy's parameter-steering effect on RM updates. We instantiate FPO via a scalable first-order approximation and demonstrate that it prevents alignment collapse on both controlled environments and an LLM alignment pipeline using Llama-3.2-1B.

🏫 Affiliations: Unknown Institution

💡 Highlight: Proposes a pass-rate control method (Prefix Sampling) to steer binary-reward RL for LLMs toward the most informative regime, achieving wall-clock speedups on SWE-bench-style agentic RL.
View Abstract

arXiv:2605.05112v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: SWE-bench-style agentic reinforcement learning relies on expensive stateful trajectories, yet substantial compute is wasted on sampled rollout groups with skewed pass rates, where binary rewards provide a weak contrastive signal. We frame this inefficiency as a pass-rate control problem and show that a 50% pass rate is the most informative operating point: it maximizes reward entropy, the probability of surviving group filtering, RLOO advantage energy under GRPO, and success--failure contrastive structure. Guided by this principle, we propose Prefix Sampling (PS), which replays trajectory prefixes to steer skewed groups toward this regime: successful prefixes serve as head starts for mostly failing groups, while failing prefixes serve as handicaps for mostly passing groups. In stateful agent environments, prefix states are reconstructed through replay while replayed tokens are excluded from the loss, restricting optimization to continuations generated by the current policy. On SWE-bench-style agentic RL, PS delivers end-to-end wall-clock speedups of 2.01x on Qwen3-14B and 1.55x on Qwen3-32B while preserving or improving final verified performance. For 14B, the SWE-bench Verified peak rises from the baseline peak of 0.273 to 0.295 under PS. Additional mathematical reasoning experiments on AIME 2025 show the same pass-rate control pattern and decompose the gains into replay, bidirectional coverage, and adaptive control.

arXiv:2605.03871v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Language models encode substantial evaluative knowledge from pretraining, yet current post-training methods rely on external supervision (human annotations, proprietary models, or scalar reward models) to produce reward signals. Each imposes a ceiling. Human judgment cannot supervise capabilities beyond its own, proprietary APIs create dependencies, and verifiable rewards cover only domains with ground-truth answers. Self-improvement from a model's own evaluative capacity is a reward source that scales with the model itself, yet remains largely untapped by current methods. We introduce EVOLM, a post-training method that structures this capacity into explicit discriminative rubrics and uses them as training signal. EVOLM trains two capabilities within a single language model in alternation: (1) a rubric generator producing instance-specific evaluation criteria optimized for discriminative utility, which maximizes a small frozen judge's ability to distinguish preferred from dispreferred responses; and (2) a policy trained using those rubric-conditioned scores as reward. Overall, EVOLM demonstrates that structuring a model's evaluative capacity into co-evolving discriminative rubrics enables self-improvement without external supervision.

8. EP-GRPO: Entropy-Progress Aligned Group Relative Policy Optimization with Implicit Process Guidance

👥 Authors: Song Yu, Li Li, Wenwen Zhao, Zhisheng Yang

🏫 Affiliations: Unknown Institution

💡 Highlight: Proposes EP-GRPO, a novel RL framework for LLMs that addresses credit assignment failures in GRPO via entropy-gated modulation and implicit process signals from policy divergence.
View Abstract

arXiv:2605.04960v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards (RLVR), particularly Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO), has advanced LLM reasoning. However, GRPO suffers from three credit assignment failures: uniform token-level granularity that ignores heterogeneous informational value, uniform polarity that penalizes correct steps and rewards incorrect ones, and zero-variance collapse that erases outcome-driven gradients. We systematically quantify these failures, revealing highly non-uniform token informativeness, widespread step-level polarity misalignment, and substantial training waste. To address these limitations, we propose Entropy-Progress Aligned GRPO (EP-GRPO), a framework that mines the model's intrinsic information flow for dense, self-supervised guidance. EP-GRPO integrates entropy-gated modulation to prioritize high entropy decision pivots, implicit process signals from policy divergence anchored to outcome advantages for directional token-level feedback without external reward models, and cumulative entropy mapping that enables progress-aligned advantage normalization, naturally maintaining gradient flow under zero reward variance. Extensive experiments on mathematical reasoning benchmarks demonstrate that EP-GRPO achieves superior accuracy and efficiency compared to GRPO and its variants. The code will be available.

💡 Highlight: Identifies and fixes aggregation bias in GRPO-style RLVR for LLMs by proposing Balanced Aggregation, a new reward aggregation method that improves training stability and reasoning performance.
View Abstract

arXiv:2605.04077v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards (RLVR) has become a central paradigm for improving reasoning and code generation in large language models, and GRPO-style training is widely adopted for its simplicity and effectiveness. However, an important design choice remains underexplored: how token-level policy gradient terms are aggregated within each sampled group. Standard GRPO uses sequence aggregation, while recent work has advocated token aggregation as a better alternative. We show that these two rules induce different optimization biases: token aggregation introduces sign-length coupling, while sequence aggregation implicitly downweights longer responses through sequence-level equal weighting. To address this tension, we propose \textbf{Balanced Aggregation (BA)}, a simple drop-in replacement that computes token-level means separately within the positive and negative subsets and then combines them with sequence-count-based weights. Experiments with Qwen2.5-Math-7B and Qwen3-1.7B on DAPO-17k and Polaris, evaluated on six reasoning and coding benchmarks, show that BA consistently improves training stability and final performance over standard token and sequence aggregation. Our analysis further shows that the relative effectiveness of token and sequence aggregation is largely governed by response-length variation and the positive-negative length gap, highlighting aggregation as a critical design dimension in GRPO-style RLVR.

arXiv:2605.05040v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: On-policy distillation is an efficient alternative to reinforcement learning, offering dense token-level training signals. arXiv:2605.04078v1 Announce Type: new Abstract: Reasoning distillation aims to transfer multi-step reasoning capabilities from large language models to smaller, more efficient ones. While recent methods have shown promising gains, they typically rely on static teacher-student hierarchies and frame distillation as trajectory imitation. This is misaligned with the structure of reasoning, where intermediate steps are often locally under-specified: global correctness constrains the final answer, but does not uniquely determine each intermediate move. We propose validity-calibrated reasoning distillation, a framework that treats reasoning distillation as a problem of local learning-signal allocation rather than path alignment. Instead of enforcing token-level imitation, we compare the student's and teacher's proposed next-step actions under the same prefix and use their relative local validity to modulate the strength of the distillation update. This yields a dynamic, context-dependent supervision mechanism that preserves the teacher's structural guidance while adapting update strength to local reasoning quality. Across mathematical reasoning, code generation, and instruction-following benchmarks, our method consistently outperforms strong distillation baselines. These results indicate that effective LLM reasoning distillation is governed not by rigid trajectory imitation, but by principled, locally calibrated allocation of learning signal.

Research Interest Criteria

Please evaluate papers using a strict filter. We only want a small set of strongly aligned papers, not a broad survey of adjacent work.

Global Selection Principle

  • Mark a paper as relevant only if one of the three topics below is a central contribution of the paper, not a side application, benchmark, or discussion point.
  • Be conservative by default: if the connection is indirect, partial, or ambiguous, treat it as not relevant or score it low.
  • Prefer papers that introduce new methods, training frameworks, reward designs, agent improvement loops, or latent reasoning architectures.
  • Do not favor papers just because they mention "LLM", "agent", "reasoning", "RL", "alignment", or "CoT" in a generic way.
  • Exclude broad foundation model papers, generic post-training papers, generic tool-use agents, application/system papers, domain-specific deployments, and pure benchmark papers unless their core method strongly matches one of the topics below.

1. Reinforcement Learning (RL) for LLMs

We care about RL methods that directly improve LLM behavior, alignment, or reasoning through explicit reward design, RL pipelines, verifier-driven optimization, or scalable preference/feedback mechanisms.

  • Strong match: RLHF/RLAIF variants, verifiable reward based RL, rejection sampling plus RL pipelines, reward model design, outcome/process reward methods, online RL for LLMs, and methods that materially improve helpfulness, honesty, harmlessness, or hallucination behavior.
  • Especially valuable: Papers that propose a new reward function, a new scalable RL training recipe, or a new data-generation / self-improvement loop for post-training LLMs.
  • Usually not relevant: Generic reinforcement learning papers, RL for robotics/control/games, prompt optimization without RL, supervised finetuning only, inference-time search without a training contribution, or alignment papers with no substantive RL component.

2. Agents for LLMs

We care specifically about self-evolving agents rather than static agent systems. The central question is whether the agent can improve itself over time through interaction, reflection, tool-use traces, self-generated supervision, or automated curriculum/data generation.

  • Strong match: Self-improving agent frameworks, reflection/self-critique loops that feed back into learning, trajectory-driven continual improvement, automatic tool-use policy improvement, environment interaction that produces new supervision, and long-horizon agent adaptation.
  • Especially valuable: Closed-loop systems showing measurable capability gain over time, reliable agent learning from interaction traces, or agent architectures that turn execution experience into better future performance.
  • Usually not relevant: Vanilla ReAct-style agents, workflow orchestration, multi-agent communication only, tool calling systems without learning, benchmark papers, memory systems without self-improvement, or application-focused agent papers.

3. Latent CoT/Reasoning

We care about methods that move reasoning away from verbose explicit text chains into compact latent computation while preserving or improving reasoning quality.

  • Strong match: Latent reasoning architectures, hidden-state reasoning, implicit CoT, recurrent depth / recurrent latent computation, pause or thinking tokens for internal computation, explicit-to-latent distillation, and methods that reduce reasoning token cost or KV-cache use while retaining accuracy.
  • Especially valuable: New architectures or objectives for latent reasoning, hidden-state alignment/distillation, compression of reasoning traces, or methods that materially improve reasoning efficiency on math and logic tasks.
  • Usually not relevant: Standard explicit CoT prompting, generic reasoning finetuning, test-time scaling without latent computation, reasoning benchmarks, or papers whose main contribution is evaluation rather than a latent reasoning mechanism.

Final Preference

If a paper is merely adjacent to these areas, do not select it. Favor precision over recall. It is better to miss a borderline paper than to include many weakly related ones.
