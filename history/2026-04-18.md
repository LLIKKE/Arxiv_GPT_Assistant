## 💡 Daily Research Summary
<div style='background-color: #f6f8fa; border-left: 4px solid #24292e; padding: 15px; margin-bottom: 20px; border-radius: 4px;'>
### Reinforcement Learning for LLMs & Agents
Today's research demonstrates a significant maturation of reinforcement learning (RL) techniques for large language models, moving beyond foundational alignment to address nuanced challenges in training stability, credit assignment, and reward design. A clear trend is the development of novel, scalable RL paradigms tailored for LLMs, such as framing behavior-regularized RL as an optimal transport problem solved via discrete gradient flow (Value Gradient Flow) or integrating fine-grained process supervision for better credit assignment in complex tasks like search (CW-GRPO). Concurrently, critical analyses of failure modes, such as reward hacking in verifiable reward settings (RLVR), highlight the community's focus on robustness. These advancements are being applied across diverse domains—from topology optimization (TOPCELL) and financial strategy generation to visual RAG (UniDoc-RL) and recommendation systems (GenRec)—indicating RL's role as a versatile engine for specializing and optimizing LLM behavior for specific, high-stakes applications.

### Self-Improving & Evolving Agents
The frontier of agent design is increasingly defined by frameworks that enable continuous, autonomous improvement through interaction and reflection. A key breakthrough is the shift from static agents to systems where the agent's core policy or skill set is inherently evolvable. This is exemplified by personalized user policy skills that self-evolve through interaction (SAGER) and embodied agents that employ long short-term reflective optimization for manipulation tasks. Furthermore, the automation of the entire LLM training lifecycle via multi-agent systems (TREX) represents a paradigm shift towards meta-optimization, where agents themselves plan exploration and distill insights from iterative trials. These approaches collectively move the field toward more adaptive, persistent, and capable autonomous systems that learn from experience without constant human intervention.

### Efficient & Latent Reasoning
A major research thrust focuses on making reasoning processes within models more computationally efficient and structurally explicit. Innovations include novel architectures like looped transformers with internal recall, which allow for test-time compute scaling, and methods that leverage latent activation patterns to guide sparse updates for long-context RL (LongAct). There is also a strong emphasis on dynamic resource allocation: new frameworks adaptively select efficient reasoning formats for visual tasks to reduce token usage, while constrained policy optimization methods dynamically allocate test-time compute. The introduction of explicit, ordered sequences of latent thoughts as a middle layer for tasks like sign language translation further underscores the trend towards making reasoning tractable, inspectable, and efficient, whether through architectural innovation or adaptive inference-time strategies.

### Multimodal & Visual Reasoning
Research in multimodal domains is rapidly integrating advanced RL and reasoning techniques to tackle complex, open-ended tasks. The unifying theme is the use of structured reinforcement learning to orchestrate sophisticated agent behavior over visual and textual modalities. For instance, new frameworks employ hierarchical action spaces and dense multi-reward schemes for visual retrieval-augmented generation (UniDoc-RL), while others use RL to train models for adaptive visual reasoning that balances performance with efficiency. Systematic studies on reward design for physical reasoning in vision-language models highlight the critical role of tailored incentive structures. Beyond agents, similar RL principles are being applied to improve generative models themselves, such as through reward-aware trajectory shaping for visual generation and step-level alignment for diffusion models with multiple objectives.

### Model Efficiency & Specialization
Efforts to enhance the practical deployment of large models are advancing on multiple fronts, particularly through specialized routing and targeted fine-tuning. A notable development is Counterfactual Routing (CoR), a training-free inference method for Mixture-of-Experts models that dynamically reallocates computation to mitigate hallucinations, directly improving reliability without retraining. For flow matching models, new direct-gradient fine-tuning methods like LeapAlign enable efficient reward propagation to early generation steps. In the GUI automation space, lightweight agent frameworks utilize multi-role orchestration and RL for cooperative exploration, demonstrating that significant capability can be achieved with efficient, specialized architectures rather than monolithic models.

# Research Interest Criteria

Please evaluate papers using a strict filter. We only want a small set of strongly aligned papers, not a broad survey of adjacent work.

## Global Selection Principle
- Mark a paper as relevant only if one of the three topics below is a central contribution of the paper, not a side application, benchmark, or discussion point.
- Be conservative by default: if the connection is indirect, partial, or ambiguous, treat it as not relevant or score it low.
- Prefer papers that introduce new methods, training frameworks, reward designs, agent improvement loops, or latent reasoning architectures.
- Do not favor papers just because they mention "LLM", "agent", "reasoning", "RL", "alignment", or "CoT" in a generic way.
- Exclude broad foundation model papers, generic post-training papers, generic tool-use agents, application/system papers, domain-specific deployments, and pure benchmark papers unless their core method strongly matches one of the topics below.

## 1. Reinforcement Learning (RL) for LLMs
We care about RL methods that directly improve LLM behavior, alignment, or reasoning through explicit reward design, RL pipelines, verifier-driven optimization, or scalable preference/feedback mechanisms.
- **Strong match:** RLHF/RLAIF variants, verifiable reward based RL, rejection sampling plus RL pipelines, reward model design, outcome/process reward methods, online RL for LLMs, and methods that materially improve helpfulness, honesty, harmlessness, or hallucination behavior.
- **Especially valuable:** Papers that propose a new reward function, a new scalable RL training recipe, or a new data-generation / self-improvement loop for post-training LLMs.
- **Usually not relevant:** Generic reinforcement learning papers, RL for robotics/control/games, prompt optimization without RL, supervised finetuning only, inference-time search without a training contribution, or alignment papers with no substantive RL component.

## 2. Agents for LLMs
We care specifically about self-evolving agents rather than static agent systems. The central question is whether the agent can improve itself over time through interaction, reflection, tool-use traces, self-generated supervision, or automated curriculum/data generation.
- **Strong match:** Self-improving agent frameworks, reflection/self-critique loops that feed back into learning, trajectory-driven continual improvement, automatic tool-use policy improvement, environment interaction that produces new supervision, and long-horizon agent adaptation.
- **Especially valuable:** Closed-loop systems showing measurable capability gain over time, reliable agent learning from interaction traces, or agent architectures that turn execution experience into better future performance.
- **Usually not relevant:** Vanilla ReAct-style agents, workflow orchestration, multi-agent communication only, tool calling systems without learning, benchmark papers, memory systems without self-improvement, or application-focused agent papers.

## 3. Latent CoT/Reasoning
We care about methods that move reasoning away from verbose explicit text chains into compact latent computation while preserving or improving reasoning quality.
- **Strong match:** Latent reasoning architectures, hidden-state reasoning, implicit CoT, recurrent depth / recurrent latent computation, pause or thinking tokens for internal computation, explicit-to-latent distillation, and methods that reduce reasoning token cost or KV-cache use while retaining accuracy.
- **Especially valuable:** New architectures or objectives for latent reasoning, hidden-state alignment/distillation, compression of reasoning traces, or methods that materially improve reasoning efficiency on math and logic tasks.
- **Usually not relevant:** Standard explicit CoT prompting, generic reasoning finetuning, test-time scaling without latent computation, reasoning benchmarks, or papers whose main contribution is evaluation rather than a latent reasoning mechanism.

## Final Preference
If a paper is merely adjacent to these areas, do not select it. Favor precision over recall. It is better to miss a borderline paper than to include many weakly related ones.
