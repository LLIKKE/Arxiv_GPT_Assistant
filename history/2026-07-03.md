## 💡 Daily Research Summary

### RL for LLMs
A major cluster of work this week fundamentally advances reinforcement learning for language model post-training. Central to this is a unifying theoretical result proving that GRPO, Dr. GRPO, and DAPO are all governed by a single group-standard-deviation identity, revealing that the core update size is simply the answer disagreement—a clean insight that demystifies the mechanics of these popular algorithms. Several papers challenge the status quo of full-parameter RL updates: one shows that RL gains are concentrated in a single transformer layer, while another introduces staleness-learning rate scaling laws for asynchronous RLHF (GRPO) to stabilize training with stale rollouts. New methods also emerge for specific training regimes, including SLIM-RL, a trace-free RL objective for diffusion LLMs that avoids trajectory slicing, and DASH, a segment-level credit assignment method that reduces overthinking in reasoning models by identifying productive self-reflection. Furthermore, Active-GRPO proposes dynamic switching between imitation and self-improvement, and the GR2 technical report provides industrial-scale insights on combining RL with verifiable rewards and on-policy distillation for recommendation. The theme is clear: the field is moving from monolithic RL recipes toward more principled, efficient, and theoretically grounded training frameworks.

### SFT and Preference Optimization
The boundary between supervised fine-tuning (SFT) and RL is being actively blurred. A key paper introduces an adversarial generator-discriminator framework that augments verifiable rewards with a learned signal from human demonstrations, effectively bridging RL and SFT for LLM training. This work addresses the fundamental tension between reward-based optimization and imitation learning. Separately, the Confidence-Adaptive Thinking (CAT) framework proposes a preference optimization approach that uses the model's intrinsic self-certainty to dynamically modulate reasoning length, directly targeting efficiency in reasoning models. These contributions suggest a growing sophistication in how we combine human data, verifiable rewards, and model-internal signals for training.

### Latent Reasoning Architectures
Innovation in reasoning architectures is accelerating, with a strong focus on separating and specializing computational pathways. The State-Prediction Separation Hypothesis proposes a novel Transformer variant with distinct computation streams for state tracking and prediction, directly advancing the theory of latent reasoning. DiscoLoop introduces a looping architecture that mixes discrete embedding and continuous hidden-state channels for multi-hop reasoning, achieving near-perfect accuracy with fewer training steps. Complementing these, Message Passing Language Models (MPLMs) offer a parallel reasoning framework where LLM threads communicate via lightweight send/receive primitives, reducing context length and enabling preemption. The multimodal domain also sees progress with Asymmetric Mutual Variational Learning (AMVL), which resolves train-inference mismatch in continuous latent reasoning for MLLMs via a dual-KL objective. These works collectively point toward more modular, efficient, and theoretically grounded reasoning architectures.

### Multimodal Reasoning
Multimodal reasoning continues to mature with a focus on decoupling and process-level supervision. Perceive-to-Reason introduces a framework that explicitly separates perception and reasoning stages, using a role-aware RL (GRPO) strategy that alternates updates to each component for fine-grained visual reasoning. GeoSearcher applies process-supervised RL (PF-GRPO) with a process-aware reward for progressive reasoning in remote sensing visual grounding, demonstrating the power of step-level rewards in complex visual tasks. The Multimodal Continuous Reasoning work via AMVL also bridges the gap between latent reasoning and multimodal inputs. The trend is toward more structured, stage-aware training that respects the distinct challenges of perception and reasoning.

### Test-Time Scaling and Sample Efficiency
A practical breakthrough in test-time computation comes from QuasiMoTTo, which introduces quasi-Monte Carlo sampling as a drop-in replacement for i.i.d. sampling in both test-time scaling and policy-gradient RL. This method significantly improves sample efficiency by reducing redundancy, a critical advance for both inference-time compute scaling and training. This work directly addresses the computational cost of large-scale sampling, a bottleneck for many RL and reasoning applications.

### Domain-Specific Applications
RL for LLMs is being applied to increasingly specialized domains with tailored reward designs. Graph-Native Reinforcement Learning introduces GRPO-based fine-tuning for generating traceable scientific hypotheses through conceptual recombination, demonstrating a novel reward and training recipe for scientific discovery. The GR2 technical report applies RL with verifiable rewards to industrial-scale recommendation re-ranking, providing key insights on reward design and the failure of SFT at scale. Efficient Multilingual Reasoning Transfer via Progressive Code-Switching uses RL with a language consistency curriculum to transfer English reasoning to target languages. These applications show the versatility of RL-based training when combined with domain-specific reward structures.

# Research Interest Criteria

Please evaluate papers using a strict filter. We only want a small set of strongly aligned papers, not a broad survey of adjacent work.

## Global Selection Principle
- Mark a paper as relevant only if one of the three topics below is a central contribution of the paper, not a side application, benchmark, or discussion point.
- Be conservative by default: if the connection is indirect, partial, or ambiguous, treat it as not relevant or score it low.
- Prefer papers that introduce new methods, training frameworks, reward designs, scalable self-improvement loops, or latent reasoning architectures with clear technical substance.
- Do not favor papers just because they mention "LLM", "agent", "reasoning", "RL", "alignment", or "CoT" in a generic way.
- Exclude broad foundation model papers, generic post-training papers, generic tool-use agents, application/system papers, domain-specific deployments, and pure benchmark papers unless their core method strongly matches one of the topics below.

## 1. Reinforcement Learning (RL) for LLMs
We care about RL methods that directly improve LLM behavior, alignment, or reasoning through explicit reward design, RL pipelines, verifier-driven optimization, or scalable preference/feedback mechanisms.
- **Strong match:** RLHF/RLAIF variants, verifiable reward based RL, rejection sampling plus RL pipelines, reward model design, outcome/process reward methods, online RL for LLMs, and methods that materially improve helpfulness, honesty, harmlessness, or hallucination behavior.
- **Especially valuable:** Papers that propose a new reward function, a new scalable RL training recipe, or a new data-generation / self-improvement loop for post-training LLMs.
- **Usually not relevant:** Generic reinforcement learning papers, RL for robotics/control/games, prompt optimization without RL, supervised finetuning only, inference-time search without a training contribution, or alignment papers with no substantive RL component.

## 2. Latent CoT/Reasoning
We care about methods that move reasoning away from verbose explicit text chains into compact latent computation while preserving or improving reasoning quality.
- **Strong match:** Latent reasoning architectures, hidden-state reasoning, implicit CoT, recurrent depth / recurrent latent computation, pause or thinking tokens for internal computation, explicit-to-latent distillation, and methods that reduce reasoning token cost or KV-cache use while retaining accuracy.
- **Especially valuable:** New architectures or objectives for latent reasoning, hidden-state alignment/distillation, compression of reasoning traces, or methods that materially improve reasoning efficiency on math and logic tasks.
- **Usually not relevant:** Standard explicit CoT prompting, generic reasoning finetuning, test-time scaling without latent computation, reasoning benchmarks, or papers whose main contribution is evaluation rather than a latent reasoning mechanism.

## 3. On-Policy Distillation for LLMs
We care about distillation methods that improve LLM capabilities by training on the model's own on-policy outputs, trajectories, preferences, corrections, or interaction data, especially when the student distribution is actively refreshed during training.
- **Strong match:** On-policy distillation, self-distillation from sampled rollouts, online teacher-student distillation, policy-improvement distillation, iterative distillation from model-generated reasoning traces, distillation from verifier/reward-selected on-policy samples, and methods that close the gap between training data distribution and the model's current behavior.
- **Especially valuable:** Papers that propose a new iterative distillation loop, sampling-and-selection strategy, online data refresh mechanism, teacher/student update schedule, or objective that measurably improves reasoning, instruction-following, alignment, tool use, or general LLM capability.
- **Usually not relevant:** Standard offline knowledge distillation, static SFT on teacher-generated data, model compression only, data cleaning only, speculative decoding/distillation for speed only, or generic synthetic data generation without an on-policy or iterative policy-improvement component.

## Final Preference
If a paper is merely adjacent to these areas, do not select it. Favor precision over recall. It is better to miss a borderline paper than to include many weakly related ones.
