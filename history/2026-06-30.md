## 💡 Daily Research Summary
<div style='background-color: #f6f8fa; border-left: 4px solid #24292e; padding: 15px; margin-bottom: 20px; border-radius: 4px;'>
### SFT and Data Efficiency
The theoretical analysis in "Learning to Reason with Curriculum II" provides a rigorous framework demonstrating that curriculum decomposition can dramatically reduce the token supervision required for supervised fine-tuning (SFT) and lower the reference model coverage demands for reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards (RLVR). This work offers a principled justification for why structured training curricula can achieve compositional generalization with far less data, directly informing practical SFT pipeline design.

### RL for LLMs and Reasoning
This is the dominant theme today, with a remarkable convergence around novel reward design, credit assignment, and training stability. Several papers advance process reward modeling: "The Weakest Link Tells It All" introduces a learnable credit assignment mechanism with multiple instance learning (MIL) pooling, enabling outcome-supervised process rewards that are more sample-efficient than traditional dense reward models. The "Retroactive Advantage Correction" (RAC) paper tackles the practical challenge of delayed rewards in RLHF by providing a closed-form, unbiased correction via clipped residual reinjection into the advantage estimate, which is theoretically grounded and directly applicable to long-horizon reasoning tasks. "Tandem Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards" proposes a novel co-generation paradigm where a senior and junior model collaborate on rollouts, improving human/weaker-agent compatibility without sacrificing reasoning performance—a clever twist on standard RLVR. The "Supersede" environment introduces a verifiable reward targeting temporal fact-currency in agents, demonstrating that GRPO fine-tuning can effectively train down the memory-update gap, which is a concrete new benchmark for RL-based agent training. "EchoSonar-R" and "MER-R1" both apply GRPO with task-specific composite rewards to medical and multimodal domains, respectively, showing the transferability of these RL reasoning methods to specialized verticals. "Internalizing the Future" proposes a three-stage paradigm (WM-AMT, FE-SFT, FC-RL) that internalizes world model planning via a novel reward/RL pipeline, effectively teaching agents to use latent foresight during reasoning. The "Textual Belief States" paper introduces fGRPO, a tree-structured RL method that enforces strict latent state mediation in text-based world models, advancing both RL-for-LLMs and latent reasoning. Finally, "NormGuard" identifies and mitigates norm inflation in RL post-training for flow-based generators, offering a reward-preserving hinge penalty that improves perceptual quality—a practical regularization insight for RL fine-tuning stability.

### On-Policy Distillation and Alignment
A clear sub-trend is the integration of on-policy distillation with RL. The "Qwen-Image-2.0-RL" technical report applies RLHF and on-policy distillation to diffusion models, using a composite reward system and GRPO-based training that directly mirrors the RL-for-LLMs paradigm. "ATOD" introduces an annealed turn-aware on-policy distillation algorithm specifically for multi-turn LLM agents, combining hybrid distillation with turn-level reweighting to improve agent performance. "Low-Agreeableness Persona Conditioning" takes a data-centric approach to safety alignment by rewriting training data to condition on low agreeableness, achieving safe fine-tuning without modifying the RL objective—a complementary method to reward-based alignment. "PEBS" provides a closed-form per-rater empirical-Bayes shrinkage estimator for calibrating RLHF reward models, reducing held-out RMSE by 8-10% without retraining, which is a practical statistical fix for noisy human feedback.

### Agent Architectures and Latent Reasoning
Several papers push the frontier of agentic reasoning beyond standard RL. "The Context-Ready Transformer" introduces a new recurrent architecture with a correction network that pre-contextualizes tokens, enabling efficient sequential inference and strong performance on composition tasks—a direct contribution to latent reasoning architectures. "Internalizing the Future" and "Textual Belief States" both emphasize internal world models, with the former using a three-stage training pipeline and the latter using tree-structured RL for strict mediation, collectively pointing toward a new paradigm where agents learn to reason about future states internally before acting. "Supersede" provides a concrete RL environment and training loop for temporal fact-currency, which is a practical benchmark for agent memory-update capabilities.

### Multimodal and Video Generation
The multimodal domain sees two notable RL applications. "TempAct" proposes a planner-executor RL framework with hierarchical group exploration and rewards for temporally plausible autoregressive video generation, directly extending RL-for-LLMs to the video domain. "MER-R1" applies a dual-objective RL framework with slow-fast confidence calibration to multimodal emotion reasoning, showing that RL can optimize nuanced perceptual tasks. "EchoSonar-R" brings GRPO with task-specific rewards to echocardiography, demonstrating RL's utility in medical image analysis and report generation.

### Compression and Efficiency
While not a major category today, "NormGuard" touches on efficiency by identifying and correcting norm inflation in flow-based models, which has implications for maintaining model quality during RL fine-tuning without additional computational overhead. "PEBS" also contributes an efficient, closed-form calibration method for reward models that requires no retraining, directly improving the signal-to-noise ratio in the RLHF pipeline.

# Research Interest Criteria

Please evaluate papers using a strict filter. We only want a small set of strongly aligned papers, not a broad survey of adjacent work.

## Global Selection Principle
- Mark a paper as relevant only if one of the three topics below is a central contribution of the paper, not a side application, benchmark, or discussion point.
- Be conservative by default: if the connection is indirect, partial, or ambiguous, treat it as not relevant or score it low.
- Prefer papers that introduce new methods, training frameworks, reward designs, scalable self-improvement loops, or latent reasoning architectures with clear technical substance.
- Do not favor papers just because they mention "LLM", "agent", "reasoning", "RL", "alignment", or "CoT" in a generic way.
- Exclude broad foundation model papers, generic post-training papers, generic tool-use agents, application/system papers, domain-specific deployments, and pure benchmark papers unless their core method strongly matches one of the topics below.

## 1. Reinforcement Learning (RL) for LLMs
We care about RL methods that directly improve LLM behavior, alignment, or reasoning through explicit reward design, RL pipelines, verifier-driven optimization, or scalable preference/feedback mechanisms.
- **Strong match:** RLHF/RLAIF variants, verifiable reward based RL, rejection sampling plus RL pipelines, reward model design, outcome/process reward methods, online RL for LLMs, and methods that materially improve helpfulness, honesty, harmlessness, or hallucination behavior.
- **Especially valuable:** Papers that propose a new reward function, a new scalable RL training recipe, or a new data-generation / self-improvement loop for post-training LLMs.
- **Usually not relevant:** Generic reinforcement learning papers, RL for robotics/control/games, prompt optimization without RL, supervised finetuning only, inference-time search without a training contribution, or alignment papers with no substantive RL component.

## 2. Latent CoT/Reasoning
We care about methods that move reasoning away from verbose explicit text chains into compact latent computation while preserving or improving reasoning quality.
- **Strong match:** Latent reasoning architectures, hidden-state reasoning, implicit CoT, recurrent depth / recurrent latent computation, pause or thinking tokens for internal computation, explicit-to-latent distillation, and methods that reduce reasoning token cost or KV-cache use while retaining accuracy.
- **Especially valuable:** New architectures or objectives for latent reasoning, hidden-state alignment/distillation, compression of reasoning traces, or methods that materially improve reasoning efficiency on math and logic tasks.
- **Usually not relevant:** Standard explicit CoT prompting, generic reasoning finetuning, test-time scaling without latent computation, reasoning benchmarks, or papers whose main contribution is evaluation rather than a latent reasoning mechanism.

## 3. On-Policy Distillation for LLMs
We care about distillation methods that improve LLM capabilities by training on the model's own on-policy outputs, trajectories, preferences, corrections, or interaction data, especially when the student distribution is actively refreshed during training.
- **Strong match:** On-policy distillation, self-distillation from sampled rollouts, online teacher-student distillation, policy-improvement distillation, iterative distillation from model-generated reasoning traces, distillation from verifier/reward-selected on-policy samples, and methods that close the gap between training data distribution and the model's current behavior.
- **Especially valuable:** Papers that propose a new iterative distillation loop, sampling-and-selection strategy, online data refresh mechanism, teacher/student update schedule, or objective that measurably improves reasoning, instruction-following, alignment, tool use, or general LLM capability.
- **Usually not relevant:** Standard offline knowledge distillation, static SFT on teacher-generated data, model compression only, data cleaning only, speculative decoding/distillation for speed only, or generic synthetic data generation without an on-policy or iterative policy-improvement component.

## Final Preference
If a paper is merely adjacent to these areas, do not select it. Favor precision over recall. It is better to miss a borderline paper than to include many weakly related ones.
